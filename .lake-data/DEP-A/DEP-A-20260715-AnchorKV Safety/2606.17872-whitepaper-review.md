# A safety constraint on what a compressed cache is allowed to forget

## A detailed review, technical reconstruction, and independent re-conceptualization of “AnchorKV: Safety-Aware KV Cache Compression via Soft Penalty with a Refusal Anchor”

**Source paper:** Ning Ni and Yingjie Lao, “AnchorKV: Safety-Aware KV Cache Compression via Soft Penalty with a Refusal Anchor,” arXiv:2606.17872v1, 2026.[^source-paper]

**Artifact prepared:** 2026-07-15  
**Local source reviewed:** locally archived 14-page PDF and matching full-paper HTML, including references and the limitations section; private path retained only in the local archive  
**Review scope:** full-paper reconstruction, quantitative and claim audit, external primary-source contextualization, independent re-conceptualization, and replication agenda  
**Review status:** paper and public primary records inspected; no released AnchorKV implementation was verified, and no experiment was rerun

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

AnchorKV modifies an existing token-selection KV-cache compressor by penalizing retention scores for prompt tokens that project onto an offline, layer-specific harmful-prompt direction, yielding a tunable safety–utility trade-off without changing the cache budget.

### The deeper idea

The most durable contribution is not the particular “anchor” name. It is the recognition that a resource allocator can preserve ordinary task quality while selectively erasing evidence needed by a safety policy. AnchorKV therefore treats retention as a constrained allocation problem: the compressor still selects a fixed number of tokens, but a second signal asks whether its preferred subset would weaken refusal-related state. This is a form of policy-aware lossy compression. The compressor is not merely asked to reconstruct downstream utility; it is asked to avoid a behaviorally asymmetric loss channel.

### Bottom-line judgment

The paper establishes a useful, narrow result under its tested setup. On Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, FastKV at 25% retention increases AdvPrompter attack success rate (ASR), especially for a transferred attack, and a key-space direction derived from harmful versus harmless prompts sharply separates the two classes. Adding a moderate penalty reduces ASR while causing a small LongBench average loss. Those findings support “safety behavior can regress under KV eviction” and “a calibrated auxiliary score can reduce that regression in this setting.” They do not establish a general jailbreak defense, robustness to adaptive attackers, cross-model transfer of the anchor, or monotonic safety improvement as penalty strength rises. The paper itself usefully exposes the last limitation: excessive penalty enters a reversal regime and can become less safe than the unmodified compressor.

### Principal strengths

- It identifies a concrete behavioral regression hidden by accuracy-only compressor evaluation and measures it under self and transfer attacks.
- Its intervention is budget-orthogonal: the retention count remains fixed, and setting the penalty to zero is reported to reproduce FastKV bit-exactly.
- It separates anchor construction, layer selection, held-out discrimination, jailbreak behavior, and long-context utility, making the causal chain inspectable.
- The non-monotonic penalty sweep is more informative than reporting a single favorable operating point.

### Principal qualifications

1. Safety evidence is concentrated on one target model, one compressor family, AdvBench/AdvPrompter, and a single operational layer selected with in-distribution validation data.
2. High harmful-versus-harmless AUROC is not equivalent to adversarial robustness; the attack objective is not shown to adapt to the anchor-aware scoring rule.
3. LongBench averages hide task heterogeneity, and the paper reports a single-seed penalty sweep for the main safety curve rather than uncertainty over training, anchor construction, and inference.
4. No independently runnable implementation or reproduced result was verified for this review.

## 1. The problem the paper is actually solving

### 1.1 Problem and stakes

For an autoregressive transformer, each previously processed token contributes keys and values used by later attention. The KV cache therefore grows approximately linearly with sequence length. Eviction-based compressors keep only a subset of past tokens, reducing memory and sometimes attention work. Most such systems optimize a benign-task proxy: retain tokens with high attention-derived importance and verify that downstream accuracy remains close to the full-cache model.

AnchorKV asks what that protocol omits. Instruction-tuned models do not use context only to recover facts or code dependencies; context also carries internal evidence that supports refusal behavior. If an importance rule preferentially drops those tokens, the model can preserve ordinary benchmark accuracy while becoming more willing to answer a harmful request. The practical target is thus not “make compression safe in general.” It is narrower: reduce the safety-alignment regression induced by aggressive FastKV token eviction, while preserving its fixed retention rate and most of its LongBench utility.

### 1.2 Prior method families

The paper distinguishes three relevant families. First, accuracy-oriented KV compressors such as FastKV, SnapKV, and DynamicKV rank tokens or allocate cache capacity using attention or related relevance signals. Second, jailbreak defenses operate at the prompt, representation, or cache level. RobustKV is the closest cache-level comparator, but the authors characterize it as a defense that evicts tokens rather than a general compression layer that preserves a small cache. Third, representation-engineering work finds directions associated with concepts or refusal in residual activations. Arditi et al. report that refusal behavior is mediated by a direction that can be extracted by contrasting harmful and harmless prompts.[^refusal]

AnchorKV’s proposed gap lies between the first and third families: construct the behavioral direction directly in the key space used by an eviction layer, then incorporate it into the compressor’s ranking rather than projecting a residual-stream direction through another mapping.

### 1.3 The paper’s proposed gap

The paper’s preliminary experiment is the key motivation. For self-attack on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, mean ASR rises from 0.440 with FullKV to 0.506 with FastKV at retention rate 0.25. For attack transfer from Mistral-NeMo-Instruct-2407, ASR rises from 0.299 to 0.632. Both comparisons use ten attack seeds. This demonstrates an interaction, not its universal cause: changing the cache policy changes attack success materially, and the transferred condition shows the larger shift.

The authors interpret the interaction as refusal-evidence dilution and a change in response mode when the retained prompt backbone is distorted. That mechanism is plausible and is partly supported by the penalty and utility sweeps, but token-level mediation is not directly measured. A stricter mechanistic experiment would trace which evicted tokens alter refusal logits and compare that attribution with the anchor score.

## 2. Formal and technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Definitions and assumptions

Let a prompt contain (L) tokens. At a chosen layer \(\ell^*\), each token has per-head key vectors (K_t^{(\ell^*,h)}\), with (H) heads and head dimension (d). The unmodified FastKV/TSP procedure assigns each eligible token an importance score and retains (N-w) positions from the earlier prompt plus a protected recent window of (w) tokens. The retained count (N) is controlled by retention rate \(\rho\).

Anchor construction assumes labeled harmful and harmless prompt sets drawn from the AdvPrompter 60/20/20 split of AdvBench. It also assumes that a single linear direction in key space is a useful summary of harm/refusal-relevant variation and that the selected layer remains appropriate at deployment.

### 2.2 Objective and central equations

For each prompt (p), the paper first averages layer-(\ell^*\) key tensors across its tokens, producing a prompt representation \(\phi(p)\). The harm anchor is a difference of class means:

\[
v_h = \frac{1}{|D_h^{tr}|}\sum_{p\in D_h^{tr}}\phi(p)
- \frac{1}{|D_s^{tr}|}\sum_{p\in D_s^{tr}}\phi(p).
\]

The subtraction direction matters: it points from the harmless class mean toward the harmful class mean under the authors’ convention. For token (t), the harm score averages the dot product between each head’s key and the normalized anchor for that head. Prompt-level anchor validation averages token scores again. A validation-set threshold \(\theta\) is the midpoint between harmful and harmless median prompt scores, which compensates for the shared negative bias observed in both distributions.

Online, AnchorKV uses the positive excess (h_t^+=\max(h_t-\theta,0)\). The modified ranking subtracts a scaled penalty from the compressor’s original token score. The exact normalization is important: it aims to make \(\lambda\) interpretable despite differing score scales. Two safeguards set the penalty to zero for a short system-prefix region and for a protected fraction of top attention-ranked backbone tokens. The final set remains a top-k selection plus the recent window.

Two identities define the guarantee boundary. At \(\lambda=0\), the authors report bit-exact equivalence to FastKV. Separately, the retention count is independent of \(\lambda\), and the original importance score is independent of \(\rho\). These are interface properties, not behavioral guarantees: the cache size is fixed, but which tokens survive can change sharply.

### 2.3 Components

The pipeline contains an offline contrastive dataset; layerwise key extraction; per-layer anchors; validation AUROC and effect-size selection; a threshold; the inherited FastKV token score; a nonnegative harm-intensity transform; protected prefix and content-backbone masks; and a top-k selector. The backbone model, attack generator, LongBench evaluation, and FastKV implementation are inherited rather than novel.

### 2.4 Training process

AnchorKV does not train the language model or a classifier. Its offline fitting process is:

1. Run length-matched harmful and harmless prompts through the target model and collect keys at candidate layers.
2. Average token keys per prompt and subtract class means to obtain one per-layer anchor.
3. Score validation prompts and compare harmful/harmless distributions using AUROC and absolute Cohen’s (d).
4. Select layer 15, which reports validation AUROC 0.991 and |(d)| 3.84; layer 18 is nearly tied.
5. Calibrate \(\theta\) from validation medians, then freeze the anchor, layer, and threshold for testing and online use.

The held-out test AUROC is 0.996 for 104 harmful and 104 harmless prompts. This is good evidence that the contrast persists within the sampled distribution. It does not validate out-of-distribution harmless queries, multilingual prompts, novel jailbreak families, or model revisions.

### 2.5 Inference process

At the eviction point, the inherited compressor computes its normal importance score. AnchorKV computes each token’s projection onto the key-space anchor, removes values below the threshold, applies structural protections, subtracts \(\lambda\) times the remaining penalty, and selects the same number of tokens as FastKV. The recent window is always retained. Decode thereafter uses the compressed cache normally.

### 2.6 Notation and implementation audit

The central sign convention is internally coherent only if larger post-threshold anchor scores correspond to tokens the method intends to penalize in the retention ranking. The paper’s class distributions are both negative, so threshold calibration is not optional. Omitting it would make zero a misleading semantic boundary. The headwise anchor normalization also means magnitude differences across heads are intentionally discarded; that improves scale control but could suppress useful confidence information.

No implementation was available to compare equation defaults, exact score normalization, prefix length (S=4), protected attention fraction \(\alpha=0.30\), or deterministic top-k tie handling. Therefore “bit-exact at zero” remains paper-reported rather than independently checked.

## 3. Architecture and information flow

### 3.1 Representation

The safety signal lives in (H\times d) key space at one layer rather than the residual stream. That choice aligns the auxiliary score with the representation the compressor actually ranks. It also binds the artifact to a particular model architecture, layer numbering, head layout, tokenizer, and prompt template.

### 3.2 Decision module

AnchorKV is a score-composition module, not a new cache manager. Its decision is scalar per token: original utility score minus calibrated safety penalty. This makes it operationally simple and preserves FastKV’s downstream top-k machinery.

### 3.3 Dependencies and post-processing

The system depends on a safety-labeled anchor set, an eviction-based compressor exposing key vectors and token scores, and protected structural regions. There is no independent safety classifier at generation time, no refusal verifier after decoding, and no fallback to FullKV when the anchor is uncertain.

## 4. The complete operational pipeline

### 4.1 End-to-end flow

Data preparation establishes harmful/harmless splits. Offline extraction builds and selects an anchor. Deployment freezes that configuration. Each compressed request then flows through ordinary prefill, anchor-aware ranking at the designated layer, fixed-budget selection, cache continuation, and normal generation. Evaluation measures both behavior (AdvPrompter ASR) and benign long-context utility (sixteen LongBench tasks).

### 4.2 Resource allocation

The paper argues that anchor scoring adds linear work over prompt tokens and no model call. Cache memory is unchanged relative to FastKV because the number of retained tokens is identical. However, deployment cost should include key-score computation, masking, top-k selection, anchor storage, and any loss of fused-kernel efficiency. Those effects are not reported as end-to-end latency or throughput.

### 4.3 Guarantee boundary

The method guarantees neither safe outputs nor robustness. It preserves a cache cardinality and, at \(\lambda=0\), is claimed to preserve the baseline selection exactly. All safety effects are empirical. The reversal at \(\lambda\ge100\), and especially the rebound beyond baseline at \(\lambda=300\), is direct evidence against treating penalty strength as a safety certificate.

## 5. Independent re-conceptualization

### 5.1 Behaviorally asymmetric rate–distortion

Ordinary compression asks which distortion is acceptable at a target rate. AnchorKV adds the observation that equal average task distortion can hide asymmetric behavioral harm. Losing one set of tokens may reduce a QA score slightly; losing another may unlock a prohibited behavior. The method approximates a constrained rate–distortion objective in which refusal-related loss carries extra cost.

**Boundary of the analogy:** the paper does not estimate a formal distortion measure or solve a constrained optimization problem. The anchor penalty is a heuristic surrogate, and refusal-related behavior is not fully represented by one direction.

### 5.2 A safety overlay for a resource allocator

The score composition resembles a policy overlay: a base allocator proposes high-utility items, while a safety rule demotes a subset. This framing explains why the safeguard masks matter. Without protecting the content backbone, the overlay can overrule the allocator so aggressively that the prompt becomes semantically incoherent and safety degrades.

### 5.3 Transferable design principle

When a systems optimization discards internal state, evaluate behavioral invariants separately from task averages, and make the constraint signal native to the representation where the discard decision occurs.

## 6. Experimental design reconstructed

### 6.1 Data

Anchor construction and jailbreak evaluation use AdvBench through the AdvPrompter split. The reported split supplies matched harmful and harmless prompts; validation and test each contain 104 examples per class for the anchor analysis. Safety evaluation uses AdvPrompter under self-attack and transfer settings. Benign utility uses the complete sixteen-task LongBench suite.[^longbench]

### 6.2 Models, settings, and hardware

The central target is Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct. A Mistral-NeMo-Instruct-2407 attacker is used in the transfer condition. The compressor retention rate is 0.25 in the motivating experiment. The paper reports ten seeds for Figure 1, but Figure 4’s penalty sweep is described as using the same single seed. Hardware, wall-clock overhead, energy, and anchor-extraction cost are not sufficiently characterized for a systems-efficiency claim.

### 6.3 Baselines

FullKV establishes the uncompressed target behavior, and FastKV is the inherited compressor to which AnchorKV is added. Prior methods such as SnapKV and RobustKV motivate the design but are not a matched, broad experimental baseline table for the anchor-aware method. This limits claims about superiority over alternative safety-preserving cache policies.

### 6.4 Metrics

ASR is higher-is-worse and depends on the attack generator and success judge. AUROC measures class ranking, not calibration or robustness. Cohen’s (d) measures standardized separation. LongBench uses each task’s official metric and reports an unweighted average across sixteen heterogeneous tasks. The average is convenient but not a universal utility measure.

### 6.5 Runtime semantics

The paper’s computational-overhead argument is asymptotic and component-level. It does not show request latency, time-to-first-token, decode throughput, memory allocator effects, or hardware-normalized serving results. Thus “drop-in scalable” is plausible at the algorithmic interface but not established as an optimized serving result.

## 7. Results: what is reported and what it means

### 7.1 Compression-induced safety regression

Figure 1 reports that FastKV raises mean ASR from 0.440 to 0.506 for self-attack and from 0.299 to 0.632 for transferred attack. The across-seed distributions support a real configuration effect. The transfer result is especially material because it more than doubles mean ASR.

**Interpretation:** an accuracy-preserving cache policy is not behavior-preserving under these attacks.

**Qualification:** one model/compressor/attack setting cannot establish that KV compression generally weakens alignment.

### 7.2 Anchor discrimination

The candidate-layer sweep peaks in the middle layers. Layer 15 reports validation AUROC 0.991 and absolute effect size 3.84; layer 18 has the same AUROC and effect size 3.80. The frozen layer obtains test AUROC 0.996. Harmful and harmless medians remain negative but are separated (approximately -0.66 versus -1.32).

**Interpretation:** a linear class-separating signal exists in the chosen key space for this dataset.

**Qualification:** class separation does not show that the same projection is causally necessary for refusal or robust against optimized prompts.

### 7.3 Safety–utility frontier

Figure 4 reports an effective range around \(\lambda\in[1,30]\), reducing ASR from 0.68 at the FastKV baseline to roughly 0.13–0.17, with a best relative reduction near 81%. The curve has rapid onset below 1 and reverses at very large penalties. Figure 5 and Table 1 show small average LongBench loss for \(\lambda\le3\), about one point at \(\lambda=10\), and larger losses later. LCC drops from 60.00 to 43.60 at \(\lambda=300\).

**Interpretation:** moderate re-ranking can improve the measured attack/utility trade-off; excessive re-ranking destroys useful context.

**Qualification:** the main sweep’s single-seed design and lack of adaptive attack evaluation leave uncertainty around the width and stability of the “effective” region.

## 8. Ablations and causal evidence

### 8.1 Layer and threshold selection

The layer sweep shows that the signal is not equally distributed, and the held-out split limits direct selection leakage. Threshold calibration addresses the shared negative bias. What is missing is a downstream ASR-by-layer ablation: high AUROC need not select the layer that best preserves refusal under eviction.

### 8.2 Penalty and safeguards

The \(\lambda\) sweep demonstrates that the auxiliary score matters and that more is not always better. The protected prefix and top-attention backbone are mechanistically motivated, but the evidence would be stronger with a factorial ablation: no prefix protection, no backbone protection, both protections, and matched retention.

### 8.3 Randomness and statistics

Ten attack seeds in the motivating experiment are a strength. The main AnchorKV sweep does not provide comparable uncertainty, independent anchor-data resampling, multiple target-model seeds, or correction for selecting an operating range. No statistical test establishes unchanged LongBench quality.

## 10. External views and field context

### 10.1 Predecessor evidence

Representation-engineering work provides the conceptual basis for a refusal-related direction, but it primarily studies residual activations rather than eviction-layer keys.[^refusal] AnchorKV’s space alignment is therefore a meaningful adaptation rather than a new proof that refusal is linear.

### 10.2 Benchmark perspective

LongBench is a multi-task long-context benchmark, so it is appropriate for detecting broad benign-task degradation.[^longbench] Its unweighted mean should not be interpreted as a deployment-weighted utility score. A production service would weight tasks and failure costs differently.

### 10.3 Alternatives

RobustKV and attention-based cache defenses represent an alternative intervention family; the paper argues their operational objective differs. A matched evaluation should compare them at equal retained tokens, equal attack budget, and equal runtime. Security-aware prompt compression is another neighboring approach but acts before or outside the specific FastKV selection point.

### 10.4 Publication, code, and reproducibility status

The inspected source is arXiv v1. No official AnchorKV repository, tagged release, checkpoint, or immutable evaluation configuration was verified. The experiment therefore remains inspectable as a paper artifact but was not independently runnable or reproduced.[^code-status]

## 11. Research notes and critical considerations

### 11.1 Internal consistency

The main narrative, figures, and reported regime boundaries are consistent: moderate penalties help, excessive penalties harm. The most important qualification is methodological, not a numerical contradiction: Figure 1 uses ten seeds, while the principal penalty curve uses one seed.

### 11.2 Metric and baseline concerns

ASR depends on success classification and attack fluency. LongBench average can conceal localized regressions. The baseline set does not isolate whether gains come from the specific refusal anchor, from any contrastive prompt direction, or from generic diversity added to the retention score.

### 11.3 Unsupported or overbroad claims

“Safety-aware” is supported as a design intent and measured effect, not a safety guarantee. “Scalable” lacks optimized serving evidence. Generalization to other models, languages, compressors, safety policies, and adaptive attacks is unresolved.

### 11.4 Alternative explanations

The penalty may work because it preserves or removes prompt-format tokens correlated with the harmful dataset rather than because it preserves a causal refusal state. It may also regularize FastKV’s overly concentrated selection more generally. Random-direction, shuffled-label, and topic-matched controls are needed.

### 11.5 Unresolved questions

- Does an adaptive attacker that optimizes against anchor-aware retention recover the lost ASR?
- Is key-space harm direction stable across quantization, fine-tuning, safety templates, languages, and model upgrades?
- Which protected tokens causally change refusal logits, and how do they overlap with the anchor-ranked set?
- Can uncertainty in the anchor score trigger a conservative FullKV or safer-compressor fallback?

## 12. Potential implications

### 12.1 Scientific implications

Behavioral alignment can be more fragile than aggregate task capability under systems compression. This motivates evaluating internal-state approximations with behavioral suites rather than only perplexity or accuracy.

### 12.2 System-design implications

Cache managers should expose policy hooks, version safety artifacts with the model, log selected-token statistics, and validate operating points after model or prompt-template changes. Retention configuration is part of the model’s behavioral provenance.

### 12.3 Broader implications

The same pattern may arise in retrieval, memory consolidation, pruning, and speculative execution: an efficiency layer can selectively erase safeguards while preserving average utility. The transfer is a hypothesis, not an established result.

## 13. New hypotheses derived from the paper

These are reviewer proposals, not paper findings.

### Hypothesis 1: anchor benefit is mediated by token-set overlap

**Proposition:** safety improves in proportion to the overlap between anchor-affected tokens and tokens whose restoration changes refusal logits.  
**Motivating evidence:** moderate penalties reduce ASR while a large penalty destroys the content backbone.  
**Predicted observation:** restoring the highest-causal-refusal tokens to FastKV will approximate AnchorKV’s benefit.  
**Falsifying observation:** safety changes without overlap or causal-logit effect.  
**Minimum test:** token-level counterfactual cache restoration on a stratified attack set.

### Hypothesis 2: a low-rank anchor improves transfer but worsens attack surface

**Proposition:** multiple directions will generalize across safety concepts better than one direction but will give an adaptive attacker a larger, learnable scoring surface.  
**Predicted observation:** cross-family ASR falls for nonadaptive attacks, while white-box adaptive attacks recover more strongly.  
**Falsifying observation:** low-rank anchors improve both transfer and adaptive robustness.  
**Minimum test:** rank-1, rank-4, and nonlinear scorers under matched attack compute.

### Hypothesis 3: safety–utility reversal can be detected online

**Proposition:** the reversal regime correlates with observable collapse in retained-token diversity or protected-backbone coverage.  
**Predicted observation:** a threshold on these statistics identifies high-\(\lambda\) failures before generation.  
**Falsifying observation:** unsafe reversal occurs with normal coverage statistics.  
**Minimum test:** log token categories, entropy, and refusal outcomes across the full penalty sweep.

## 14. Deployment and governance considerations

### 14.1 Appropriate use

AnchorKV is best treated as an experimental mitigation layered on a known compressor, not as a standalone jailbreak defense. It requires frozen model, tokenizer, template, anchor data, layer, threshold, and penalty provenance.

### 14.2 Poor fit and failure modes

It is a poor fit when attackers know the scoring rule, the harm distribution differs from anchor data, the model changes frequently, or refusal must be guaranteed. Failure modes include false positives on benign sensitive topics, false negatives for novel attacks, content-backbone cannibalization, and silent drift.

### 14.3 Required safeguards

1. Keep an independent output-level safety policy and deterministic deny/fallback path.
2. Revalidate ASR and benign false-positive rates after any model or prompt change.
3. Monitor anchor-score and retained-token distributions for drift and reversal indicators.
4. Use FullKV or a conservative mode for uncertain/high-impact requests.
5. Preserve request, model, anchor, and cache-policy provenance without logging sensitive prompt content unnecessarily.

## 15. Replication and falsification agenda

### 15.1 Highest-priority unresolved result

Rerun the full \(\lambda\) sweep with at least ten attack seeds, bootstrap intervals, multiple anchor resamples, and the same attacker configurations as Figure 1. Archive ASR, benign refusal rate, and token selections. The central result survives only if the moderate region remains separated from FastKV under uncertainty.

### 15.2 Reproduce the full pipeline

Archive dataset hashes, model revision, tokenizer, chat template, candidate layers, key tensors or deterministic extraction commands, thresholds, FastKV revision, attack configuration, judge, and LongBench evaluator. Verify bit-exact \(\lambda=0\) equivalence before evaluating gains.

### 15.3 Normalize evaluation

Compare FullKV, FastKV, AnchorKV, RobustKV, random-direction penalty, shuffled-label anchor, and topic-matched anchors at equal retained tokens and attack compute. Report task-level LongBench results, latency, TTFT, throughput, and memory.

### 15.4 Test the proposed mechanism directly

Measure token overlap with causal cache-restoration effects and refusal-logit changes. If anchor-ranked tokens are not causally special, the mechanism should be revised.

### 15.5 Test realistic shifts and alternatives

Evaluate multiple aligned models, compressors, languages, jailbreak families, benign safety-adjacent prompts, adaptive attackers, and model revisions. A deployment claim requires stable performance across these shifts.

## 16. Durable restatement

> AnchorKV shows that fixed-budget KV-cache compression can preserve average long-context utility while changing refusal behavior, and that a calibrated key-space contrastive signal can partially correct the retention policy under one tested model and attack regime. The result is an empirical mitigation with a non-monotonic control knob, not a safety guarantee.

What should be remembered is the evaluation principle: a cache policy is part of behavioral model state. If it discards information, its correctness contract must include the behaviors the system is expected to preserve.
