# Learning the Eviction Boundary from Future Attention

## A full-paper review and independent re-conceptualization of “KVpop — Key-Value Cache Compression with Predictive Online Pruning”

**Paper:** Lukas Hauzenberger, Niklas Schmidinger, Anamaria-Roberta Hartl, David Stap, Thomas Schmied, Sebastian Böck, Günter Klambauer, and Sepp Hochreiter, arXiv:2607.05061v2, revised 8 July 2026.[^1]  
**Scope:** complete 28-page paper, main text, appendices, three tables, nine numbered figures, three algorithms, central equation families, acknowledgments, public checkpoint, and external primary-source checks.  
**Review activity:** full-source reconstruction and artifact inspection. Models were not downloaded or executed; results were not reproduced.

---

## Executive assessment

### One-sentence assessment

KVpop turns permanent KV eviction into a supervised ranking problem whose target is post-window future attention, and its released 8B checkpoint makes the method unusually concrete, although headline retention percentages conflict across the abstract, introduction, table, and conclusion, and the evaluation remains concentrated on sampled mathematical reasoning.

### What is genuinely new

Many eviction methods score tokens by past or current attention. KVpop asks a cleaner training question: once a token leaves a protected recent window and must compete for a fixed long-range budget, did future dense-attention queries actually need it? That target supervises a per-head scorer. A stateful mLSTM variant delays the score until the eviction boundary, allowing the scorer to observe the token’s near future while it is still protected. At inference, future targets disappear; only the scorer and fixed sink/window/top-k cache remain.

The implementation contribution is as important as the loss. The authors derive a transposed-attention computation that obtains per-key future-attention mass from log-sum-exp outputs without materializing an `S×S` dense map. They sort static token priorities and use a Fenwick tree to maintain the running top-k cutoff, handing the predicate to FlexAttention rather than storing a dense mask. This aligns the learning objective, bounded-cache data structure, and sparse kernel.

### Bottom-line judgment

The paper is technically substantial and much more reproducible than most cache-compression preprints. It gives full hyperparameters, reports 8 H100 training, describes numerical precision, distinguishes stateless and stateful scorers, includes algorithms and derivations, and links a self-contained Hugging Face checkpoint with custom code, cache backends, weights, requirements, and a generation check.[^2]

The empirical evidence supports the narrow claim that future-attention supervision plus delayed stateful scoring preserves sampled reasoning accuracy better than the tested eviction baselines at 75% and 88% nominal compression. In Table 1, KVpop reaches 0.98 and 0.97 of the Qwen3-4B teacher at those budgets and 1.00 for Qwen3-8B at both budgets. It also generalizes reasonably to GPQA-D and LiveCodeBench and holds memory near 19 GB in the reported long-generation benchmark.

Three qualifications matter. First, the document is internally inconsistent. The abstract says Qwen3-4B retains 98% at 75% compression and 97% at 88%, matching Table 1. The introduction and conclusion instead say 95% and 94%. For Qwen3-8B, Table 1 reports 100% relative at both budgets, while the conclusion says 95% and 99%. These are not rounding differences and must be corrected.

Second, pass@1 uses 16 stochastic rollouts per prompt, but no confidence intervals or paired tests are reported. AIME and HMMT have small item counts, so ratios near 1.00 can move materially with a few successes. “Retains 100%” does not establish equivalence.

Third, future attention is a teacher-policy proxy, not ground-truth utility. Attention mass can preserve redundant or stylistic tokens and can miss low-mass tokens whose values matter. The target also uses sparse-student log normalizers as an approximation to dense normalizers, making the teacher signal partly dependent on the current sparse policy. The paper reports downstream success, but the exact versus approximate target deserves a direct ablation.

Overall, KVpop is a credible, implementation-backed learned eviction system with strong preliminary results. It should be replicated on deterministic long-context retrieval, summarization, dialogue, and mixed workloads before its math-reasoning frontier is generalized to serving.

## 1. Problem framing and baseline taxonomy

Autoregressive attention avoids recomputing past keys and values by storing them. The persistent cache grows linearly with sequence length. Sparse retrieval reads a subset per query but retains full history, reducing bandwidth rather than persistent memory. Eviction permanently removes entries, enforcing a hard cap but risking unrecoverable loss.

KVpop’s cache per KV head contains three regions:

- `s` sink tokens that are always retained;
- `w` recent tokens protected from eviction;
- `k` older tokens chosen by learned score.

The fixed budget is Equation 1: `B = s + w + k`. This is important because every head has the same bounded layout. DMS can allocate capacity unevenly across heads, which may improve flexibility but creates ragged execution. KVpop’s homogeneous budget makes static buffers and compiled sparse kernels easier.

The paper distinguishes training-free StreamingLLM and TOVA, a trained fixed-pattern StreamingLLM+, learned Dynamic Memory Sparsification, a stateless KVpop MLP, and stateful KVpop. Sparse-retrieval systems such as Quest and TokenButler are excluded because they keep the full cache. That boundary is principled for memory evaluation, although a serving study should compare both persistent bytes and attention-read bandwidth.

## 2. Technical reconstruction of supervision

### 2.1 Future-attention mass

Let sequence length be `S`, protected window `w`, KV head `h`, and GQA query group `g`. For token `t`, the target averages causal attention it receives from queries at least `w` positions later:

`m(h,g,t) = (1/Nt) Σ[d=t+w to S-1] p(h,g,d→t)`.

`Nt = max(1, S-(t+w))` normalizes the available future-query count. The target score aggregates log mass across query heads sharing one KV head:

`r_tgt(h,t) = Agg_g [log(ε + m(h,g,t))]`.

The experiments use maximum aggregation across GQA groups. Max protects a token needed by any query head but can over-retain head-specific outliers. The appendix considers alternatives, count normalization, temporal decay, priorities, and sampling.

This target improves on accumulated past attention because it directly labels future usefulness under the teacher. It remains an attention-based proxy. A token with modest attention but a large or cancellation-sensitive value can matter more than mass suggests. Conversely, repeated high attention can reflect sinks or formatting.

### 2.2 Eligibility and effective scores

At query `q`, tokens are eligible for the long-range budget if `s ≤ t ≤ q-w`. The newly eligible token is `q-w`. Both teacher and predicted scores include a discrete age-decay term controlled by learned per-head `γh`. The policy keeps the top `k` eligible scores.

Training focuses on the eviction boundary. The important error is whether the newly eligible token should displace the current weakest retained token. A pairwise boundary loss separates the new token’s score from the teacher boundary with optional margin weighting and keep/drop balancing. This is more aligned than regressing every score uniformly because only ordering around the cutoff changes cache membership.

### 2.3 Efficient transposed-attention target

Naively computing every key’s future post-softmax attention mass requires an `S×S` map. The paper reuses log-sum-exp normalizers from the forward attention pass. After transposing query and key roles, a per-key column sum becomes a row-wise reduction available through the same masked attention kernel interface.

The exact identity needs dense causal query normalizers. For efficiency, KVpop substitutes sparse-student LSE values. This avoids a separate dense-attention pass but changes the target. If the student already evicted evidence, its denominator differs from the teacher’s. The authors say the approximation matches dense-LSE downstream performance, but no prominent table isolates exact versus sparse LSE, runtime, and target correlation. This should be a required replication.

The training-only target adds no inference overhead. It does add training compute and interacts with knowledge distillation: existing model weights train at constant `8e-5`, new scorer parameters warm up then decay from `1e-3`, and the KL teacher term uses only the top 256 teacher logits.

## 3. Online scorer architecture and inference pipeline

### 3.1 Stateless scorer

The stateless variant concatenates a token’s cached key and value and applies a per-head two-layer MLP with SiLU. It scores at insertion using only the token representation. Its advantage is low overhead and easy parallelism; its limitation is no evidence from later tokens.

### 3.2 Stateful delayed scorer

The stateful scorer maintains an mLSTM-like matrix memory per KV head. When token `t=q-w` leaves protection, the scorer has already processed context through `q`; it queries the accumulated state with a projection of `t` and emits a scalar score. This is causal at inference because it only uses tokens already generated. Relative to token `t`, it sees `w` steps of near-future evidence.

The distinction from merely delayed eviction is important. A policy can keep a token in a window yet decide its fate at insertion. KVpop delays the decision itself while remaining causal.[^4] Figure 5 reports only a 0.2-point token-accuracy gain after 2,000 steps, so the dramatic task gains cannot be assigned to delay alone; future-target supervision and full-model distillation likely dominate.

The paper correctly charges scorer memory against the stateful top-k budget. The released 4× 8B checkpoint uses 4 sinks, 256 recent tokens, 3,772 scored tokens, and an mLSTM memory cost equivalent to 64 KV entries, totaling the intended 4,096-entry quarter budget.[^2]

### 3.3 Running top-k with a Fenwick tree

Tokens receive static priorities adjusted by structured age decay. As one token becomes eligible per step, its rank is inserted into a Fenwick tree. Binary lifting retrieves the rank of the kth best eligible token, yielding cutoff `τq`. The mask checks whether a token’s rank is at most the cutoff.

The cutoff computation costs `O(S log S)` time and `O(S)` space per head over the sequence. FlexAttention evaluates the predicate inside the sparse kernel, avoiding a dense mask. A custom Triton kernel parallelizes batch elements and KV heads. This is a concrete systems design, though latency must include scorer, tree, mask, and attention work.

### 3.4 Full training path

Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 and Qwen3-8B are trained for 2,000 steps on high-reasoning-effort Nemotron-Math v2 examples, packed to 16,384 tokens. The paper states a 2B-token budget, global batch 128, AdamW, weight decay `1e-4`, beta `(0.9,0.95)`, FSDP across 8 H100 GPUs, bf16 parameters/compute, float32 reductions and ranking, and gradient clipping 1.0. All existing parameters are trainable, so “retrofit” here is full fine-tuning plus new scorers, not parameter-efficient attachment alone.

Packed segments intentionally share the attention mask rather than resetting at boundaries. That increases token utilization but allows cross-sample attention. The paper says it improved the hybrid architecture. It also changes the training distribution and should be tested for leakage-like artifacts between unrelated packed examples.

## 4. Experimental design and metrics

Main reasoning evaluation uses AIME 2024/2025 and HMMT February/November 2025. Pass@1 is estimated with 16 rollouts per prompt. Two budgets are tested: `B=4,096` or 75% compression and `B=2,048` or 88% compression, with sink/window components inside the budget. Qwen3-8B thinking mode is enabled.

Out-of-domain evaluation uses GPQA Diamond accuracy and LiveCodeBench v6 pass@1 for Qwen3-4B. These are useful because training is math-only, though both remain reasoning-heavy. There is no natural-language long-context retrieval, summarization, dialogue, or multi-document test.

Inference efficiency uses Qwen3-8B, batch one, 75% compression, generation lengths to 131k, and five steady-state runs excluding warmup and compilation. The paper reports peak allocated VRAM and end-to-end latency. It acknowledges that vLLM or SGLang paged-cache behavior remains untested.

The public checkpoint’s model card says inherited `max_position_embeddings` is 40,960 even though the paper benchmarks generation to 131k.[^2] This may reflect RoPE extrapolation, a benchmark-specific config, or a difference between released and measured artifacts. It needs explicit reconciliation.

## 5. Results reconstruction

### 5.1 Qwen3-4B

Teacher average pass@1 is 0.45 across four math sets. At 75% compression, KVpop and KVpop-MLP both average 0.44, or 0.98 relative. DMS is 0.43/0.96, StreamingLLM+ 0.41/0.92, StreamingLLM 0.34/0.76, and TOVA 0.33/0.73.

At 88%, KVpop remains 0.44/0.97, MLP is 0.42/0.93, DMS 0.40/0.89, StreamingLLM+ 0.33/0.74, TOVA 0.26/0.58, and StreamingLLM 0.21/0.47. The stateful method’s resilience at the tighter budget is the strongest table result.

### 5.2 Qwen3-8B

Teacher average is 0.43. At 75%, KVpop is 0.44/1.00, MLP 0.43/1.00, DMS 0.41/0.95, StreamingLLM+ 0.39/0.91, TOVA 0.26/0.60, and StreamingLLM 0.08/0.19. At 88%, KVpop is 0.43/1.00, MLP 0.42/0.98, DMS 0.36/0.84, StreamingLLM+ 0.29/0.67, and both training-free methods 0.08/0.19.

Because compressed variants sometimes exceed teacher scores, relative retention is not bounded below 1. The paper reports ratios rather than paired confidence intervals.[^5] Sampling noise and post-training adaptation could both explain apparent improvements.

### 5.3 Out-of-domain tasks

Qwen3-4B teacher scores 0.59 GPQA-D and 0.35 LCB. At 75%, KVpop scores 0.57/0.33; its MLP scores 0.59/0.35. DMS scores 0.55/0.37, showing that KVpop is not uniformly best. At 88%, KVpop is 0.56/0.34, MLP 0.57/0.35, DMS 0.54/0.35, and TOVA 0.54/0.35. The paper appropriately says methods are comparable on these smaller-budget reasoning tasks.

### 5.4 Systems results

Dense Qwen3-8B grows from about 18 GB at 16k to 36 GB at 131k. DMS and KVpop reportedly grow only 19% to about 19 GB. KVpop has lower latency at the longest generation lengths, attributed to homogeneous per-head capacity versus DMS’s ragged head allocation. Exact plotted latency values are not stated in prose and were not digitized.

## 6. Ablations and diagnostic evidence

The main controlled progression is StreamingLLM → StreamingLLM+ → learned policies. Training a fixed sparse pattern recovers substantial performance, so some benefit is model adaptation rather than the scorer. DMS versus KVpop compares objectives and execution at roughly matched scorer parameter count.

Stateful versus MLP scoring shows a larger advantage at tight budgets, but delayed versus immediate mLSTM yields only 0.2 token-accuracy points. Figure 7 reports 81% mean recall of the full-attention teacher’s top-k tokens. This validates policy alignment, not direct task causality. Figures 6 and 9 show qualitative retention of discourse, operation, and symbolic tokens and more eviction of pure numbers. Figure 8 shows DMS concentrating long-range capacity in a subset of heads.

Missing ablations include exact dense-LSE versus sparse-LSE targets, future-attention versus past-attention target under the same scorer, score-decay sensitivity, sink/window/top-k reallocation, max versus mean GQA aggregation, packed-mask reset, teacher-logit truncation, and deterministic task tests.

## 9. Research notes and critical considerations

### 9.1 Teacher attention is a policy, not an oracle

“Oracle” should be reserved for task-optimal retention. Full-attention mass is a teacher signal. Matching it can preserve the teacher’s quirks and attention sinks. A value-aware or loss-delta target may improve utility at greater training cost.

### 9.2 Irreversible eviction needs protected classes

Math traces are redundant, but production prompts contain system instructions, authorization constraints, citations, and exact tool outputs. Some tokens should be pinned by policy regardless of learned score. The cache API should support protected spans beyond initial sinks.

### 9.3 Full fine-tuning changes the comparison

KVpop trains all existing parameters with KL distillation. Its gains cannot be attributed solely to a small scorer attached to an unchanged pretrained model. A scorer-only run and a full-finetune fixed-pattern run at equal tokens are needed.

### 9.4 Ratio denominators should include scorer and indexes

The released checkpoint correctly accounts for mLSTM memory in the top-k budget. Reports should also include Fenwick/tree state, score buffers, static ranks, kernel workspace, and model-code overhead in peak-memory tables.

## 10. Limitations, implications, and deployment applicability

The authors identify the narrow scorer family, post-training retrofit setting, homogeneous per-head budget, and untested hybrid dense/sparse layers. Additional limitations are math-heavy training/evaluation, stochastic small-sample benchmarks, irreversible error, sparse-normalizer teacher approximation, no paged-serving evaluation, and licensing constraints.

For deployment, KVpop is most plausible for long reasoning generations where cache growth dominates and some intermediate steps are redundant. It is less proven for exact document retrieval, codebase navigation, conversation commitments, and tool-agent traces. A system should retain raw prompts externally, expose cache-pressure metrics, allow critical-span pinning, and fall back when scorer confidence degrades.

The model repository’s custom code and community license require supply-chain and policy review. Public availability does not imply permission for every commercial use. The paper reports EU funding and compute acknowledgments, but no energy or carbon accounting.

## 11. Independent re-conceptualization and falsifiable hypotheses

### Hypothesis 1: loss-delta supervision beats attention mass

For a sampled subset of boundary tokens, estimate teacher-logit KL increase when each token is removed. Train the same scorer on this target. It should improve exact reasoning at 88% compression. Falsify if future-attention mass matches or exceeds it.

### Hypothesis 2: protected semantic spans prevent catastrophic tails

Pin system instructions, citations, and tool-result delimiters while keeping total budget fixed by reducing learned top-k. Worst-case compliance and exact-retrieval failures should fall without reducing average reasoning more than one point.

### Hypothesis 3: sparse-LSE targets self-reinforce

Measure target correlation and downstream score for dense-LSE, sparse-LSE, and periodically refreshed dense-LSE training. If sparse denominators bias toward already retained tokens, periodic refresh should improve oracle recall. Falsify if all variants coincide.

### Hypothesis 4: stateful advantage grows with delayed dependencies

Construct tasks where a token’s value is revealed 16, 64, 256, or 1,024 steps later. Stateful delayed scoring should increasingly outperform insertion-time MLP until the protected window is exceeded.

## 12. Replication and falsification agenda

1. Correct the contradictory retention percentages in abstract, introduction, Table 1, and conclusion.
2. Publish exact commits for paper code, checkpoint, DMS baseline, Transformers, FlexAttention, and Triton.
3. Report prompt counts, rollout seeds, per-item successes, confidence intervals, and paired tests.
4. Add deterministic Needle, passkey, long-document QA, summarization, dialogue, and tool-trace workloads.
5. Compare dense versus sparse LSE targets at equal compute.
6. Run scorer-only, base-only, fixed-pattern full-finetune, and scorer-plus-base ablations.
7. Reconcile 131k benchmarking with the released checkpoint’s 40,960 configured maximum.
8. Measure tree/scorer/workspace memory and paged vLLM/SGLang throughput.
9. Stress critical instructions, negations, exact numbers, citations, and adversarial distraction.
10. Evaluate recovery strategies when an evicted token becomes relevant.

The central hypothesis is falsified if future-attention supervision does not outperform a matched past-attention or fixed-pattern policy across deterministic long-range tasks, or if full memory accounting removes its serving advantage.

## 13. Durable restatement

A fixed KV budget turns attention into an online ranking problem. The most informative training label is observed after a token leaves short-term protection: did later teacher queries use it enough to justify long-range residency? KVpop makes that label efficient, delays the decision to gain causal evidence, and realizes the ranking with a uniform cache plus sparse kernel. The design is credible and released. Its present evidence is strongest for sampled mathematical reasoning and should be read with corrected denominators, uncertainty, and broader long-context tests.
