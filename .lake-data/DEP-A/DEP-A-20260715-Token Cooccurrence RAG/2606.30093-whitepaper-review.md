# Statistical Graph Routing for Multi-Hop Retrieval

## A detailed review, technical reconstruction, and independent re-conceptualization of “Efficient Retrieval-Augmented Generation via Token Co-occurrence Graphs”

**Source paper:** Gianluca Bonifazi, Christopher Buratti, Michele Marchetti, Federica Parlapiano, Giulia Quaglieri, Davide Traini, Domenico Ursino, and Luca Virgili, “Efficient Retrieval-Augmented Generation via Token Co-occurrence Graphs,” arXiv:2606.30093v1.[^source-paper]

**Artifact prepared:** 2026-07-15  
**Local source reviewed:** complete 24-page PDF, matching full-paper arXiv HTML, references, declaration, data statement, ten tables, two figures, and source archive; private path withheld  
**Review scope:** full-paper reconstruction, claim vetting, external contextualization, research notes, implications, and replication agenda  
**Review status:** source artifacts and reported experiments were inspected; no experiment was independently rerun, and no official TIGRAG implementation was verified

---

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

TIGRAG replaces LLM-extracted knowledge graphs with a weighted token co-occurrence graph, then combines Personalized PageRank, lexical scoring, dynamic candidate pooling, dense reranking, and entity-driven query expansion to retrieve compact evidence for multi-hop question answering.

### The deeper idea

The contribution is best understood as **statistical topology used as a cheap proposal distribution**. Local token co-occurrence does not encode a semantic relation in the knowledge-graph sense; it encodes repeated textual neighborhood. TIGRAG turns that inexpensive neighborhood graph into a high-recall router and reserves the more semantically discriminating dense model for the last ranking step. The graph proposes paths; lexical and dense signals correct the proposal; the generator consumes only the surviving evidence.

### Bottom-line judgment

The paper supports a useful, bounded claim: on HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and MuSiQue, under one Gemma/Nomic evaluation stack, the proposed hybrid retrieves supporting chunks more effectively than the tested flat and graph baselines while using much less graph-construction machinery than LLM-extraction GraphRAG systems. The component ablations and hyperparameter sweeps make the mechanism reasonably credible. The evidence does not establish a universal GraphRAG replacement, causal or symbolic reasoning, robustness to domain shift, or a deployment-independent speedup. Exact resource comparisons depend on implementation, corpus size, hardware, retrieved-context length, and the shared generator. The lack of verified released code prevents inspection of preprocessing details and independent reproduction.

### Principal strengths

- The offline graph is deterministic given tokenization, windowing, filtering, and counts; it avoids a generative extraction stage whose cost and errors are difficult to audit.
- Evaluation connects retrieval quality, answer quality, indexing time, online time, and context footprint instead of reporting only final QA accuracy.
- Ablations distinguish PPR propagation, BM25 contribution, and neural reranking, while sweeps expose the noise/coverage trade-offs in chunk count, hop count, expansion, and cumulative-score pooling.

### Principal qualifications

1. Co-occurrence edges indicate textual proximity, not typed factual relations; topological relevance can therefore follow boilerplate, common entities, or corpus artifacts.
2. The study uses three related multi-hop QA benchmarks and one principal generation/embedding stack. Generality across domains, languages, corpus scales, and modern rerankers remains untested.
3. The paper reports relative improvements and within-study timings, but the public artifact boundary did not yield a verified official repository; experiments were not rerun.

## 1. The problem the paper is actually solving

### 1.1 Problem and stakes

Standard dense RAG maps a query and each passage into vectors and ranks passages largely as independent units. Multi-hop questions break this simplifying assumption when the first supporting passage contains a bridge entity needed to find the second. A flat retriever may retrieve one locally similar passage but miss the less obvious continuation. GraphRAG methods can represent connections, but many build entity-relation graphs with LLM calls or learned extractors. That raises an offline cost and creates an additional failure surface: omitted entities, hallucinated relations, inconsistent schemas, and hard-to-reproduce prompts.

TIGRAG targets the middle ground. It seeks structural routing without a semantic graph-construction model. The practical stakes are twofold. First, retrieval should reach dispersed supporting evidence. Second, the total system should not spend so much time or so many tokens constructing and traversing a graph that the structural gain is operationally unattractive.

### 1.2 Prior method families

The paper compares or discusses flat dense retrieval, hierarchical summary structures such as RAPTOR, LLM-built GraphRAG and LightRAG, graph traversal methods including ToG and KGP, and PPR-based memory-style retrieval such as HippoRAG2. These families differ in their retrieval unit. Dense RAG retrieves chunks; hierarchical methods retrieve summary nodes; entity graph systems retrieve entities or relations; and passage-graph systems traverse linked evidence. That distinction matters for evaluation: chunk Recall@k is not meaningful for baselines that return different object types, so the paper omits retrieval recall for several methods and compares them on generated answers instead.

### 1.3 The paper’s proposed gap

The authors argue that graph topology can be recovered cheaply from tokens that repeatedly occur near one another. This is a weaker representation than an explicit relation graph, but cheap enough to build with counts. The missing piece is an online pipeline that converts this noisy topology into precise chunks. TIGRAG supplies PPR expansion, provenance maps from tokens to chunks, BM25-modulated graph scores, a cumulative-mass candidate cutoff, dense reranking, and iterative bridge-entity expansion.

## 2. Formal and technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Definitions and assumptions

Let the corpus contain documents split into overlapping, sentence-aligned chunks. After normalization and tokenization, a graph has token nodes and weighted undirected edges for tokens that co-occur within a sliding window. Edge weight represents frequency, not relation type or direction. A token-to-chunk map preserves provenance, and every chunk receives a dense embedding from Nomic Embed Text v1.5. A query supplies graph seed tokens and a dense query vector.

The design assumes that local co-occurrence accumulates enough corpus-level structure to connect terms that participate in multi-step evidence chains. It also assumes the query contains or can acquire bridge tokens that occur in the graph. Rare entities removed by filtering, morphological variants missed by normalization, and relations expressed without lexical overlap are adverse cases.

### 2.2 Objective and central equations

The paper’s central operations form a sequence rather than one trainable objective. First, PPR diffuses probability from query seed tokens. In conventional notation, a stationary vector satisfies

\[
\pi = \alpha P^\top \pi + (1-\alpha)s,
\]

where \(P\) is the normalized token-graph transition matrix, \(s\) concentrates mass on query seeds, and \(\alpha\) controls continued traversal. TIGRAG selects the highest-scoring expanded tokens and L1-normalizes their PPR mass. The sign and normalization are straightforward: scores are nonnegative and interpreted as a distribution over selected tokens; a high score is greater graph proximity to the query under repeated weighted walks.

Second, a candidate chunk receives a graph-weighted lexical score. In plain language, each expanded token contributes its BM25 evidence in that chunk, scaled by the token’s PPR importance. This preserves term-frequency and inverse-document-frequency behavior while favoring tokens reached through the query-centered topology. It is not a probability of relevance, and BM25 magnitude is corpus-dependent.

Third, sorted candidate scores are accumulated until a fraction \(th_\rho\) of total score mass is retained. This is an adaptive pool-size rule: peaked score distributions yield small pools, while diffuse distributions yield larger pools. Dense cosine similarity between the query and candidate embeddings then produces the final order. For later hops, named entities from high-ranked retrieved chunks augment the query. This step is a heuristic state transition, not a learned reasoning policy.

### 2.3 Components

The inherited components are substantial: NLTK tokenization, spaCy named-entity recognition, BM25, PPR, Nomic embeddings, cosine reranking, and Gemma 3 27B generation. Novelty lies primarily in their arrangement around a token co-occurrence graph, the score-mass candidate pool, and iterative entity-driven multi-hop use. The paper should therefore be read as a retrieval architecture contribution, not a new graph algorithm, embedding model, or generator.

### 2.4 Training process

TIGRAG itself has no reported task-specific training phase.

1. Split documents into overlapping sentence-aligned chunks and normalize tokens.
2. Count sliding-window co-occurrences and construct the weighted token graph.
3. Create token-to-chunk provenance indexes and dense chunk embeddings.
4. Configure retrieval hyperparameters on the experimental setup.

The embedding and generation models are pretrained dependencies. Their original training is outside the method and evaluation budget.

### 2.5 Inference process

1. Normalize the user question and map surviving query tokens to graph seeds.
2. Run PPR, select graph-near tokens, and map them back to candidate chunks.
3. Score candidates with PPR-weighted BM25, retain a cumulative score-mass pool, and rerank it by dense similarity.
4. Return the top chunks to the generator under a fixed evidence-grounding prompt.
5. For multi-hop operation, extract bridge entities from the best evidence, augment the query, and repeat retrieval before final generation.

### 2.6 Notation and implementation audit

The equations are directionally consistent: larger PPR, BM25, and cosine scores all indicate higher priority, while the cumulative threshold retains rather than discards score mass. The main audit concern is semantic terminology. Calling the structure a knowledge graph may invite a stronger interpretation than the construction warrants. Nodes are tokens and edges are untyped co-occurrence counts. The graph can route lexical associations but cannot by itself distinguish “causes,” “located in,” “not,” or “formerly.” Implementation-sensitive choices—case folding, stop words, lemmatization, window width, edge pruning, overlap, tie breaking, PPR tolerance, and NER behavior—are central to reproduction and could not be checked against released code.

## 3. Architecture and information flow

### 3.1 Representation

TIGRAG maintains three synchronized views: a sparse token graph for structural expansion, an inverted provenance relation from tokens to chunks, and dense chunk vectors for semantic reranking. No one view is sufficient. The token graph connects distant evidence but is noisy; provenance returns readable text; dense vectors recover semantic query alignment after broad graph exploration.

### 3.2 Decision or action modules

PPR decides which graph neighborhood is worth exploring. The hybrid lexical score decides which chunks contain enough topological and lexical evidence. Cumulative pooling decides how many candidates survive. The dense model decides their final order. NER decides how the state changes between hops. These decisions are deterministic for fixed dependencies and parameters, except for any nondeterminism in model serving.

### 3.3 Dependencies and post-processing

The final answer depends on Gemma 3 27B and a fixed prompt. Therefore answer-level gains are jointly caused by retrieved evidence and generator behavior. TIGRAG does not validate citations or entailment after generation. A production system would need passage-level citations, answer-evidence checks, and an abstention rule when retrieval confidence or source agreement is low.

## 4. The complete operational pipeline

### 4.1 End-to-end flow

Offline processing transforms a document corpus into chunks, a weighted token graph, a provenance index, and embeddings. Online retrieval begins with graph seeds from the query, spreads relevance through PPR, translates expanded tokens into candidate chunks, ranks them lexically under graph weights, chooses an adaptive candidate set, and reranks semantically. Selected chunks feed a standardized QA prompt. Multi-hop retrieval reuses entities from earlier evidence as new cues.

### 4.2 Resource allocation

The principal budgets are graph size, PPR expansion count, cumulative-mass threshold, number of chunks sent to generation, number of entity-bearing chunks used for expansion, and hop count. The sweeps favor five final chunks, one top chunk for bridge-entity induction in most settings, two hops, and a 0.8 cumulative-score threshold. Three hops exceed the benchmark’s maximum intended chain length and add noise. Those defaults are evidence for these datasets, not universal constants.

### 4.3 Guarantee boundary

There is no guarantee that the graph contains the required bridge, that PPR reaches it, that the candidate cutoff preserves it, or that the generator uses it. Statistical graph construction guarantees reproducibility only after all preprocessing details are fixed. Reported superiority is empirical. Exact matching of a source passage can be validated after retrieval, but final correctness and faithfulness require separate checks.

## 5. Re-conceptualization

### 5.1 Cheap proposal, expensive correction

The durable mechanism is a proposal–corrector pipeline. Token topology cheaply expands recall beyond direct query similarity. BM25 and dense similarity then correct the noisy proposal. This interpretation predicts that gains will be largest where a bridge is lexically present in an intermediate passage but weakly aligned with the original query.

**Boundary of the analogy:** the corrector is only a reranker over proposed candidates. If graph expansion never proposes the needed evidence, dense similarity cannot recover it.

### 5.2 Retrieval as controlled diffusion

PPR behaves like controlled diffusion from query tokens through repeated corpus neighborhoods. The cumulative-score threshold sets a porous boundary around that diffusion. Bridge-entity expansion restarts it from newly observed coordinates. This explains why degree centrality fails in the ablation: global popularity is not the same as query-conditioned reachability.

**Boundary of the analogy:** diffusion over co-occurrence does not carry logical truth conditions. Negation, role direction, and temporal ordering can be lost.

### 5.3 Transferable design principle

When a precise structural representation is expensive, use a cheap high-recall structure to generate candidates and preserve provenance so a semantically stronger but bounded component can make the final decision. The principle applies beyond text, provided proposal errors remain observable and correction has access to the original evidence.

## 6. Experimental design reconstructed

### 6.1 Data

The study uses HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and MuSiQue, all multi-hop QA datasets. The graph is built per dataset corpus, and Table 1 reports graph statistics. The reviewed source describes standard benchmark protocols but does not establish cross-domain or multilingual transfer. These datasets emphasize discoverable supporting facts and short answers; they do not cover enterprise document churn, adversarial content, long legal or scientific synthesis, or access-control constraints.

### 6.2 Training and hardware

No TIGRAG training is reported. Generation uses `gemma3:27b-it-qat`; Nomic Text v1.5 supplies embeddings; NLTK and spaCy supply preprocessing and NER. The paper reports indexing and query timings, but timing meaning remains tied to the authors’ software and hardware environment. Without released runnable code and a normalized hardware record, the results should not be converted into general latency promises or cost estimates.

### 6.3 Baselines

Baselines include NoRAG, NaiveRAG, RAPTOR, GraphRAG, LightRAG, ToG, KGP, and HippoRAG2. A common generation prompt reduces one source of asymmetry. Still, baseline objects differ: some return chunks, some entities, and some summaries. Retrieval recall is omitted where incompatible, which is methodologically preferable to forcing a misleading denominator, but it leaves answer metrics as the only common comparison. It is not clear from the public paper alone whether every baseline received equal tuning effort, equivalent cache reuse, or the same degree of engineering optimization.

### 6.4 Metrics

Retrieval uses Recall@2 and Recall@5: the fraction of questions whose gold supporting evidence appears within the first two or five retrieved chunks. Generation uses exact match and token-level F1. Exact match penalizes paraphrase and additional correct detail; F1 rewards partial token overlap but does not directly measure citation faithfulness. Resource metrics include graph/index build time, end-to-end online time, and retrieved-context tokens. The online time includes retrieval and LLM response time, so shorter prompts can dominate a slower retrieval stage.

### 6.5 Runtime semantics

NaiveRAG has the cheapest indexing because it does not build a graph. TIGRAG is reported to be substantially faster to index than LLM-extraction graph systems. Online, TIGRAG is slightly slower than NaiveRAG on HotpotQA but faster on 2WikiMultiHopQA and MuSiQue; the paper attributes this to reduced prompt processing outweighing graph traversal. It is reported at roughly four times the speed of RAPTOR, the fastest graph baseline in that comparison. These are end-to-end, stack-specific observations, not a pure PPR microbenchmark.

## 7. Results: what is reported and what it means

### 7.1 Retrieval and answer quality

Table 2 reports consistent retrieval gains. Relative to the named comparator, TIGRAG improves HotpotQA R@2 by 9.70% and R@5 by 8.23%; on 2WikiMultiHopQA, R@2 improves by 13.41% and R@5 by 10.76%; on MuSiQue, R@2 improves by 19.92% and R@5 by 9.73%. The comparator is not identical in every statement—NaiveRAG is used for several comparisons and HippoRAG2 for others—so these percentages must not be read as one uniform baseline delta.

Table 3 reports HotpotQA gains of 13.67% exact match and 11.97% F1 over NaiveRAG. On 2WikiMultiHopQA, the reported improvements over HippoRAG2 are 1.39% exact match and 5.45% F1. On MuSiQue, HippoRAG2 has slightly higher exact match while TIGRAG has higher F1. That mixed result is important: the evidence supports a stronger overlap score, not unqualified dominance on every answer metric.

**Interpretation:** graph-conditioned proposal plus reranking improves the chance that dispersed supporting chunks enter a small context.

**Qualification:** relative percentages obscure absolute error rates, and one fixed generator may react differently to context order or wording than other models.

### 7.2 Resource results

The paper reports dramatically lower graph construction cost than generative GraphRAG alternatives and smaller prompts. Online advantages grow on the more complex datasets, consistent with the claim that compact context reduces generation cost. NaiveRAG remains the offline-cost floor and is slightly faster online on HotpotQA.

**Interpretation:** the architecture trades a modest deterministic graph cost for potentially lower prompt cost and better multi-hop recall.

**Qualification:** index reuse frequency determines whether offline cost matters. A static corpus amortizes indexing; a frequently changing corpus may make rebuild behavior central.

### 7.3 Generalization or robustness

Robustness is tested mainly through three datasets and parameter sweeps. No multilingual, noisy OCR, temporal-update, adversarial-poisoning, or very-large-corpus study is reported. Generalization beyond multi-hop encyclopedic QA is promising but not established.

## 8. Ablations and causal evidence

### 8.1 PPR, lexical weighting, and reranking

Removing BM25 causes only a modest decline, suggesting that graph expansion already carries much of the retrieval signal. Replacing PPR with degree centrality causes a large decline, supporting query-conditioned propagation over raw token popularity. Removing dense reranking also significantly harms retrieval, showing that graph expansion alone admits noise. Together these results support the proposal–corrector explanation more strongly than a claim that the co-occurrence graph alone is sufficient.

### 8.2 Search-budget controls

Five final chunks give the best reported EM/F1 balance; more context adds distraction and less context omits evidence. Using entities from one leading chunk is usually best, with a 2Wiki R@5 exception at two chunks. Two retrieval hops outperform one and three across the study’s intended reasoning depth. A cumulative score threshold of 0.8 is best among tested values, favoring broad candidate coverage before dense filtering.

### 8.3 Randomness and statistical evidence

The reviewed source does not present independent training seeds because the pipeline is not trained, but inference can still vary through generation, serving, and possibly tie handling. Confidence intervals and named significance tests are not central to the reported tables. Repeated generation seeds, bootstrap intervals over questions, and corpus-resampling tests would make small differences—especially the 2Wiki exact-match gain—easier to interpret.

## 10. External views and field context

### 10.1 Predecessor evidence

The official Microsoft GraphRAG repository represents the contrasting LLM-assisted knowledge-extraction and community-summarization family.[^predecessor] HippoRAG provides the nearer network-retrieval precedent: PPR-like propagation is used to connect evidence, while TIGRAG changes the graph construction and later ranking stages.[^benchmark]

### 10.2 Independent evaluation perspective

The three datasets have official or author-maintained records describing multi-hop supporting-fact evaluation. Those records confirm the task definitions but do not independently reproduce TIGRAG. A decisive external evaluation would freeze corpus preprocessing, enforce equal retrieved-token budgets, and compare modern flat, late-interaction, graph, and reranking baselines under one generator.

### 10.3 Contemporaneous or later alternatives

RAPTOR and GraphRAG build more semantic hierarchical or graph abstractions at greater offline cost, while HippoRAG-style approaches use graph propagation over extracted concepts. TIGRAG occupies the low-construction-cost end of this design space. The comparison is a trade-off, not a total ordering: explicit relations can support queries for which token adjacency is weak, while co-occurrence may win where construction cost dominates.[^alternative]

### 10.4 Publication, code, and reproducibility status

The canonical public status located for this review is arXiv v1, submitted 29 June 2026.[^source-paper] Searches of the paper record and targeted GitHub queries did not verify an official TIGRAG repository. The complete PDF, HTML, and TeX archive were available locally and mutually consistent, but artifact availability is not result reproduction. The Nomic embedding model and Gemma model locator are public dependencies; their availability does not reproduce preprocessing, caches, baseline adapters, or tables.[^code]

## 11. Research notes and critical considerations

### 11.1 Internal consistency

The main narrative, table captions, and ablations align on the proposed mechanism. The paper appropriately notes the MuSiQue EM exception. The strongest terminology issue is “knowledge graph”: the data structure is a token association graph. Reframing it this way makes the evidence clearer and avoids implying relation semantics the method does not compute.

### 11.2 Metric and baseline concerns

Recall cannot be compared for baselines that return non-chunk objects. Answer metrics partly solve this but introduce generator dependence. Reported relative gains should be paired with absolute values and intervals in future work. Equal final chunk count is not necessarily equal token budget, and total online time mixes retrieval with generation. Baseline tuning, preprocessing, and cache reuse should be recorded per system.

### 11.3 Unsupported or overbroad claims

“State of the art,” “scalable,” and “real-world” should be restricted to the evaluated protocols. Nothing proves robustness to corpus poisoning, prompt injection in retrieved passages, changing documents, multilingual tokenization, or access-control filtering. The method reduces one kind of generative graph error but does not make retrieval or generation hallucination-free.

### 11.4 Alternative explanations

Gains may come from the full hybrid ensemble rather than token topology specifically: BM25, dense reranking, compact contexts, and iterative NER are all strong components. The PPR ablation argues against pure ensemble explanation, but a matched flat expansion baseline using the same candidate budget and reranker would isolate graph value more cleanly. Prompt-length reduction may also improve answer metrics by lowering distraction independently of multi-hop structure.

### 11.5 Unresolved questions

- How do graph size, memory, and query time scale with corpus vocabulary and update frequency?
- Which errors arise from missing graph paths versus candidate pruning, reranking, or generator evidence use?
- Does the approach survive adversarially common bridge tokens, boilerplate, OCR noise, and multilingual morphology?
- How sensitive are results to stop-word removal, lemmatization, window width, and edge-frequency thresholds?

## 12. Potential implications

### 12.1 Scientific implications

The paper suggests that useful retrieval structure need not be a fully semantic knowledge graph. A query-conditioned walk over cheap corpus statistics can supply a structural prior, provided later stages preserve provenance and correct noise. This shifts attention from graph expressiveness alone to the end-to-end division of labor between proposal and correction.

### 12.2 System-design implications

Teams with large static corpora and constrained graph-construction budgets could use token topology as a candidate generator. The system should expose each stage’s recall: seed coverage, PPR reach, candidate survival, reranker rank, and answer citation use. Incremental edge-count updates may be simpler than regenerating an LLM-built graph, although the paper does not test them.

### 12.3 Broader or cross-domain implications

The design may transfer to code, scientific text, or logs where local symbol co-occurrence is informative. It is a poor fit when the key relation is directional, rare, implicit, or intentionally contradicted. In those domains, typed edges or structured parsers may be worth their cost.

## 13. New hypotheses derived from the paper

These are reviewer inferences, not established findings.

### Hypothesis 1: Graph value concentrates in bridge-sparse queries

**Proposition:** TIGRAG’s advantage over matched dense retrieval grows when the original query has low embedding similarity to the second supporting passage but an intermediate passage contains a rare bridge token.  
**Motivating evidence:** the multi-hop gains and PPR ablation.  
**Predicted observation:** the delta correlates with original-query/second-passage similarity and inverse bridge-token frequency.  
**Falsifying observation:** gains remain unchanged after controlling for these variables or occur primarily on single-hop items.  
**Minimum test:** annotate hop structure, compute similarity and frequency strata, and report paired recall deltas with bootstrap intervals.

### Hypothesis 2: Dense reranking has a recoverable-noise threshold

**Proposition:** graph expansion helps only while the correct passage remains inside the reranker’s candidate capacity; beyond a graph-noise threshold, more expansion reduces recall after reranking.  
**Motivating evidence:** best performance at two hops, five chunks, and a 0.8 pool threshold.  
**Predicted observation:** recall first rises and then falls as expansion mass and hop count increase, with the turning point depending on reranker strength.  
**Falsifying observation:** a stronger reranker removes the decline even at very large pools.  
**Minimum test:** cross candidate-pool size with several rerankers and record proposal recall, final recall, latency, and tokens.

## 14. Deployment and governance considerations

### 14.1 Appropriate use

Appropriate use requires a corpus whose access permissions can be enforced before graph traversal, stable tokenization, inspectable provenance, and questions for which retrieved text is evidence rather than authority. It is suitable as a retrieval component, not an autonomous truth system.

### 14.2 Poor fit and failure modes

Poor fits include high-stakes domains without citation verification, corpora with frequent deletions, languages poorly served by the tokenizer/NER stack, relation-heavy queries, and adversarial corpora where repeated terms can manipulate graph centrality. Failure modes include missing seed tokens, hubs dominated by boilerplate, semantic drift across hops, stale edges, and a generator ignoring correct evidence.

### 14.3 Required safeguards

1. Enforce document-level authorization before token-to-chunk expansion and propagate deletion/update events into every index.
2. Return citations and validate answer claims against retrieved spans; abstain on weak or conflicting evidence.
3. Log graph seeds, expansion nodes, candidate scores, reranker order, context tokens, latency, and corpus/index versions for every decision.

## 15. Replication and falsification agenda

### 15.1 Highest-priority unresolved result

Reproduce Tables 2–5 under a matched token budget and shared hardware. Archive per-question retrieved IDs, scores, generated answers, runtime components, seeds, and exact baseline commits. Success means the direction of retrieval and resource results survives with intervals; failure means gains disappear after equal tuning and accounting.

### 15.2 Reproduce the full pipeline

Freeze corpus hashes; record chunk boundaries and overlaps; export normalized tokens, graph edges, and weights; preserve PPR parameters; archive token-to-chunk maps and embeddings; then rerun candidate scoring, pooling, reranking, hop expansion, and generation independently. Each stage should have a deterministic fixture.

### 15.3 Normalize evaluation

Compare equal retrieved-token budgets as well as equal chunk counts. Separate graph build, embedding, retrieval, reranking, prompt ingestion, and decoding time. Report CPU/GPU models, parallelism, warm-up, cache state, query batch size, and corpus-update amortization.

### 15.4 Test the proposed mechanism directly

Measure correct-passage proposal recall before BM25, after pooling, and after reranking. Stratify queries by bridge frequency, graph distance, entity-recognition success, and semantic similarity. This would locate where topology adds information rather than relying only on final QA gains.

### 15.5 Test realistic shifts and alternatives

Add multilingual corpora, OCR noise, conflicting documents, temporal updates, permission filters, and prompt-injection passages. Compare with matched flat lexical+dense fusion, late-interaction retrieval, relation-extraction graphs, and graphless iterative query expansion. Preserve all negative results.

---

## 16. Durable restatement

> TIGRAG demonstrates that a cheap token-association graph can be useful as a high-recall router for multi-hop retrieval when its noisy output is corrected by lexical evidence, adaptive pooling, and dense reranking. Its evidence is strongest as a benchmarked systems trade-off, not as proof that co-occurrence represents knowledge or that the method universally supersedes semantic graphs.

What should be remembered is the division of labor. Deterministic corpus statistics provide reach, provenance connects topology back to readable evidence, and a semantic corrector preserves precision. The resulting system can reduce graph-building cost and prompt footprint under the tested setup. Its next scientific test is not another aggregate leaderboard alone, but a stage-wise audit showing which bridge paths are recovered, which are lost, and how those outcomes change under realistic corpus scale and noise.
