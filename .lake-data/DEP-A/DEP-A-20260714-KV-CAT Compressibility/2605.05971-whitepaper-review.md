# KV-CAT Makes Compression Easier, but Its Theory and Evaluation Address Different Objects

## A full-paper review and independent re-conceptualization of “Training Transformers for KV Cache Compressibility”

**Paper:** Yoav Gelberg, Yam Eitan, Michael Bronstein, Yarin Gal, and Haggai Maron, arXiv:2605.05971v2.[^1]
**Scope:** complete PDF, full-paper HTML, source and appendices, all definitions, theorem/propositions and proofs, router implementation, training objective, nine tables, four figures, limitations, broader impact, and artifact vetting.
**Evidence boundary:** all performance and resource values are paper reports. No checkpoint was trained, compression optimization was rerun, or benchmark output was independently reproduced.

---

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

KV-Compression Aware Training (KV-CAT) continued-pretrains a transformer under learned KV masking so ordinary post-hoc compressors can preserve its behavior more easily, and the reported gains are broad, but the existential compressibility theorem does not explain the learned mechanism and the evaluation lacks artifact, uncertainty, and serving-cost evidence.

### Durable contribution

The durable shift is to treat compressibility as a property of the model representation, not only of a compressor applied afterward. Two models can compute the same function while exposing radically different internal state. Training under a memory bottleneck can encourage representations that tolerate later cache reduction.

KV-CAT operationalizes that idea without permanently changing inference architecture. A masked forward pass is distilled from a dense pass; a learned budgeted router chooses active KV slots during continued pretraining; a dense next-token loss preserves ordinary behavior. At evaluation, routers are removed and existing optimization-based compressors act on the resulting base-model-compatible cache.

### Bottom-line judgment

The empirical signal is meaningful. Dense multiple-choice averages remain almost unchanged: Qwen2.5-0.5B rises from 51.5 to 52.2, while 1.5B falls from 60.6 to 60.1. Under attention-matching prefix compression, KV-CAT reduces suffix distribution error at every primary setting. At 10% keep for the 1.5B model, reported delta perplexity falls from 1.027 to 0.320 and KL from 0.0796 to 0.0366. LongBench-v2 total accuracy rises from 22.2 to 30.3 at 10% keep and from 25.3 to 31.2 at 50%.

The results are not universal. Needle accuracy for the 0.5B model is worse at 5%, 10%, and 20% keep, then much better above 25%. At 40% on held-out arXiv completion, delta perplexity worsens from 2.002 to 2.842 even while KL and top-1 agreement improve. LongBench subdomains include losses on many-shot learning and table QA at some budgets. These exceptions are useful evidence that “compressibility” depends on compressor, metric, context, and task.

The theory establishes an important existence result: nearly any finite sequence-to-vector function can have both a one-slot-compressible transformer realization and a same-architecture realization for which no nontrivial compression uniformly works. But the compression policy is an arbitrary function that can synthesize KV pairs from the whole prefix, and the theorem is worst-case over all suffixes. KV-CAT instead trains ordinary Qwen models with token masking and evaluates gradient-based compression. No theorem links that objective to the existential construction or predicts the observed gains.

The cost is substantial: each checkpoint sees 5.24 billion tokens over 40,000 steps on eight H100 GPUs, with dense and masked passes. That can be worthwhile for a widely deployed model, but “training for compressibility” is an infrastructure investment, not a free compressor. No official code or released checkpoints were verified, which is the main practical blocker.[^2]

## 1. Problem formulation and novelty boundary

For each transformer layer, a compression policy maps `n` prefix key–value pairs to `r(n) <= n` pairs. The compressed model replaces only the prefix cache while processing a suffix normally. A transformer is `(N, epsilon, r)`-compressible if one policy keeps the output within `epsilon` of the full model for every prefix/suffix pair of combined length at most `N`.

This definition usefully separates model, policy, budget, and error. It is also stronger and more permissive than practical cache compression in different ways. Uniformity over every suffix is much stronger than an average language-model distribution. Conversely, the policy can be an arbitrary function and can create synthetic keys and values; it is not restricted to pruning, merging, quantization, runtime, or storage constraints. Compressibility under this definition does not imply an efficient compressor can be learned.

KV-CAT is not itself an inference-time compression algorithm. It changes model parameters by continued pretraining, then relies on attention matching or gradient-based compact-KV optimization. It is also not the first training-time sparsification or attention masking method. Its novelty is explicitly optimizing a standard transformer so downstream post-hoc KV optimization reaches better quality or converges faster.

## 2. Theory reconstruction

### 2.1 Same function, different cache geometry

Theorem 3.1 considers a finite alphabet and a bounded-length sequence-to-vector function that depends nontrivially on a suffix. For any positive error constants, it asserts two same-architecture transformer implementations: one approximates the function and is compressible to one KV pair; another approximates it but cannot tolerate any strict prefix-length reduction within a constant error.

The conclusion is existential and architecture-level. It does not say typical training produces either construction, that one has comparable parameter norm or optimization difficulty, or that language modeling resembles the proof’s finite lookup construction. “Almost any function” in the prose is conditional on the theorem’s suffix-dependence premise and bounded domain.

### 2.2 Histogram example

The paper builds intuition with a character histogram. A two-layer transformer can average one-hot token values with uniform attention. Surprisingly, simply replacing `n` prefix items by `r<n` synthesized values changes the attention denominator when suffix tokens arrive. One fixed prefix summary cannot correct the suffix-dependent normalization for every suffix, yielding a lower bound.

A modified implementation becomes one-slot compressible by preserving prefix length and an unnormalized histogram in auxiliary value dimensions. Positional information lets a final feed-forward network correct the mixed prefix/suffix denominator.

This is an excellent mechanistic example: the computed function is aggregative, but the naive internal representation is not compositional under continuation. Compressibility requires sufficient statistics plus bookkeeping about how they combine with future tokens.

It also exposes the gap to KV-CAT. Learned token dropping does not explicitly store counts, normalization factors, or sufficient statistics. The experiment does not probe whether trained representations acquire such algebraic structure. Linear decodability of prefix length, merge associativity, and summary compositionality would be stronger mechanism tests.

### 2.3 Limits of the theorem

The one-pair policy in the proof knows the whole prefix and can encode synthetic state. Practical gradient-based compressors may require many optimization steps per prompt and may not find the existential pair. The incompressible construction is adversarial and depends on uniform suffix guarantees; high expected-case performance can coexist with that lower bound.

Therefore the theorem supports the *possibility* that training matters, not the efficacy of KV-CAT. The empirical section must stand on its own.

## 3. Technical reconstruction of the KV-CAT pipeline

### 3.1 Learned masking

Lightweight routers read token representations before selected layer groups and emit scores in `[0,1]`. Thresholding creates binary attention masks. Routers start with all tokens active and are shared across groups to limit overhead. The main checkpoints route at layers 0/6/12/18 for 0.5B and 0/7/14/21 for 1.5B, with 64-dimensional router features, threshold 0.5, and target retention 50%.

Because a router score is based on the current token representation, the mask is context dependent. Yet the paper does not report router calibration, token survival by category, overlap with test-time optimized caches, or behavior when the realized keep ratio deviates from 50%. The threshold operation also requires a gradient estimator; exact estimator and stability details should be prominent, not implementation-only.

### 3.2 Three losses

The total objective is

`L = lambda_mask L_mask + lambda_budget L_budget + lambda_anchor L_anchor`.

`L_mask` is forward KL from a stop-gradient dense distribution to the masked distribution. This self-distillation teaches the bottlenecked path to mimic the current model. `L_anchor` is ordinary dense next-token cross-entropy, keeping the teacher useful and reducing drift. `L_budget` uses average binary activity `F`, average router score `G`, and target `rho`; its scaled quadratic-like form is minimized near the target when scores and masks agree.

The main weights are 1, 0.1, and 1. Dense and masked passes share changing parameters, so the teacher is not fixed. That saves a third forward pass but makes the target nonstationary. The dense anchor partly controls collapse; Table 1 suggests success on six multiple-choice tasks, though a broader dense language-model evaluation is absent.

### 3.3 Training and inference

Both Qwen2.5 checkpoints are continued-pretrained on streaming FineWeb-Edu at length 1,024. AdamW uses peak learning rate `1e-4`, minimum `5e-6`, 600 warmup steps, 0.01 weight decay, gradient norm 1, and 131,072 tokens per optimizer step. Forty thousand steps equal 5.24B tokens on eight H100s.

At inference, KV-CAT uses the ordinary dense forward pass and does not use its router masks directly. A separate compressor optimizes or selects a prefix cache. This clean compatibility is valuable, but it means training retention (50%), test retention (5–50%), and compression mechanism differ. The learned benefit transfers across those differences empirically; the paper does not establish why.

### 3.4 Runtime and memory semantics

The paper distinguishes three caches that should not be conflated. During continued pretraining, the dense path constructs a full causal cache and the masked path applies router decisions inside attention; this increases training work beyond an ordinary next-token pass. During evaluation, the model first produces dense prefix keys and values. Attention matching or gradient optimization then constructs a smaller synthetic prefix cache, after which suffix decoding attends to that compact state plus newly generated suffix state. The reported keep ratio describes the compact prefix slots only.[^4]

This distinction matters for claims about memory. Optimization needs the original cache, gradients, and compact variables before the original can be released. Peak preparation memory can therefore exceed steady-state compact-cache memory. If the same prompt serves many suffixes, preparation can be amortized; for one-shot prompts, it may dominate. The paper measures optimization steps but does not provide peak bytes, time to first token, break-even output length, or number of reused generations.

Distribution metrics are likewise conditional. Delta perplexity and KL compare each checkpoint’s compressed-prefix suffix distribution with that checkpoint’s own dense-prefix distribution.[^5] A lower delta says compression perturbs KV-CAT less; it does not prove KV-CAT has lower absolute perplexity or better calibrated probabilities than the base. The dense multiple-choice table partially checks capability preservation, but absolute validation loss and generative quality are needed.

Finally, binary token masks at shared layer boundaries do not necessarily correspond to a physically smaller production cache at every layer. An implementation must define whether dropped slots are compacted, merely masked, or retained for later layer groups, and whether selection differs by head. The training objective can encourage representational redundancy even when its router layout is not itself the deployment layout. This is consistent with the paper’s design, but makes end-to-end memory claims dependent on the downstream compressor rather than KV-CAT alone.

## 4. Experimental design

Dense retention uses 1,000 examples each from HellaSwag, WinoGrande, PIQA, OpenBookQA, ARC-Easy, and ARC-Challenge. Prefix-completion experiments use 768-token prefixes and 256-token suffixes, with attention matching and gradient-based cache optimization. Needle retrieval uses 100 examples per keep ratio. Long-form QA uses seven LongBench-v2 subdomains.

The breadth is a strength: output-distribution fidelity, exact retrieval, and answer accuracy test different failure modes. Two model sizes reveal some scaling behavior. Cross-domain completion on arXiv, PG-19, and WikiText-103 tests transfer beyond FineWeb-Edu.

Important details remain missing or underemphasized: number of completion examples, seed counts and intervals, exact compressor steps and initialization, generation parameters, LongBench question counts per subdomain, checkpoint selection, hardware for compression timing, wall-clock training cost, and whether hyperparameters were tuned on reported tasks. The “up to 5x” convergence claim is curve-based rather than a standardized target-quality latency table.

## 5. Results

### 5.1 Dense performance

The 0.5B average changes from 51.5 to 52.2; individual changes range from -1.0 HellaSwag to +2.8 ARC-Easy. The 1.5B average changes 60.6 to 60.1, including -2.2 HellaSwag and -1.7 ARC-Challenge. This supports approximate preservation, not exact parity. No significance estimates accompany the 1,000-example scores.

Router training beats random and attention masks on dense averages: 52.2 versus 50.7 and 50.2 for the 0.5B model. This is evidence that mask policy affects retained base capability, not just compression.

### 5.2 Suffix fidelity

For 0.5B at 5/10/20/40% keep, KV-CAT delta perplexity is 0.461/0.422/0.438/0.360 versus base 0.780/0.662/0.777/0.592. Top-1 agreement improves by roughly two points. For 1.5B the reductions are larger, especially 1.027 to 0.320 at 10% and 1.093 to 0.536 at 20%.

Cross-domain results are mostly favorable. WikiText-103 at 10% improves delta perplexity 6.568 to 1.242 and top-1 84.8% to 91.5%. But arXiv at 40% worsens delta perplexity while KL improves. Perplexity is sensitive to a few token probabilities; the disagreement should be analyzed rather than hidden by averages.

### 5.3 Retrieval and QA

Needle mean accuracy improves from 19.6 to 26.0 for 0.5B and 31.7 to 36.9 for 1.5B. The curve is nonmonotonic and several low-budget 0.5B cells favor the base. Only 100 examples per ratio makes differences of one to three cases weak evidence.

LongBench-v2 totals favor KV-CAT at 10/20/50%: 30.3/30.8/31.2 versus 22.2/22.2/25.3. Legal gains are especially large; many-shot learning is worse at 10% and 50%, and table QA is worse at 50%. Macro totals should be accompanied by task sizes and uncertainty.

### 5.4 Optimization speed

The convergence curves show KV-CAT reaches comparable delta perplexity in fewer gradient steps, up to a claimed 5x. A step reduction is operationally valuable because per-prompt optimization can be expensive. Real latency also depends on forward/backward kernels, cache parameterization, stopping rule, and initialization. Those are not reported as end-to-end seconds, energy, or throughput.

## 6. Ablations

Router, random, and attention-based train-time policies are compared. Router training generally produces the best dense and compressed results. At 40% attention matching, router delta perplexity 0.360 is essentially tied with random 0.361, while other ratios separate more. This suggests adaptive masking is helpful but not solely responsible; exposure to any bottleneck contributes.

Missing ablations include loss removal, budget weights, target keep rate, router depth/features, layer grouping, teacher stop-gradient, training token count, and whether the router itself can be reused at inference. Without them, the 5.24B-token recipe is difficult to optimize economically.

## 10. Independent re-conceptualization

KV-CAT can be viewed as training a *mergeable state machine*. A good long-context representation should expose approximate sufficient statistics that remain valid when prefix state is shortened and later tokens arrive. Random or learned masking creates interventions that reward this compositionality.

A stronger objective would train explicit merge operations. Split a prefix into chunks, encode them separately, merge their compressed states, and require agreement with dense continuation across multiple suffixes. This would target the denominator/bookkeeping issue illustrated by the histogram proof more directly than token deletion.

The learned router could also amortize test-time optimization. Its mask or hidden features could initialize compact-KV variables, reducing per-prompt steps. Jointly measuring router overlap with the optimized cache would reveal whether KV-CAT learns importance or merely redundancy tolerance.

Finally, compressibility should be a vector, not a scalar: quality at a budget, optimizer steps, synthetic-state bits, transfer bytes, layer distribution, and worst-case failures. A model may be pruning-friendly but quantization-hostile, or preserve KL while harming exact retrieval.

## 11. Research notes, limitations, implications, and governance applicability

The approach is most attractive for model providers who can amortize continued pretraining across many deployments. It is less accessible to downstream users without weights or large compute. Scaling from 0.5B/1.5B to modern large models may change router overhead and training stability.

Training data and cache states can contain private long-context material. Compressibility training should not be assumed to erase it; synthetic KV states may retain or expose source information. Privacy audits should test reconstruction and membership leakage.

Mask training can also alter which evidence survives compression. Safety-critical facts, system instructions, negations, and minority-language tokens need stratified retention tests. Average QA cannot establish governance readiness.

## 12. Replication and falsification agenda

1. Release code, both checkpoints, routers, exact data stream/revisions, and raw predictions.
2. Reproduce dense and compressed results with three seeds and paired confidence intervals.
3. Report wall-clock compression time, GPU memory, energy, and end-to-end decode throughput.
4. Ablate each loss, target retention, threshold, router grouping, and training-token budget.
5. Probe whether prefix length, counts, entities, and sufficient statistics become more linearly decodable.
6. Compare pruning, merging, quantization, low-rank, and synthetic-KV compressors under equal resources.
7. Expand model sizes and context lengths; include batch/concurrency serving tests.
8. Investigate every cell where KV-CAT loses, particularly arXiv 40% and low-budget 0.5B needles.
9. Audit privacy leakage and instruction/negation retention.

### Falsifiable hypotheses

- If KV-CAT learns compositional state, chunk-merge probes will agree more closely with dense continuation than the base model.
- If benefits are compressor agnostic, they will persist for quantization and low-rank methods not used in evaluation.
- If the router learns useful importance, its mask will initialize gradient compression better than random masks at equal keep rate.
- If gains scale, a larger checkpoint will preserve its dense capability and reduce fixed-quality compression latency after equal-token training.
- If the method is robust, low-budget exact retrieval losses will disappear across larger paired samples rather than average out.

## 13. Durable restatement

The paper correctly relocates part of the KV-cache problem upstream: representations can be trained to tolerate compression. KV-CAT supplies broad reported evidence that learned masking plus dense self-distillation improves later compact-cache optimization without materially changing ordinary inference.

The defensible claim is narrower than “compressible transformers” in general. The theorem proves existence under an arbitrary synthetic policy and worst-case definition; the experiments study two small continued-pretrained Qwen models and optimization-based compressors. The bridge is empirical, not theoretical. Public artifacts, uncertainty, larger-scale models, and complete serving economics are needed before the idea becomes an engineering prescription.
