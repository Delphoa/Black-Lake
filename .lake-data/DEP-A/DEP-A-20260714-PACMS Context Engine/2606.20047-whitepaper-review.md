# PACMS Is a Sensible Context Selector with an Undersized QA Study

## A full-paper review and independent re-conceptualization of “PACMS: Submodular Context Selection as a Pluggable Engine for LLM Agents”

**Paper:** Manu Ghulyani, Arunabh Singh, Karan Bharadwaj, Ankit Nath, and Suranjan Goswami, arXiv:2606.20047v1.[^1]
**Scope:** complete PDF, HTML and source, facility-location objective, CELF integration, OpenClaw demo, two tables, three figures, appendix session record, LongMemEval protocol, baselines, limitations, and public artifact search.
**Evidence boundary:** recall, accuracy, and demo behavior are paper reports. No selector service, plugin, OpenAI model call, or LongMemEval run was reproduced.

---

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

PACMS treats an agent’s pooled turns, memories, and tool outputs as a token-costed facility-location problem and reports better QA than relevance, recency, or LangChain MMR at one operating point, but the study has only 100 questions, few-question margins, synthetic redundancy, no uncertainty, and no verified public implementation.

### Durable contribution

The practical contribution is to put context policy at the final assembly boundary. Rather than separately retrieve memory, truncate conversation, and append tool outputs, PACMS gives every candidate a common embedding, cost, and selection decision under one token budget. The agent runtime can swap PACMS, top-k, recency, RAG, or MMR without changing upstream components.

Facility location is a good objective for this job. It rewards a selected item when it represents many query-relevant candidates, then exhibits diminishing returns once that region is covered. Repetitive tool output or paraphrases should be suppressed without requiring pairwise penalties against only the selected set.

### Bottom-line judgment

Evidence-round recall is nuanced. At a 20% budget, pure top-k beats PACMS for every redundancy level: 0.790 versus 0.704 with no injected paraphrases and 0.817 versus 0.804 at `R=8`. At 45%, PACMS overtakes top-k only as redundancy grows: 0.952 versus 0.938 at `R=4`, 0.972 versus 0.940 at `R=8`. LangChain MMR is slightly higher than PACMS at those high-redundancy points. At 70%, PACMS and MMR saturate near one.

The paper’s central claim comes from QA at `R=2`, 45% budget. PACMS scores 52% with GPT-5-mini and 68% with GPT-5.4-mini, versus top-k 50/64, MMR 44/56, and recency 44/42. PACMS beats MMR by 8–12 points despite similar evidence recall.

With 100 questions, however, the top-k margin is only two and four answers. No paired significance test, confidence interval, repeated API call, error taxonomy, or per-question result is provided. MMR’s larger gap could reflect prompt extractability, selector quality, its untuned default lambda, or stochastic/judge effects. The experiment supports a hypothesis, not a general superiority claim.

The submodular mathematics is standard and relevant, but the approximation statement needs exact algorithmic conditions. Monotone facility location under a knapsack constraint admits constant-factor algorithms; simple cost-benefit greedy/CELF alone does not automatically inherit the cardinality `1-1/e` guarantee without safeguards such as best-singleton comparison or partial enumeration. The paper says “cost-aware greedy with a constant-factor guarantee” but does not specify the theorem, factor, mandatory-set preprocessing, infeasible-item handling, or exact pseudocode.

The demonstration is useful system evidence: a local FastAPI selector, cached Ollama embeddings, OpenClaw hook, fail-soft recency fallback, debug UI, and a captured 168-candidate session. Yet one scripted session is not production validation. The paper says a `pacms-context-engine` plugin v0.1.0 is published, but no canonical repository/package URL was verified in the source or search.[^2]

## 1. Problem framing and novelty boundary

Long-running agents accumulate heterogeneous context: user/assistant turns, persistent memories, and large tool outputs. Recency truncation is query blind. External RAG fetches documents but does not arbitrate already-present pooled context. Summarization rewrites content and can lose details.

PACMS selects original candidate units rather than rewriting them. It is query-aware, token-budgeted, and redundancy-sensitive. It does not solve candidate generation, memory writing, semantic chunking, summarization, or reader reasoning.

Facility-location selection, CELF lazy greedy, MMR, and context-engine hooks are inherited. The novelty is their integration as a pluggable agent assembly engine and evaluation on long-memory conversational histories.

## 2. Technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Candidate pool and relevance

Let `C={c_1,...,c_n}`, latest query `q`, budget `B`, and mandatory set `M`. Each candidate and query has embedding `e`. Relevance is clipped cosine:

`rel(i,q)=max(0,cos(e_i,e_q))`.

Clipping ignores negative similarity rather than treating it as evidence of conflict. Cosine scale and embedding model determine calibration. Candidates can vary from ten-token turns to 5,000-token tool outputs, but one vector represents each unit. Long, multi-topic outputs can be poorly summarized by a single embedding and are indivisible under selection.

The mandatory set is sensible for system instructions or current query. The method must ensure `sum_(m in M) tok(m)<=B`; otherwise the constraint is infeasible. It should also reserve output/system tokens and define tokenizer consistency with the reader.

### 2.2 Coverage weights and objective

Coverage weight from selected item `j` to target item `i` is

`w_ij = rel(i,q) * max(0,cos(e_i,e_j))`.

Objective is

`F(S)=sum_i max_(j in S) w_ij`, subject to token cost and `M subset S`.

Each target candidate contributes according to query relevance and similarity to its best selected representative. Self-coverage gives a selected candidate at least its own relevance. Once one selected item covers a cluster, near-duplicates add little, yielding submodular diminishing returns.

The objective covers *candidate mass*, not facts. Many redundant paraphrases increase the total relevance weight of their region and can make it more valuable, even though within-cluster marginal returns diminish. Template injection therefore changes both redundancy and cluster mass. Normalizing cluster contribution or using capped density could prevent repetition from increasing global priority.

Facility location also has no explicit recency, source authority, instruction priority, contradiction, or provenance term. Mandatory rules can protect critical candidates, but broad mandatory sets consume budget.

### 2.3 Greedy optimization

PACMS uses CELF lazy evaluation to avoid recomputing all marginal gains. For each candidate, marginal gain is improvement in `F` when added; a cost-aware policy presumably compares marginal gain per token. Cached upper bounds accelerate repeated selection inside one call.

Classic CELF preserves the selection order of ordinary greedy for monotone submodular objectives under a cardinality constraint. Under variable token costs/knapsack, guarantee depends on the specific variant. The manuscript should state pseudocode, comparison with best singleton, handling of mandatory items, candidates exceeding remaining budget, tie-breaking, and claimed approximation factor.

Worst-case similarity construction is `O(n^2)` in time/memory. Embedding cache reduces encoding work across turns but not pairwise graph growth. The paper reports no selection latency, pool-size scaling, cache memory, or approximation/pruning strategy for thousands of messages.

### 2.4 Runtime integration

OpenClaw exposes `ingest`, `assemble`, and `compact`. PACMS implements assembly and sets `ownsCompaction=false`, so runtime summarization remains separate. A TypeScript/plugin-side call posts candidates, query, and budget to local FastAPI at loopback. One persistent selector caches embeddings from local Ollama. `/select` returns kept indices; `/select_debug` exposes scores. If unavailable, the plugin falls back to recency.

This failure design avoids blocking the agent but changes semantics silently. Production should log fallback, protect mandatory items locally, enforce timeouts, authenticate even loopback if multiple users exist, and version embeddings. Debug endpoints can expose sensitive conversation content or scores.

The shared selector class dispatches PACMS, top-k, last-k, RAG, and MMR over common embeddings/token estimates, reducing implementation confounds. LangChain MMR is nevertheless described with default lambda 0.5, while PACMS is designed specifically for the task. Baseline tuning is unequal.

### 2.5 Demonstration

The live walkthrough interleaves topics and tools, re-asks an old topic, and visualizes keep/drop decisions as budget changes. The appendix shows a real workspace trace where Rust availability was discussed; PACMS selected 17 of 168 candidates.

The screenshot confirms integration and observability, not correctness. A single old fact returning is expected from query similarity. Latency, answer accuracy, privacy behavior, prompt injection, and failure fallback are not measured in the demo.

## 3. Experimental design

The study samples 100 LongMemEval questions uniformly across six types. Each has multi-session chat and annotated evidence rounds. The same sample supports recall and QA. The paper cites compute constraints: about 1,600 API calls and 30–60k input tokens per call.

Selectors use `nomic-embed-text` via local Ollama and token budgets as a fraction of each question’s total context. Baselines are cosine top-k, LangChain MMR ranked then budget-walked, and recency last-k. Exact model revision, embedding dimensionality, Ollama version, tokenizer, random sample seed/IDs, and candidate segmentation are not fully supplied.

Evidence recall counts annotated rounds retained. It ignores distractor amount, ordering, token position, partial tool outputs, and whether evidence is extractable. QA is therefore necessary, but only one budget/redundancy point is tested.

The redundancy stress test injects template paraphrases of filler rounds at `R=0,2,4,8`. Placement matters greatly for recency: appending duplicates near the end will mechanically push old evidence out and explains last-k collapse. Injection order, token lengths, and semantic similarity should be released. Template variation is controlled but not representative of real tool repetition or user paraphrase.

QA readers are GPT-5-mini with minimal reasoning and GPT-5.4-mini with low reasoning; GPT-4o-mini judges using the official LongMemEval prompt. Hosted model aliases are mutable. No API snapshots, full responses, judge disagreements, human audit, or repeated-call variance are provided.

A full-context comparator is omitted because histories can reach 750k characters while reader input is truncated at 300k. This means absolute accuracy has no in-study ceiling. Published LongMemEval full-context numbers use different pipelines and are not directly comparable.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Tight budget

At 20%, top-k is strongest until `R=8`, where MMR edges it 0.836 to 0.817 and PACMS is 0.804. PACMS is consistently below top-k by 1.3–8.6 points. Facility coverage spends scarce capacity representing regions; when only a few candidates fit, direct relevance is rationally stronger.

MMR at default lambda performs poorly at low redundancy (0.421) and improves as duplicates are added. The paper itself flags likely over-diversification. A tuned lambda curve is required before concluding PACMS’s objective is better.

### 4.2 Medium budget

At 45%, top-k leads at `R=0` (0.928 versus PACMS 0.860) and `R=2` (0.933 versus 0.909). At `R=4`, MMR/PACMS reach 0.966/0.952 versus top-k 0.938; at `R=8`, 0.994/0.972 versus 0.940. Coverage/diversity helps only when sufficient budget and redundancy coexist.

### 4.3 High budget

At 70%, all relevance-aware methods are strong. PACMS reaches 0.991/0.999/1.000 for `R=2/4/8`; MMR 0.993/0.999/1.000. This is parity, not a PACMS recall advantage. Recency declines sharply with injected redundancy.

### 4.4 QA

PACMS leads both readers. The stronger reader improves PACMS by 16 points, top-k 14, MMR 12, while recency falls two. Better selected context can indeed be more usable than slightly higher evidence-round recall.

With 100 binary questions, the 52 versus 50 and 68 versus 64 top-k margins are small. A paired McNemar or bootstrap interval and question-level contingency table are essential. The larger PACMS–MMR gap is more notable but baseline tuning remains a confound.

The claim that facility location makes prompts more “extractable” is plausible but unmeasured. Evidence order, redundancy, number of kept items, token density, and lost evidence type should be analyzed.

## 5. Ablations and missing controls

Budget and redundancy sweeps act as mechanism probes for recall. Two readers probe capability interaction. The shared implementation helps fairness.

Missing are objective-term ablations, relevance clipping, candidate granularity, embedding models, MMR lambda, cost-aware versus cardinality greedy, mandatory items, ordering of selected context, pool size/latency, real redundancy, summarization hybrid, and multiple samples/seeds. A single QA operating point cannot establish robustness.

### 5.1 Approximation and feasibility audit

Mandatory items can be handled by initializing `S=M`, subtracting their token cost, and computing marginal gain relative to them. If they exceed `B`, the API needs an explicit error or a priority policy; silently dropping system instructions is unsafe. Candidates individually larger than the remaining budget should be skipped or chunked. Neither case is specified.

For a cardinality constraint, greedy submodular maximization achieves `1-1/e`. For a knapsack constraint, marginal-gain-per-cost greedy alone can fail badly when one high-value expensive item competes with many low-cost items. Comparing against the best single feasible candidate restores a constant guarantee in common variants, while stronger guarantees use enumeration/continuous methods. PACMS should cite the exact implemented rule and theorem rather than transferring a generic guarantee.

CELF is an optimization, not a different selector: it lazily reuses upper bounds because submodular marginal gains only fall as `S` grows. Correctness requires recomputing stale candidates until the top bound is current. Unit tests on exhaustive small pools should verify the implementation.

### 5.2 Prompt assembly semantics

The objective returns a set, but transformer prompts require an order. Chronological ordering preserves dialogue causality; score ordering moves evidence closer to the query; source grouping can improve coherence. The paper does not specify assembly order or delimiters, yet the “extractability” claim can depend heavily on them.

Token budget is computed on candidates before separators, role labels, system messages, and serialization. A safe assembler must reserve exact overhead with the reader’s tokenizer. Fractional budget relative to source context is useful for experiments but production budgets are absolute and include output reservation.

Selection preserves original text, avoiding summarization hallucination, but can remove the turn that explains a pronoun, correction, or tool result. Candidate-level embeddings do not model discourse dependencies. Mandatory adjacency or graph constraints could keep parents/children together.

### 5.3 Statistical power and reader audit

At 100 questions, binomial standard error around 50% is about five percentage points before paired structure. The two-point PACMS–top-k difference under GPT-5-mini is plainly within ordinary sampling noise unless errors are exceptionally anti-correlated. Even the four-point stronger-reader difference needs paired analysis.[^5]

The same GPT-4o-mini judge evaluates outputs from both reader families. Automatic judges can favor style, exactness, or model-family conventions. Human audit of disagreements, judge-repeat stability, and official answer normalization are necessary. Hosted aliases may change after publication, so raw responses are the only durable evidence.

The stronger reader improves most selectors but not recency. This supports the claim that missing evidence caps reader capability, yet it could also reflect stochastic variation or model prompt sensitivity. Per-question transitions—wrong to correct and correct to wrong—would reveal the mechanism.

### 5.4 Systems and security audit

Pairwise similarity requires `n^2` scores. For 168 candidates this is trivial; for 10,000 tool chunks it is 100 million. Approximate neighbor graphs, clustering, or streaming submodular methods may be required. Embedding cache warmth helps encoding but introduces memory growth and invalidation complexity.

The local service boundary broadens attack surface. An untrusted webpage or process could call a debug endpoint if origin/network controls are weak. Candidate text, queries, embeddings, and keep/drop decisions can be sensitive. Authentication, request limits, tenant keys, content redaction, encrypted storage, and deletion are required.

Fail-soft recency is operationally sensible but should be conspicuous. A timeout or service crash could silently change answers and discard old critical facts. The fallback must still preserve mandatory items and emit telemetry. Plugin and model versions should be part of every trace.[^4]

## 9. Independent re-conceptualization

PACMS should be reframed as *budgeted evidence coverage with policy constraints*. Candidate similarity is only a proxy for shared facts. A fact extractor or lightweight entailment model could define coverage edges, while provenance and contradiction edges prevent one representative from standing in for incompatible statements.

Long tool outputs should be hierarchically selectable: first choose the output, then choose passages within it. This preserves the facility objective while avoiding all-or-nothing 5,000-token units.

A hybrid policy could select originals for exact evidence and summaries for covered background. Marginal gain would include answer utility, authority, freshness, and prompt-injection risk per token.

Finally, selection confidence should control fallback. If embeddings are flat or the query is ambiguous, allocate a safety margin, include recent/mandatory context, or ask for clarification rather than trusting one ranking.

## 10. Research notes, limitations, implications, and governance applicability

PACMS is most useful for agents with heterogeneous, redundant pools and moderate budgets. Top-k is a better default under very tight budgets in the reported data. Recency remains a useful fail-safe but not the primary relevance policy.

Context selection is security-sensitive. Embedding similarity can be manipulated by prompt-injected tool output; debug APIs can leak private context; cached embeddings need tenant isolation and deletion. Mandatory system instructions must never depend solely on semantic selection.

Auditable keep/drop logs are valuable, but should store hashes/IDs and policy versions rather than raw sensitive text where possible. Model/provider changes can shift selection and must be versioned.

## 11. Replication and falsification agenda

1. Release the plugin/service, exact version, license, configs, and demo trace.
2. Publish LongMemEval sample IDs, injection generator/placement, prompts, responses, judgments, and selectors.
3. Repeat across samples/seeds and use paired McNemar/bootstrap intervals.
4. Tune MMR lambda and compare stronger retrieval/diversification baselines.
5. Sweep QA across all budgets/redundancy levels and include an attainable ceiling.
6. Report selection latency, pairwise memory, cache hit rate, and scaling with pool size.
7. Ablate objective weights, clipping, ordering, granularity, embeddings, and mandatory rules.
8. Test real tool redundancy, prompt injection, contradictions, multilingual sessions, and privacy deletion.

### Falsifiable hypotheses

- If facility coverage improves extractability, it will reduce distractor tokens per retained evidence fact versus top-k at matched recall.
- If QA gains are robust, paired intervals across larger samples will exclude top-k and tuned MMR.
- If redundancy drives the benefit, PACMS advantage will grow with real semantic duplication but vanish on nonredundant pools.
- If the greedy guarantee is implemented correctly, returned value will match the specified bound on exhaustive small cases.
- If embedding cache makes the hot path practical, p95 selection latency will remain acceptable as pools grow beyond 1,000 candidates.

## 12. Durable restatement

PACMS correctly treats final prompt assembly as an explicit optimization problem over all agent context, not an accidental side effect of recency. Query-weighted facility location is interpretable, redundancy-aware, and compatible with a pluggable runtime.

The current evidence is a solid prototype study, not proof of superior context management. Recall favors simpler top-k under tight/low-redundancy conditions; the QA win is based on 100 questions and tiny margins over top-k; MMR is untuned; algorithmic guarantees and public artifacts are underspecified. A released, statistically powered, security-aware evaluation could turn the design into a durable agent component.
