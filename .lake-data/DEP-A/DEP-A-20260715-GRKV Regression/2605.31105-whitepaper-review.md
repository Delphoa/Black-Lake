# Global Attention Reconstruction After KV Eviction

## A detailed review and independent re-conceptualization of “GRKV: Global Regression for Training-Free KV Cache Compression in Long-Context LLMs”

**Source paper:** Junjie Peng, You Wu, Haoyi Wu, Jialong Han, Xiaohua Xie, Kewei Tu, Jianhuang Lai, “GRKV: Global Regression for Training-Free KV Cache Compression in Long-Context LLMs,” arXiv:2605.31105v1.[^source-paper]

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

GRKV replaces local post-eviction token merging with alternating ridge-regression updates that use all retained tokens as carriers and minimize the discrepancy between full-cache and compressed-cache attention outputs on a surrogate query window.

### The deeper idea

The paper identifies a compatibility failure between two generations of cache methods. Modern eviction increasingly preserves contiguous spans, but older local merge rules were designed around dispersed retained tokens. Span retention moves the surviving carriers into islands; local matching then funnels many evicted tokens into a few boundary states and blurs them. GRKV changes the objective from local key resemblance to global functional reconstruction: the compressed cache should reproduce the attention output that the full cache would have produced.

### Bottom-line judgment

The evidence is unusually well aligned with the mechanism. Merge maps show carrier concentration, a first-order appendix explains why limited carrier support reduces reconstruction capacity, global ridge updates improve both keys and values, and matched experiments show consistent gains across 16 LongBench and 13 RULER tasks for multiple eviction backbones and models. The absolute average gains are modest, and the method uses full-cache information during prefill optimization, but its claim is appropriately bounded: a plug-in merging step that recovers some evicted information with moderate overhead, not lossless compression.

The most defensible reading is therefore conditional. The paper supplies evidence about GRKV under its named models, datasets, budgets, and implementation. It does not convert those observations into a universal law about compression or adaptation. A deployment decision should preserve the exact experimental coordinates—model revision, context length, hardware, baseline implementation, retained capacity, calibration data, and metric definition—because changing any one of them can change which design wins.

### Principal strengths

- The method optimizes an attention-output objective rather than relying only on local token similarity.
- Experiments cover two main backbones, two benchmark suites, multiple eviction backbones, 10% and 20% budgets, and systems measurements.
- Regularization, fixed-token ratio, update steps, surrogate-window size, key/value updates, and compatibility are separately ablated.
- Negative comparisons show that prior merging methods can degrade span-based eviction, clarifying why the proposed intervention is needed.

### Principal qualifications

1. The evaluation centers on English long-context and synthetic retrieval benchmarks and three open model families.
2. The regression target is estimated from a surrogate query window; future queries may require information that this window does not expose.
3. A global closed-form or dual update still adds prefill work and can become expensive at larger windows, batches, or unsupported kernels.
4. Improvements are aggregate and modest enough that implementation variance, search choices, and task-level regressions remain important.

## 1. The problem the paper is actually solving

### 1.1 Problem, stakes, and operational setting

Eviction keeps a fixed number of KV entries and discards the rest. Merging tries to salvage discarded information by incorporating it into retained states. With span-based retention such as SnapKV, nearby retained positions are clustered inside spans. A local adjacency or key-similarity merge therefore assigns many evicted entries to the same span-boundary carriers. Those carriers must represent their original content plus many unrelated evicted states, producing over-merging. The paper seeks a training-free transformation that distributes recovery across the full retained set without increasing the cache budget.

The relevant resource is not merely a file size or nominal ratio. It is a constrained system state that affects memory residency, transfer volume, arithmetic intensity, batch capacity, latency, and downstream quality. A method can reduce one component while adding calibration, reconstruction, routing, metadata, or kernel overhead elsewhere. For that reason, this review keeps algorithmic compression, task quality, and realized systems efficiency separate throughout.

### 1.2 Prior method families

Eviction baselines include H2O, SnapKV, PyramidKV, CriticalKV, Ada-KV, DuoAttention, and HeadKV. Merge baselines include CaM, KVMerger, D2O, and AsymKV, which use local adjacency, key similarity, weighted averaging, or position-dependent choices. These local rules are plausible when retained tokens are dispersed but become capacity-limited when span-based selection leaves only a small active carrier neighborhood. GRKV instead treats all retained tokens as candidate carriers and fits them to the full-cache function.

These families encode different assumptions about where redundancy lives and what information is safe to share or discard. A fair comparison must therefore hold the true capacity constraint constant and disclose whether preprocessing, fine-tuning, calibration, index construction, task exemplars, or special kernels are included in the cost boundary.

### 1.3 Claimed gap and contribution

The paper argues that no earlier merging method directly solves a global functional objective designed for span-based retention. GRKV captures a stable query window from the end of the prompt, computes full-cache attention outputs, then alternates value and key updates for the retained cache. Ridge penalties keep the solution near the original retained states and reduce over-smoothing. A subset of high-attention, sink, and window tokens can be fixed to preserve important anchors.

The paper's contribution is best treated as a mechanism plus an evidence package. The mechanism proposes a new intervention; the evidence package attempts to show that the intervention, not a hidden increase in resources or an incomparable baseline, explains the measured result. The rest of this review reconstructs both parts.

## 2. Formal and technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Method overview

Let the full cache contain n key/value rows and the retained cache c rows after eviction. The last m queries form a surrogate window. GRKV first computes the full-cache output Y for that window. With keys fixed, updating values is ridge regression because attention weights are fixed. With values fixed, the key update is locally linearized and solved in a regularized form; a dual/Woodbury expression reduces cost. One alternating step is used by default. Information from evicted rows is thereby spread across all unfixed retained carriers rather than assigned one-to-one by a local merge map.

At a high level, GRKV maps an original state into a constrained representation and then uses that representation to approximate, route, or compose the computation that would otherwise operate on the full state. Three quantities must remain distinct: the declared budget, the effective representation actually retained, and the downstream objective used to judge adequacy. Conflating them would make a nominally equal-budget comparison misleading.

### 2.1 Functional reconstruction objective

$$\widetilde{\mathcal L}_{win}(K,V)=\|Y-\operatorname{softmax}(Q_{win}K^\top/\sqrt d)V\|_F^2$$

Y is the attention output obtained with the full cache. The compressed keys and values are optimized to match this output on the surrogate queries rather than to copy individual evicted vectors. The equation is reconstructed as a conceptual model of the paper's operation; its symbols should be interpreted with the definitions and constraints given in the source paper, not as an independent theorem supplied by this review.

### 2.2 Ridge-regularized value update

$$V^*=\arg\min_V\|Y-AV\|_F^2+\lambda_v\|V-V^0_{Ret}\|_F^2$$

With attention A fixed by the retained keys, the value step is convex ridge regression. The regularizer preserves carrier identity while allowing distributed correction. The equation is reconstructed as a conceptual model of the paper's operation; its symbols should be interpreted with the definitions and constraints given in the source paper, not as an independent theorem supplied by this review.

### 2.3 Carrier-support view

$$K^*_{Ret}=K^0_{Ret}+B_KK_{evict},\quad V^*_{Ret}=V^0_{Ret}+B_VV_{evict}$$

Local methods constrain nonzero rows of B to a small carrier set. GRKV expands the effective support to all eligible retained rows, increasing the first-order space available to approximate the full output. The equation is reconstructed as a conceptual model of the paper's operation; its symbols should be interpreted with the definitions and constraints given in the source paper, not as an independent theorem supplied by this review.

### 2.4 Assumptions and invariants

GRKV assumes the surrogate query window is sufficiently representative of later attention demands, the full-cache prefill output is available when constructing the compressed state, and a local linearization for key updates is adequate. It assumes ridge penalties prevent destructive over-fitting and that one alternating step balances fidelity with cost. The method also relies on the base eviction policy producing a retained set rich enough to reconstruct useful outputs; regression cannot recover evidence absent from both the retained span structure and the observed target.

The important invariant is that every reported comparison should account for the same constrained resource. A second invariant is lineage: calibration signals, selected policies, compressed states, and evaluation outputs must remain linked so an anomalous result can be traced to the decision that produced it. A third is separation of claims. Fidelity to an intermediate tensor or graph is evidence about mechanism, not automatically evidence about final utility; final accuracy is evidence about the evaluated task, not automatically evidence about latency, energy, or generality.

## 3. Architecture and information flow

The pipeline is explicitly post-eviction. The base method decides what survives, GRKV observes the full and retained behavior on a fixed query window, the optimizer modifies retained states, and ordinary decoding consumes the result. This separation makes compatibility testable across different eviction backbones.

| Stage | Input | Transformation | Output | Main failure surface |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Base eviction | Full prompt KV | SnapKV, CriticalKV, PyramidKV, Ada-KV, or H2O | Fixed-budget retained cache | Decisive evidence is entirely removed |
| Target capture | Full cache and surrogate queries | Compute full attention outputs | Window target Y | Window is not representative of future queries |
| Value regression | Retained attention matrix and Y | Ridge update values | Corrected V | Weak regularization blurs carriers |
| Key regression | Corrected values and target | Linearized regularized key update | Corrected K | Approximation or numerical instability |
| Decode | Optimized retained cache | Standard attention with compressed history | Generated tokens | Future demand differs from surrogate target |

This staged view exposes where information can be lost. The first loss can occur when a calibration or similarity signal is estimated. The second can occur when a discrete policy, cluster, rank, carrier, or task vector is selected. The third can occur in the compressed representation itself. The fourth can occur when the runtime reconstructs or combines that representation. Because final accuracy aggregates all four, component diagnostics are necessary to assign cause.

The dataflow also distinguishes offline and online work. Offline work may be acceptable when a model and workload remain stable, but its cost must be amortized and its results invalidated when the model revision, tokenizer, layer shapes, workload distribution, or task inventory changes. Online work must be measured in the actual serving path, including dispatch, metadata access, reconstruction, and synchronization.

## 4. Experimental design reconstructed

### 4.1 Models, data, and baselines

The main experiments use Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 with SnapKV and CriticalKV at a 10% cache budget on all 16 LongBench tasks and 13 RULER tasks. Compatibility tests add PyramidKV, Ada-KV, H2O, and Qwen3-14B. Appendices report 20% budgets. Efficiency is profiled on A6000-class GPUs from 16K to 96K contexts, with extra two-GPU and batch-size tests. CaM, D2O, and AsymKV serve as merge comparisons where feasible.

### 4.2 Metrics and comparison logic

Quality is reported per task and as LongBench or RULER average, with a count of tasks improved over the base eviction method. Systems metrics include peak memory, time to first token, prefill time, and decoding milliseconds per token. Ablations use LongBench averages under SnapKV at 10%, while compatibility always compares a base method directly against the same method plus GRKV.

The design is strongest when the baseline and proposed method share the same model, data, prompt, retained capacity, and evaluation harness. It is weaker when a claimed speedup mixes different kernels, when a quality average hides task-specific regressions, or when one configuration is selected from a larger search without accounting for selection variance. Reported means should be interpreted alongside task rows, repeated runs where available, and explicit out-of-memory cases.

### 4.3 Reproduction boundary

No experiment was independently rerun for this review. The source PDF, matching HTML, source package, tables, figures, equations, references, and appendices were inspected. The paper reports the numerical results summarized below; they are not measurements made by this reviewer unless explicitly labeled reviewer inference. This distinction matters: internal consistency and mechanistic plausibility increase confidence, but they do not substitute for an independent implementation, controlled seeds, or hardware-matched reproduction.

## 5. Results: what is reported and what it means

### 5.1 LongBench consistency

**Paper-reported evidence.** For Llama, GRKV raises SnapKV 33.96 to 34.58 and CriticalKV 36.00 to 36.58, improving 14 of 16 tasks in both cases. For Mistral, it raises SnapKV 33.12 to 33.75 and CriticalKV 33.69 to 34.30, improving 12/16 and 14/16 tasks.

**Interpretation.** Small but broad gains are consistent with recovery distributed across tasks rather than one outlier average.

**Boundary.** The absolute deltas are below one point and remain dependent on base eviction quality.

### 5.2 RULER consistency

**Paper-reported evidence.** For Llama, SnapKV rises 27.44 to 29.09 and CriticalKV 40.83 to 41.51. For Mistral, SnapKV rises 21.18 to 21.67 and CriticalKV 22.57 to 23.10; the named pairs improve 8 or 10 of 13 tasks.

**Interpretation.** Global reconstruction is less likely than local over-merging to destroy retrieval signals after span eviction.

**Boundary.** Scores remain far below FullKV on many severe compression tasks, so GRKV only partially recovers information.

### 5.3 Compatibility

**Paper-reported evidence.** On Llama LongBench, PyramidKV improves 34.40 to 35.21, Ada-KV 34.72 to 35.28, and token-based H2O 29.65 to 31.68. On Qwen3-14B, SnapKV improves 38.29 to 38.98 and CriticalKV 41.68 to 42.20.

**Interpretation.** The objective is not limited to one retention topology, although its motivating failure is strongest for spans.

**Boundary.** Compatibility evidence is benchmark-level, not proof for every eviction implementation.

### 5.4 Efficiency

**Paper-reported evidence.** At 64K, FullKV decode latency is reported as 64.84 ms/token versus 37.75 for SnapKV+GRKV. At 96K, GRKV prefill is 49.71 seconds, near D2O's 48.20 and below CaM's 53.87; AsymKV reaches 788.49 seconds.

**Interpretation.** The regression adds moderate prefill work while retaining most compressed-cache decode benefit and avoiding the extreme overhead of a heavier baseline.

**Boundary.** Hardware, batch, kernels, and exact context determine the realized trade-off.

### 5.5 Aggregate interpretation

Taken together, the results support a bounded mechanism claim: global regularized attention-output reconstruction consistently improves several base eviction methods at a fixed cache budget, but recovers only part of the quality lost to aggressive compression. The evidence is not a blanket endorsement. Averages can conceal categories for which the intervention harms performance, and nominal compression can overstate realized benefit when reconstruction or routing remains expensive. The practical decision rule should therefore be multi-objective: require an acceptable task-quality floor, a measured resource reduction in the target stack, and stable behavior across the expected workload distribution.

## 6. Ablations and causal evidence

### 6.1 Ridge strength

The default lambda_k=lambda_v=1e-2 scores 34.58, while no regularization falls to 32.06. This contrast supports the need to limit destructive carrier updates. It does not by itself establish one universal ridge value across models and budgets.

### 6.2 Surrogate window

Window m=32 scores 34.58, while m=48 and 64 fall to 33.86 and 33.49. This contrast supports a compact recent target rather than assuming more target queries are always better. It does not by itself establish future-query representativeness outside tested prompts.

### 6.3 Alternating steps

One update step is best; two or three reduce average quality. This contrast supports the paper's claim that repeated fitting can over-specialize to the surrogate window. It does not by itself establish that every prompt requires exactly one step.

### 6.4 Key and value components

Updating both keys and values reaches 34.58 and exceeds key-only or value-only variants. This contrast supports complementary correction of routing and content. It does not by itself establish equal causal contribution from the two updates.

### 6.5 What the ablations establish

The ablations improve causal attribution because they vary internal choices while holding much of the surrounding evaluation fixed. They show whether the named component tracks the headline effect and whether more aggressive settings create the expected trade-off. They remain observational within one implementation and benchmark suite. Interactions among components, search-selection effects, and hardware-specific optimizations can still contribute to the final result.

## 9. Re-conceptualization: cache merging as functional knowledge distillation at request time

GRKV can be understood as a tiny request-local distillation problem. The full cache is a teacher, the fixed-size retained cache is a student state, and the surrogate queries define the lesson. This interpretation clarifies both the strength and the failure mode. The student is trained on only a small query neighborhood, so it may reproduce the teacher locally while forgetting a future query direction. A governed implementation could retain the distillation residual and a query-coverage descriptor; if later decode queries move outside that descriptor, it can flag the cache as low confidence or refresh from a slower tier when possible.

This reframing suggests a four-ledger design. A **source ledger** identifies the immutable model, data, and full-state reference. A **decision ledger** records calibration measurements and the selected compression or routing action. An **execution ledger** records the realized representation, runtime path, hardware, and failures. An **outcome ledger** records task quality and resource measurements. Linking these ledgers turns an optimization result into a traceable trajectory rather than a single score.

The resulting self-assessment should ask four questions. Did the source state match the one used for calibration? Did the decision rule receive valid inputs? Did execution honor the declared budget and selected configuration? Did the outcome meet both quality and resource thresholds? This is not security certification. It is an auditability, observability, and lineage mechanism for detecting when a previously valid decision has become stale or when a metric was produced under different conditions.

## 10. Research notes and critical considerations

- The method's advantage should be compared against simply allocating the regression compute to a slightly larger retained budget at equal end-to-end cost.
- Per-head reconstruction residuals could reveal which layers or prompts are unsafe to compress globally.
- The fixed-token set protects anchors but also shrinks carrier capacity; its optimal size may correlate with prompt structure.
- Full-cache target capture makes the method best suited to prefill compression, not recovery after the full state has already been discarded.
- Task-level gains should be paired with residual statistics to test whether functional fit predicts downstream quality.

Two cross-cutting concerns deserve emphasis. First, intermediate proxies can be useful while still being imperfect. Similarity, entropy, reconstruction error, graph connectivity, and validation loss each compress a complex future effect into one number. They should be validated against the downstream task and monitored for distribution shift. Second, a static artifact can hide dynamic behavior. Per-layer policies, cache contents, retrieved neighbors, cluster assignments, and task mixtures change which information is available at each step; evaluation should sample those trajectories rather than only the final output.

## 11. Potential implications

- Post-eviction compression modules need compatibility tests with the topology produced by the base selector.
- Functional objectives can be more reliable than representation similarity when the real requirement is preserving model outputs.
- Request-local calibration artifacts can support self-assessment if residuals and fixed-token choices are logged.
- Systems should compare equal total cost, not only equal retained slots, when adding regression work.
- Fallback can be triggered by high residual or out-of-range query geometry rather than silently trusting every compressed cache.

For a production system, these implications point toward explicit fallback modes. When calibration evidence is missing, stale, or out of range, the system should prefer a conservative full-state or less-compressed path if capacity permits. When capacity does not permit that fallback, the output should carry a degraded-confidence marker and the exact policy evidence used. Silent extrapolation is the least auditable option.

## 12. New hypotheses derived from the paper

The following are reviewer inferences and proposed experiments, not claims made or established by the paper:

1. Adaptive surrogate-window selection based on query diversity will outperform a fixed last-32 window at equal optimization cost.
2. Residual-gated fallback will capture a large fraction of GRKV failures with a small loss in compression coverage.
3. Block-diagonal carrier groups will recover most global-regression gains with lower synchronization cost on long contexts.
4. Jointly selecting retained spans and regression carriers will outperform the current sequential eviction-then-reconstruction pipeline.
5. At equal end-to-end prefill latency, GRKV will remain preferable only below a workload-dependent cache-budget threshold.

These hypotheses are useful only if they are falsifiable. Each should specify an intervention, a comparison, a metric, and a failure threshold before results are observed. Otherwise, post-hoc explanations can make almost any outcome appear consistent with the mechanism.

## 13. Deployment and governance considerations

A GRKV receipt should record the full-cache source hash, base eviction method and revision, retained indices, fixed-token indices, surrogate queries, lambda values, update count, residual before and after, and realized timing. The optimized cache is derived state and should carry the receipt hash. If later queries exceed a declared residual or coverage threshold, the system should record a degraded state. These records support lineage and diagnosis, not security certification.

A deployment record should include model and tokenizer hashes, source-data identity, calibration-data identity, selected policy parameters, declared and realized budgets, code and kernel revisions, device type, benchmark or workload slice, random seeds, and all metric definitions. Public summaries should omit private paths and internal records while preserving canonical source URLs and content hashes. A failure or rollback should append a new record rather than rewrite the decision that was originally made.

The methodology's purpose is auditability, observability, and traceable lineage. Passing these checks does not certify security, privacy, fairness, or correctness. Those require separate threat models, access controls, data-governance reviews, and domain-specific evaluation. The contribution of the ledger is narrower but important: it makes claims inspectable and lets an operator distinguish stale calibration, invalid inputs, execution drift, and downstream quality failure.

## 14. Replication and falsification agenda

1. Reproduce all 16 LongBench and 13 RULER rows at 10% and 20% cache budgets for each base method.
2. Verify merge maps and quantify carrier-load concentration before and after global regression.
3. Recreate ridge, fixed-token, window-size, update-step, key-only, value-only, and joint ablations.
4. Measure functional residuals and correlate them with task-level gains and regressions.
5. Compare against a larger retained cache at equal prefill time and total memory traffic.
6. Repeat A6000 profiling with fixed kernels, batch sizes, synchronization, warm-up, and context lengths.
7. Test multilingual, code-heavy, multimodal, and distribution-shift prompts.
8. Publish request-level configurations and raw timings so average overhead can be recomputed.

The minimum acceptance package should contain raw task rows, configuration files, environment and hardware descriptions, immutable model identifiers, calibration artifacts, and scripts that recompute all aggregates. A systems claim should include warm-up policy, batch size, sequence lengths, memory measurement method, synchronization points, kernel versions, and out-of-memory handling. A quality claim should include seeds or repeated runs where randomness exists and should disclose configuration-search breadth.

Falsification matters as much as reproduction. A successful rerun of one headline average does not test whether the mechanism is correct. Stronger tests intentionally invert the decision signal, shuffle calibration labels, equalize budgets, substitute a neighboring model, move to an out-of-domain workload, and isolate the alleged bottleneck. If the method's advantage survives when its supposed causal signal is destroyed, the explanation needs revision.

## 15. Durable restatement

GRKV shows that a merge rule should match the geometry created by the eviction rule. When spans concentrate local carriers, global regularized fitting to the full-cache attention function recovers more useful behavior than one-to-one local similarity. The method is not lossless and its surrogate window is a real boundary, but its functional objective, broad compatibility tests, and moderate overhead make it a credible post-eviction design pattern.

The durable lesson is not that GRKV should always be selected. It is that a resource-constrained representation should be treated as a structured decision whose evidence can be inspected. The paper contributes one concrete decision rule and a set of measurements. Its lasting value will depend on whether future work reproduces the gains, clarifies the failure regions, and preserves enough lineage to explain why the policy worked or failed in a new setting.
