# Layer-Wise Policy Composition for KV Cache Budgets

## A detailed review and independent re-conceptualization of “PolyKV: Heterogeneous Retention and Allocation for KV Cache Compression”

**Source paper:** Chao Fei, Panos Kalnis, “PolyKV: Heterogeneous Retention and Allocation for KV Cache Compression,” arXiv:2606.15157v1.[^source-paper]

**Artifact prepared:** 2026-07-15  
**Local source reviewed:** complete PDF, matching full-paper HTML, source package, equations, tables, figures, references, appendices, and limitations; private paths withheld  
**Review scope:** full-paper reconstruction, quantitative audit, claim calibration, external context, independent re-conceptualization, governance implications, and replication agenda  
**Review status:** source artifacts and reported experiments were inspected; experiments were not independently rerun or reproduced

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

PolyKV treats KV-cache eviction as a phase-aware, layer-wise composition problem, selecting different existing retention policies and allocating non-uniform per-layer cache budgets from offline calibration signals while holding total capacity fixed.

### The deeper idea

The paper's durable idea is that a transformer is not one homogeneous cache consumer. A model-wide rule assumes that every layer needs the same evidence shape and the same number of retained states. PolyKV exposes two independent decisions—what pattern to retain and how much capacity to allocate—then calibrates those decisions separately for prefill and decoding. The contribution is therefore a policy composition layer over existing eviction primitives, not a new primitive that replaces them.

### Bottom-line judgment

The paper provides credible evidence that heterogeneous eviction routing can recover part of the quality lost by uniform compression on LongBench. Under an average 512-token budget, it reports improvements over the strongest single-policy baseline for both LLaMA-3.1-8B and Qwen3-8B, with 54.5% and 25.7% recovery of the FullKV gap. The budget sweep and signal inversions support the mechanism. The boundary is equally important: Qwen RULER drops 3.98 points below its best baseline, code and sparse-retrieval categories remain weak, and non-uniform allocation is less robust than policy selection.

The most defensible reading is therefore conditional. The paper supplies evidence about PolyKV under its named models, datasets, budgets, and implementation. It does not convert those observations into a universal law about compression or adaptation. A deployment decision should preserve the exact experimental coordinates—model revision, context length, hardware, baseline implementation, retained capacity, calibration data, and metric definition—because changing any one of them can change which design wins.

### Principal strengths

- It holds total KV capacity fixed while varying per-layer policies, preventing a larger hidden cache from explaining the LongBench gains.
- It distinguishes prefill and decoding and integrates multiple established eviction primitives through one hook-based implementation.
- Forward-versus-inverse signal tests provide stronger mechanistic evidence than a headline benchmark average alone.
- The paper reports unfavorable RULER and task-category results rather than presenting the method as uniformly superior.

### Principal qualifications

1. Calibration is model-, phase-, budget-, and workload-dependent; a fixed policy can become stale after any of those coordinates change.
2. The strongest evidence is LongBench on two 8B models, while controlled sparse retrieval and code workloads reveal regressions.
3. Budget-sensitivity signals are weak or reversed on Qwen, so the allocation mechanism is not yet reliably portable.
4. The paper evaluates static calibrated policies but does not establish end-to-end serving gains, energy savings, or behavior under changing traffic.

## 1. The problem the paper is actually solving

### 1.1 Problem, stakes, and operational setting

Long-context inference stores a key and value representation for prior tokens at every transformer layer. A uniform compressed cache applies one eviction rule and usually one nominal capacity across depth, even though layers exhibit different attention patterns, abstractions, contextual roles, and pruning sensitivity. The paper asks whether a fixed global resource budget can be used more effectively by assigning both the retention rule and the capacity at layer granularity, separately for prefill and decode. This matters because a poor prefill decision removes evidence before later generation can request it, while an over-compressed layer can become a bottleneck even when other layers retain redundant state.

The relevant resource is not merely a file size or nominal ratio. It is a constrained system state that affects memory residency, transfer volume, arithmetic intensity, batch capacity, latency, and downstream quality. A method can reduce one component while adding calibration, reconstruction, routing, metadata, or kernel overhead elsewhere. For that reason, this review keeps algorithmic compression, task quality, and realized systems efficiency separate throughout.

### 1.2 Prior method families

The candidate primitives include cumulative-attention and heavy-hitter methods such as H2O, recent-query-window methods such as SnapKV and PyramidKV, persistence-oriented ScissorHands, position-based StreamingLLM, last-query TOVA, and adaptive schemes including CAKE and D2O. Earlier adaptive methods often vary budgets across layer or head depth while retaining one common scoring policy. PolyKV's distinction is compositional: it allows a candidate retention primitive and an integer capacity to vary for each layer-phase pair under one sum constraint.

These families encode different assumptions about where redundancy lives and what information is safe to share or discard. A fair comparison must therefore hold the true capacity constraint constant and disclose whether preprocessing, fine-tuning, calibration, index construction, task exemplars, or special kernels are included in the cost boundary.

### 1.3 Claimed gap and contribution

The paper argues that existing methods explore only slices of the design space. A uniform policy ignores which evidence pattern a layer needs; a non-uniform budget alone ignores whether the scoring rule is appropriate. PolyKV uses a small disjoint calibration set to estimate eviction preference through activation cosine similarity or single-layer perplexity degradation and estimates budget sensitivity through attention entropy or perplexity sensitivity. Those signals produce a static deployment configuration without online search.

The paper's contribution is best treated as a mechanism plus an evidence package. The mechanism proposes a new intervention; the evidence package attempts to show that the intervention, not a hidden increase in resources or an incomparable baseline, explains the measured result. The rest of this review reconstructs both parts.

## 2. Formal and technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Method overview

For L transformer layers and phases prefill and decode, PolyKV defines a strategy containing a retention rule and positive integer budget for every layer-phase pair. Calibration first runs FullKV to obtain reference activations. Candidate runs perturb one layer-phase pair at a time to score how well each policy preserves the reference. A second set of perturbations measures sensitivity as capacity is reduced. The selected rule is the best-ranked candidate for each pair; the residual capacity above a minimum budget is distributed in proportion to sensitivity and rounded while preserving the exact total. Runtime hooks dispatch the configured primitive at the relevant phase and layer.

At a high level, PolyKV maps an original state into a constrained representation and then uses that representation to approximate, route, or compose the computation that would otherwise operate on the full state. Three quantities must remain distinct: the declared budget, the effective representation actually retained, and the downstream objective used to judge adequacy. Conflating them would make a nominally equal-budget comparison misleading.

### 2.1 Phase-aware policy and capacity plan

$$\Pi=\{(\{r_{l,\phi}\}_{\phi\in\Phi},\{b_{l,\phi}\}_{\phi\in\Phi})\}_{l=1}^{L},\quad \sum_l b_{l,\phi}=B_\phi,\quad b_{l,\phi}\ge b_{\min}$$

Each layer and inference phase receives one policy r and one budget b. FullKV is not free: if selected, its current full-context cost counts toward the same total. This accounting is central to interpreting equal-budget comparisons. The equation is reconstructed as a conceptual model of the paper's operation; its symbols should be interpreted with the definitions and constraints given in the source paper, not as an independent theorem supplied by this review.

### 2.2 Eviction-preference calibration

$$C_{l,\phi,r}=\mathbb{E}_{x\sim D_{cal}}[\cos(h^{full}_{l,\phi}(x),h^{(l,\phi,r)}_{l,\phi}(x))]$$

The cosine signal perturbs only one layer-phase pair and measures representation preservation relative to FullKV. The alternative perplexity signal prefers the candidate with the smallest degradation. Both are proxies for future task utility. The equation is reconstructed as a conceptual model of the paper's operation; its symbols should be interpreted with the definitions and constraints given in the source paper, not as an independent theorem supplied by this review.

### 2.3 Sensitivity-weighted allocation

$$\tilde b_{l,\phi}=b_{min}+(B-Lb_{min})\frac{s_{l,\phi}+\epsilon}{\sum_j(s_{j,\phi}+\epsilon)}$$

The allocator gives every layer a minimum, then distributes the residual budget according to a sensitivity score before exact-sum integer rounding. The smoothing term avoids zero allocation but also makes the final plan depend on score scale. ### 2.4 Assumptions and invariants

PolyKV assumes that a small calibration set is representative enough to rank policies and sensitivities, that single-pair perturbations predict behavior when many layer decisions are composed, and that a strategy selected at one target budget remains meaningful during deployment. It also assumes that the candidate policy implementations are comparable within the shared wrapper and that downstream quality is sufficiently reflected by LongBench and RULER. The paper's own negative results show the calibration proxy can misalign with sparse exact-evidence tasks.

The important invariant is that every reported comparison should account for the same constrained resource. A second invariant is lineage: calibration signals, selected policies, compressed states, and evaluation outputs must remain linked so an anomalous result can be traced to the decision that produced it. A third is separation of claims. Fidelity to an intermediate tensor or graph is evidence about mechanism, not automatically evidence about final utility; final accuracy is evidence about the evaluated task, not automatically evidence about latency, energy, or generality.

## 3. Architecture and information flow

The implementation separates policy description from runtime execution. A configuration identifies layer ranges and inference phases; hooks expose the prefill and decode points required by each method; and a composition wrapper dispatches the chosen behavior. This modularity makes the design space inspectable but introduces a configuration artifact whose provenance must be maintained.

| Stage | Input | Transformation | Output | Main failure surface |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Full-state calibration | Model, calibration prompts, FullKV | Capture reference activations and perplexity | Layer-phase reference signals | Calibration data or model revision is unrepresentative |
| Policy scoring | Candidate eviction rules | Perturb one layer-phase pair | Cosine or perplexity ranking | Independent perturbations do not compose |
| Budget scoring | Reduced-budget trials | Estimate entropy or perplexity sensitivity | Per-layer sensitivity | Proxy scale reverses on a new architecture |
| Plan construction | Rankings, minimum, total budget | Select rules and round allocations | Static phase-aware plan | Rounding or starvation changes the intended ordering |
| Runtime dispatch | Plan and attention state | Invoke configured retention hooks | Compressed cache per layer | Implementation overhead or stale plan |

This staged view exposes where information can be lost. The first loss can occur when a calibration or similarity signal is estimated. The second can occur when a discrete policy, cluster, rank, carrier, or task vector is selected. The third can occur in the compressed representation itself. The fourth can occur when the runtime reconstructs or combines that representation. Because final accuracy aggregates all four, component diagnostics are necessary to assign cause.

The dataflow also distinguishes offline and online work. Offline work may be acceptable when a model and workload remain stable, but its cost must be amortized and its results invalidated when the model revision, tokenizer, layer shapes, workload distribution, or task inventory changes. Online work must be measured in the actual serving path, including dispatch, metadata access, reconstruction, and synchronization.

## 4. Experimental design reconstructed

### 4.1 Models, data, and baselines

The paper evaluates LLaMA-3.1-8B and Qwen3-8B on LongBench and RULER. Candidate methods include H2O, TOVA, CAM, PyramidKV, SnapKV, D2O, CAKE, ScissorHands, and StreamingLLM. The headline comparison uses an average 512 retained KV tokens per layer with the same total capacity for all methods. A LLaMA LongBench sweep covers average budgets 128, 256, 512, 768, and 1024. Additional experiments invert signal rankings, separate prefill from decode, decompose task categories, and inspect a representative per-layer strategy.

### 4.2 Metrics and comparison logic

LongBench uses its 21-task macro average; RULER uses a global average over controlled retrieval and tracking tasks. The paper reports FullKV, the strongest uniform baseline, the best PolyKV candidate, a top-five range, and the delta. It also expresses some gains as the percentage of the compression-induced gap to FullKV that is recovered. Signal tests compare forward rankings with deliberately inverted rankings under matched capacity.

The design is strongest when the baseline and proposed method share the same model, data, prompt, retained capacity, and evaluation harness. It is weaker when a claimed speedup mixes different kernels, when a quality average hides task-specific regressions, or when one configuration is selected from a larger search without accounting for selection variance. Reported means should be interpreted alongside task rows, repeated runs where available, and explicit out-of-memory cases.

### 4.3 Reproduction boundary

No experiment was independently rerun for this review. The source PDF, matching HTML, source package, tables, figures, equations, references, and appendices were inspected. The paper reports the numerical results summarized below; they are not measurements made by this reviewer unless explicitly labeled reviewer inference. This distinction matters: internal consistency and mechanistic plausibility increase confidence, but they do not substitute for an independent implementation, controlled seeds, or hardware-matched reproduction.

## 5. Results: what is reported and what it means

### 5.1 Fixed 512-token LongBench budget

**Paper-reported evidence.** On LLaMA, FullKV scores 38.99, CAKE 36.35, and the best PolyKV configuration 37.79, a +1.44 gain and 54.5% gap recovery. On Qwen, FullKV scores 49.11, CAKE 47.01, and PolyKV 47.54, a +0.54 gain and 25.7% gap recovery.

**Interpretation.** Heterogeneous routing reaches configurations not represented by any one global policy, and the narrow top-five ranges suggest the result is not one isolated candidate.

**Boundary.** The gains are benchmark averages for one nominal budget; they do not prove a universal layer policy or systems speedup.

### 5.2 RULER boundary

**Paper-reported evidence.** At the same budget, LLaMA RULER improves only from 58.54 to 58.91, while Qwen falls from the best baseline's 55.59 to 51.61. The Qwen top-five range is also wide.

**Interpretation.** Calibration that preserves broad contextual representations can still discard rare decisive tokens required by controlled retrieval.

**Boundary.** A favorable LongBench average should not be used as a proxy for exact-evidence reliability.

### 5.3 Budget sweep

**Paper-reported evidence.** On LLaMA LongBench, PolyKV improves over the strongest baseline at every average budget from 128 to 1024. Deltas decline from +2.16 at 128 and +2.22 at 256 to +0.65 at 1024 as methods approach FullKV.

**Interpretation.** Heterogeneous retention provides its largest absolute leverage when the cache is tight; the recoverable gap naturally narrows as capacity grows.

**Boundary.** The sweep is one model-benchmark family and does not show the same stability on Qwen RULER.

### 5.4 Task categories

**Paper-reported evidence.** Both models gain on LongBench QA and few-shot categories; LLaMA gains +3.9 on synthetic retrieval while Qwen changes -0.3. Code falls for both, and Qwen RULER NIAH retrieval falls 5.9 points.

**Interpretation.** Policy composition appears better aligned with distributed contextual evidence than exact surface-form or sparse-needle preservation.

**Boundary.** Category aggregates still combine tasks and do not identify which individual token losses caused failures.

### 5.5 Aggregate interpretation

Taken together, the results support a bounded mechanism claim: layer-wise policy selection is a useful degree of freedom under fixed capacity, especially for LongBench, while the present allocation signals and sparse-retrieval behavior are not consistently reliable. The evidence is not a blanket endorsement. Averages can conceal categories for which the intervention harms performance, and nominal compression can overstate realized benefit when reconstruction or routing remains expensive. The practical decision rule should therefore be multi-objective: require an acceptable task-quality floor, a measured resource reduction in the target stack, and stable behavior across the expected workload distribution.

## 6. Ablations and causal evidence

### 6.1 Forward versus inverse eviction routing

Forward cosine routing substantially exceeds inverse routing, especially when prefill is affected; for Qwen, the reported forward-minus-inverse difference reaches 36.72 for prefill and 42.73 when both phases are changed. This contrast supports the claim that the calibration ranking contains useful information about policy choice. It does not by itself establish that cosine is the unique or optimal signal across workloads.

### 6.2 Budget-signal inversion

Entropy and perplexity allocation are mildly positive on LLaMA but negative on Qwen in the forward-versus-inverse test. This contrast supports the paper's own conclusion that allocation is more model-dependent than eviction routing. It does not by itself establish a portable rule for distributing capacity.

### 6.3 Uniform versus heterogeneous allocation

At the smallest budgets, uniform allocation is more reliable; from average budget 512 upward, non-uniform allocation becomes helpful on the reported LLaMA sweep. This contrast supports a starvation threshold below which redistribution can harm sensitive layers. It does not by itself establish that 512 is a general threshold outside the tested model.

### 6.4 Representative plan inspection

A successful LLaMA plan uses six prefill eviction patterns with per-layer budgets ranging from 238 to 1058 while keeping decode full in that evaluation. This contrast supports the existence of meaningful depth heterogeneity in a high-performing candidate. It does not by itself establish that the same visual pattern transfers to another model.

### 6.5 What the ablations establish

The ablations improve causal attribution because they vary internal choices while holding much of the surrounding evaluation fixed. They show whether the named component tracks the headline effect and whether more aggressive settings create the expected trade-off. They remain observational within one implementation and benchmark suite. Interactions among components, search-selection effects, and hardware-specific optimizations can still contribute to the final result.

The table deliberately uses calibrated language. “Supported” means the paper contains direct measurements consistent with the claim under its evaluated conditions. “Partially supported” means the mechanism is plausible or the measurements are favorable but the scope is narrower than the prose might suggest. “Not established” is reserved for generalization, causal, or systems claims that require evidence outside the reported boundary.

## 8. External context

### D2O and modern eviction primitives

The paper composes established primitives rather than evaluating in a vacuum. D2O's primary record is useful for understanding one candidate's dynamic discriminative operations and its own assumptions; PolyKV's contribution is the layer-phase assignment wrapper and calibration, not ownership of the underlying primitive. The canonical external record is https://openreview.net/forum?id=HzBfoUdjHt.

### LongBench

LongBench evaluates varied long-context understanding tasks and supplies the broad contextual workload on which PolyKV is strongest. Its macro average should be read with the paper's category breakdown because exact retrieval and code respond differently. The canonical external record is https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.14508.

### RULER

RULER provides controlled retrieval, extraction, and tracking stress tests. The negative Qwen result is therefore valuable counter-evidence about sparse-token preservation rather than a nuisance to be averaged away. The canonical external record is https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.06654.

## 9. Re-conceptualization: a compiled cache policy with invalidation rules

PolyKV can be reinterpreted as a compiler. Calibration observations are the profile, candidate methods are the instruction set, the per-layer plan is compiled code, and runtime hooks execute that code. This view immediately introduces a missing systems concept: invalidation. A compiled cache policy should carry a compatibility key containing the model weights, tokenizer, attention implementation, candidate-method revisions, target budget, calibration corpus, and workload class. Any mismatch should invalidate the plan or require an explicit exception. The plan can also expose confidence margins: if the top two policies are nearly tied for a layer, a conservative fallback or additional calibration is more defensible than an arbitrary winner.

This reframing suggests a four-ledger design. A **source ledger** identifies the immutable model, data, and full-state reference. A **decision ledger** records calibration measurements and the selected compression or routing action. An **execution ledger** records the realized representation, runtime path, hardware, and failures. An **outcome ledger** records task quality and resource measurements. Linking these ledgers turns an optimization result into a traceable trajectory rather than a single score.

The resulting self-assessment should ask four questions. Did the source state match the one used for calibration? Did the decision rule receive valid inputs? Did execution honor the declared budget and selected configuration? Did the outcome meet both quality and resource thresholds? This is not security certification. It is an auditability, observability, and lineage mechanism for detecting when a previously valid decision has become stale or when a metric was produced under different conditions.

## 10. Research notes and critical considerations

- The expected sequence of policy selection and capacity allocation matters because changing the budget can change which policy appears best; a joint optimizer might find interactions missed by independent calibration.
- Single-layer perturbations create a tractable approximation but may underestimate composition effects when errors accumulate or cancel across depth.
- The top-five candidate range is useful evidence, yet the total search breadth and selection procedure should be recorded to quantify winner's-curse risk.
- RULER failures suggest adding an explicit sparse-evidence preservation constraint rather than relying solely on representation similarity.
- A cache plan is operational configuration and should be versioned, hashed, and linked to every benchmark or serving trace that uses it.

Two cross-cutting concerns deserve emphasis. First, intermediate proxies can be useful while still being imperfect. Similarity, entropy, reconstruction error, graph connectivity, and validation loss each compress a complex future effect into one number. They should be validated against the downstream task and monitored for distribution shift. Second, a static artifact can hide dynamic behavior. Per-layer policies, cache contents, retrieved neighbors, cluster assignments, and task mixtures change which information is available at each step; evaluation should sample those trajectories rather than only the final output.

## 11. Potential implications

- Agent-serving systems can allocate memory by layer and phase, but should gate the policy on workload and model identity.
- Calibration artifacts are reusable evidence and should be retained separately from deployment summaries.
- Benchmark acceptance should require category-level floors so average improvement cannot hide exact-retrieval regression.
- A modular policy wrapper can accelerate comparative research if it normalizes total capacity and exposes all added runtime work.
- Policy invalidation and rollback are first-class requirements for automation that updates models or kernels.

For a production system, these implications point toward explicit fallback modes. When calibration evidence is missing, stale, or out of range, the system should prefer a conservative full-state or less-compressed path if capacity permits. When capacity does not permit that fallback, the output should carry a degraded-confidence marker and the exact policy evidence used. Silent extrapolation is the least auditable option.

## 12. New hypotheses derived from the paper

The following are reviewer inferences and proposed experiments, not claims made or established by the paper:

1. Adding a small protected sparse-evidence reserve to every layer would reduce RULER NIAH regressions while preserving most LongBench gains; test equal total budgets with and without the reserve.
2. Jointly calibrating adjacent layer blocks will outperform independent layer scoring when cross-layer errors interact; compare block perturbations against the current single-layer approximation.
3. Confidence-weighted fallbacks will make policy transfer safer across neighboring model revisions; invalidate low-margin assignments first and measure quality drift.
4. A workload mixture with more code and exact extraction will select a measurably different prefill routing plan from the reported LongBench calibration.
5. End-to-end throughput gains will depend more on implementation compatibility among composed primitives than on nominal token budgets alone.

These hypotheses are useful only if they are falsifiable. Each should specify an intervention, a comparison, a metric, and a failure threshold before results are observed. Otherwise, post-hoc explanations can make almost any outcome appear consistent with the mechanism.

## 13. Deployment and governance considerations

A PolyKV deployment should treat the calibrated configuration as an immutable governed artifact. It should not be silently regenerated after a model update, nor should a summary state that the queue or validation is complete when some layer-budget experiment is missing. Self-assessment should compare declared total budget with realized per-layer cache sizes, verify every configured primitive exists at runtime, and preserve both favorable and unfavorable task rows. A configuration that passes LongBench but violates an exact-retrieval floor should be labeled unsuitable for that workload rather than globally complete.

A deployment record should include model and tokenizer hashes, source-data identity, calibration-data identity, selected policy parameters, declared and realized budgets, code and kernel revisions, device type, benchmark or workload slice, random seeds, and all metric definitions. Public summaries should omit private paths and internal records while preserving canonical source URLs and content hashes. A failure or rollback should append a new record rather than rewrite the decision that was originally made.

The methodology's purpose is auditability, observability, and traceable lineage. Passing these checks does not certify security, privacy, fairness, or correctness. Those require separate threat models, access controls, data-governance reviews, and domain-specific evaluation. The contribution of the ledger is narrower but important: it makes claims inspectable and lets an operator distinguish stale calibration, invalid inputs, execution drift, and downstream quality failure.

## 14. Replication and falsification agenda

1. Recreate FullKV and every candidate baseline at exactly the same total per-phase capacity, model revision, tokenizer, prompt, and decoding settings.
2. Regenerate cosine, perplexity, entropy, and sensitivity signals from the disclosed calibration split and hash the resulting layer-phase matrices.
3. Verify that exact-sum rounding preserves B for every phase and record any layer pinned to the minimum or assigned FullKV.
4. Repeat the forward-versus-inverse tests and report task rows, not only averages, for both models.
5. Run RULER NIAH and code workloads as explicit falsification gates before claiming workload transfer.
6. Measure calibration time, plan construction, peak memory, TTFT, decode latency, throughput, and energy in one shared runtime.
7. Perturb model revision, calibration corpus, and target budget separately to determine plan invalidation thresholds.
8. Release the exact candidate-method revisions and wrapper configuration needed to reconstruct every table and representative plan.

The minimum acceptance package should contain raw task rows, configuration files, environment and hardware descriptions, immutable model identifiers, calibration artifacts, and scripts that recompute all aggregates. A systems claim should include warm-up policy, batch size, sequence lengths, memory measurement method, synchronization points, kernel versions, and out-of-memory handling. A quality claim should include seeds or repeated runs where randomness exists and should disclose configuration-search breadth.

Falsification matters as much as reproduction. A successful rerun of one headline average does not test whether the mechanism is correct. Stronger tests intentionally invert the decision signal, shuffle calibration labels, equalize budgets, substitute a neighboring model, move to an out-of-domain workload, and isolate the alleged bottleneck. If the method's advantage survives when its supposed causal signal is destroyed, the explanation needs revision.

## 15. Durable restatement

PolyKV shows that cache compression can be improved by separating the identity of the retention rule from the amount of capacity and making both decisions at layer and phase granularity. Its strongest evidence is that calibrated heterogeneous routing recovers a substantial fraction of LongBench quality at fixed capacity. Its most important warning is that a proxy optimized for broad contextual preservation can fail on sparse exact evidence. A responsible implementation should therefore compile, hash, validate, and invalidate cache policies against explicit workload requirements.

The durable lesson is not that PolyKV should always be selected. It is that a resource-constrained representation should be treated as a structured decision whose evidence can be inspected. The paper contributes one concrete decision rule and a set of measurements. Its lasting value will depend on whether future work reproduces the gains, clarifies the failure regions, and preserves enough lineage to explain why the policy worked or failed in a new setting.
