# Semantic Grouping Helps, but SeCo Does Not Eliminate Position

## A full-paper review and independent re-conceptualization of “Beyond Position Bias: Shifting Context Compression from Position-Driven to Semantic-Driven”

**Paper:** Jiwei Tang, Zhijing Huang, Xinyu Zhang, Chen Jason Zhang, Jianxing Yu, Libin Zheng, Rui Meng, and Jian Yin, arXiv:2605.09463v1, 10 May 2026.[^1]
**Scope:** complete PDF and full-paper HTML, TeX source, main and appendix sections, every numbered table and figure, central equations and theoretical claims, training details, limitations, ethics and model-usage statements, and public artifact vetting.
**Review activity:** full-source reconstruction, numerical and complexity audit, and artifact-status check. No model, dataset split, latency result, or code path was independently reproduced.

---

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

SeCo replaces contiguous or position-indexed soft-token grouping with query-conditioned semantic clustering and reports unusually large gains across MRQA tasks, but it still consumes position-bearing encoder states, performs a potentially quadratic assignment, and lacks the split, seed, and artifact evidence needed to substantiate the breadth of its claims.

### Durable contribution

The useful idea is simple: choose `K` context tokens most similar to a pooled query as semantic centers, assign every remaining token to a center using query-weighted cosine similarity, and merge each group into one soft token. This decouples group membership from adjacency. A constraint stated early in one document can be combined with a semantically similar qualification much later, rather than being summarized only with its physical neighbors.

The paper also separates three failure modes that compression research often conflates: where special memory tokens are inserted, how raw tokens are grouped, and how grouped information is merged. The proposed center/assignment/merge decomposition is reusable even if SeCo’s particular scoring rules are replaced.

### Bottom-line judgment

The reported results are striking. At 16× compression with Llama-3.2-1B, SeCo reports average in-domain EM/F1 of 53.52/68.55 versus the strongest listed average of 42.07/54.37. On six out-of-domain tasks it reports 42.33/56.78 versus 24.46/35.54. At 32×, performance barely changes. The ablations also show large drops when query weighting, assignment/merging, or semantic center selection is removed.

Those results deserve serious follow-up, but the evidence is not yet proportionate to the claim. The manuscript does not report sample counts for evaluation splits, seed-level results, confidence intervals, statistical tests, answer-generation settings, or per-baseline tuning. “All baselines use the same datasets and hyperparameters” is not necessarily a fairness guarantee because architectures can require different optimal learning rates and ranks. The paper’s public code link points to an anonymous 4open.science snapshot rather than a versioned author repository, and the review did not execute it.[^2]

The title’s strongest wording is also too broad. SeCo avoids grouping by physical position, but its encoder hidden states still include the backbone’s positional mechanism. Center selection, token similarity, and merged vectors are therefore computed from position-conditioned representations. The appendix proves invariance of the aggregation operation under reordering of already computed vectors; it does not prove that the complete encoder–compressor mapping is position invariant. “Semantic-driven grouping” is accurate. “Eliminating position bias” is not established.

The complexity argument needs correction. With `K=ceil(Nc/τ)`, pairwise assignment costs `O(Nc K d)=O(Nc²d/τ)`. For fixed compression ratio, it remains quadratic in context length, even if the constant is smaller than attention. The experiment’s lowest measured latency at short MRQA lengths does not establish long-context scalability; NarrativeQA training reportedly used 32 H200 GPUs, which underscores the systems burden.

## 1. Problem framing and novelty boundary

Soft-prompt compressors map many context tokens to a shorter sequence of continuous vectors that a decoder consumes. Existing approaches commonly insert learned tokens at fixed positions or merge contiguous segments. The authors argue that both mechanisms can make representation quality depend on token position and can split semantically related evidence across groups.

SeCo’s novelty is query-conditioned, noncontiguous grouping in encoder hidden space. It is not the first query-aware compressor, learned soft prompt, clustering method, or encoder–decoder compression model. It differs in combining top-*K* semantic centers, global assignment, and consistency-weighted merging without assigning special positional IDs to the compressed tokens.

The architecture remains task-aware and trained. Both encoder and decoder receive LoRA adapters. Every query causes a full encoding of context plus query and a new clustering pass, so compressed representations cannot be reused across unrelated questions about the same document.

## 2. Architecture and pipeline reconstruction

### 2.1 Feature extraction

Context and query are concatenated and passed through a pretrained encoder. Last-layer hidden states are split into context matrix `Hc ∈ R^(Nc×d)` and query matrix `Hq ∈ R^(Nq×d)`. Because the encoder is a normal positional language model, `Hc` is a semantic-and-positional representation, not a position-free embedding.

The query vector is the arithmetic mean `q̄=(1/Nq)Σh_i^q`. Mean pooling is cheap and permutation invariant, but can dilute negation, alternative clauses, or multiple entities. No pooling ablation compares last token, attention pooling, multiple query vectors, or a dedicated retriever.

### 2.2 Semantic center selection

For target compression ratio `τ`, the method sets `K=max(2,ceil(Nc/τ))`. Every context token receives cosine relevance `r_i=cos(h_i,q̄)`, and the `K` largest values become centers.

This retains real context states rather than free learned slots. It also makes the bottleneck query specific. The two-token floor is sensible for tiny inputs but violates an exact compression target when `Nc/τ<2`.

### 2.3 Assignment

Every non-center token is compared with every center using cosine similarity `s_i,k`. Assignment score is `v(i,k)=r_i s_i,k`; the token joins the center with maximum product.

The product has an unexamined sign issue. If a token is negatively related to the query (`r_i<0`) and negatively related to a center (`s_i,k<0`), the product is positive and can dominate a weak positive match. Cosines can be shifted or clamped, but the paper does neither. A relevance term that is constant across candidate centers also cannot change the center ranking when it is positive; multiplying all `s_i,k` by the same positive `r_i` leaves `argmax_k` unchanged. For negative `r_i`, it reverses the ranking. Thus query relevance influences assignment only through a potentially pathological sign flip, not a smooth preference. This is a central equation-level weakness.

### 2.4 Consistency merging

Within each group, the center weight is `r_i`; non-center weights are their product scores. A softmax normalizes these weights and the compressed token is the weighted sum of the original hidden states. The `K` group vectors form the compressed prefix.

Softmax guarantees positive normalized coefficients, but a score’s meaning changes across groups and group size is ignored. Large groups may collapse more information into one vector than small groups. There is no capacity balancing, empty-center handling discussion, or guard against all relevant evidence concentrating under a few centers.

### 2.5 Training

The decoder predicts answer tokens conditioned on compressed vectors, query, and prior answer tokens under standard negative log-likelihood. Both encoder and decoder are LoRA-tuned. Training uses 16× and 32× examples, which likely explains robustness at those two ratios but complicates claims about interpolation across all rates.

The disclosed configuration is AdamW, learning rate `1e-5`, LoRA rank 128, alpha 32, dropout 0.05, all linear modules, cosine schedule, 0.1 warmup, 0.01 weight decay, bf16, and per-device batch size one. Missing items include epochs or steps, gradient accumulation, maximum lengths, random seeds, checkpoint selection, generation parameters, and loss mixing across compression ratios.

## 3. Position-bias claim audit

The introductory figures distinguish positional-encoding bias from group-by-position bias. SeCo directly addresses the second: group membership is determined by hidden-space similarity rather than adjacency.

The appendix’s permutation argument concerns the set aggregation after hidden states exist. Reordering raw tokens changes the encoder’s causal attention, positional phases, and contextual hidden states. Therefore the end-to-end output need not be permutation invariant. Likewise, averaging query token states is invariant to their ordering only after the encoder has already encoded that order.

The Nyström interpretation treats selected centers as landmarks. It is an analogy, not a derivation of attention approximation error. Centers are selected by query similarity, assignments use a product score, and merged values do not reconstruct a kernel matrix. No spectral or approximation bound connects these operations to downstream loss.

The sinusoidal and RoPE discussion argues aggregation can attenuate positional noise. That is plausible if semantic signals align and positional components cancel, but learned hidden states do not decompose cleanly into independent semantic and positional vectors. The paper’s appendix should be read as intuition and conditional analysis, not proof that SeCo removes positional bias.

## 4. Experimental design

Training samples 4,000 examples from each of six MRQA subsets: SQuAD, NewsQA, TriviaQA, SearchQA, HotpotQA, and Natural Questions. The same named datasets provide “internal testing,” but the exact official/train/dev partition and deduplication protocol are not stated. Six out-of-domain sets are DROP, BioASQ, DuoRC ParaphraseRC, TextbookQA, RelationExtraction, and RACE. DialogSum and NarrativeQA extend evaluation to summarization and long-form QA.

Backbones are Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct and Qwen3-4B-Instruct. Hardware differs materially: four RTX 4090s for the 1B model, two A800s for Qwen MRQA/DialogSum, and 32 H200s for NarrativeQA. The paper does not report wall-clock training time or energy.

Baselines are RAM, COMI, 500xCompressor, SAC, ICAE, and elsewhere EPL. Some receive enhanced position layout and others default layout. Exact implementation sources, checkpoint revisions, and whether all methods jointly tune encoder and decoder are not tabulated. Equal hyperparameters can favor SeCo if the shared rank and learning rate are closer to its optimum.

Metrics are EM and token F1 for QA and BERTScore F1 for summarization. The citation attached to F1 appears to reference ROUGE rather than the standard SQuAD evaluation implementation. No latency measurement protocol identifies GPU, batch, warm-up, synchronization, precision, or number of runs.

## 5. Results and ablations

### 5.1 Main tables

At 16× with Llama, SeCo is best in every in-domain cell, averaging 53.52 EM and 68.55 F1. At 32× it averages 52.35/67.12, a surprisingly small decline. With Qwen, SeCo has the highest average at both ratios but loses some HotpotQA and NQ cells to enhanced-layout baselines.

Out-of-domain Llama results are exceptionally large: 42.33/56.78 at 16× versus the next-highest EM 24.46 and F1 36.14; at 32× SeCo remains 41.80/56.31. Such stability and margin warrant released predictions and contamination checks, especially because the training/evaluation split protocol is underdescribed.

The DialogSum and NarrativeQA plots report superiority over RAM and COMI but do not provide a complete numerical table in source text. Plot-only values reduce auditability.

### 5.2 Latency

At 16×, SeCo reports total latency of 55.90 ms on SQuAD, 60.73 on NewsQA, and 58.67 on NQ, lowest among listed methods. Its compression time is also lowest in each row. These are end-to-end author measurements, not independently verified systems benchmarks. The input lengths and hardware are absent from the table.

The stated compression FLOPs include a full encoder pass plus selection `O(Ncd)`, assignment `O(NcKd)`, and merging `O(Ncd)`. Since `K≈Nc/τ`, assignment remains quadratic. Calling it lightweight because `NcK << L²` assumes favorable constants and lengths; asymptotically the ratio is about `1/τ`, not a different order.

### 5.3 Ablation

Default SeCo averages 54.21/68.66 across six selected ID/OOD datasets. Removing query use yields 46.28/61.65; removing assignment/merging yields 46.35/61.73; uniform centers yield 47.59/62.83. The direction supports all three components.

However, “without query” still uses query-selected centers unless the implementation changes more than the prose states. Its assignment `argmax` is already unaffected by positive `r_i`; the ablation likely measures merge weighting and negative-score behavior. Code and exact variants are necessary to interpret causality.

Stress-test plots cover ratios 2, 4, 8, 16, and 32. Five appendix positional-bias plots vary a factor `β` from 0.25 to 1.0. These figures are covered qualitatively because their exact coordinates are not tabulated.

## 9. Independent re-conceptualization

SeCo is better understood as query-conditioned vector quantization than as removal of position bias. Centers form a query-specific codebook; every token selects a code; aggregation stores a residual-free centroid. This suggests immediate improvements:

- learn a nonnegative relevance gate so query weight affects assignment continuously rather than only through sign;
- use approximate nearest-neighbor assignment or a two-stage router to avoid `Nc×K` comparisons;
- enforce balanced capacity or recursively split oversized clusters;
- retain multiple query vectors for entities, constraints, and relations;
- attach evidence spans to centers so latent outputs can be audited; and
- distinguish semantic grouping from positional debiasing in evaluation.

### A safer assignment rule

Let `g_i=sigmoid(a r_i+b)` be a nonnegative query gate and let `z_i,k` be a temperature-scaled center similarity. Assignment can minimize distance while the gate controls whether the token is merged, passed through, or stored as residual evidence. Unlike multiplication by `r_i`, this rule does not reverse center ranking for negative relevance. A small residual pool can retain low-gate tokens whose omission would otherwise be irreversible.

### Budget-aware grouping

Fixed `K` controls the number of outputs but not the information load per output. A balanced variant should constrain maximum cluster mass, split clusters with high within-group variance, and merge redundant centers. The effective budget then depends on semantic complexity rather than context length alone. This is closer to rate–distortion optimization than selecting exactly `Nc/τ` landmarks.

### Reusable and query-specific layers

A production system could build query-independent micro-clusters once, then select and merge those cached clusters per query. That would retain SeCo’s semantic routing while avoiding a full `Nc×K` assignment for every question. Evaluation should report the crossover query count at which the two-level design becomes cheaper than recomputing SeCo.

## 10. Implications

If the large out-of-domain gains reproduce, grouping policy can matter as much as soft-token capacity. Research should compare grouping under matched encoders and decoders rather than treating each compressor as an indivisible model. The results would also argue for query-dependent compression budgets: easy factoid questions may need a few tight clusters, while multi-hop questions need several evidence-bearing clusters and explicit coverage constraints.

The negative implication is auditability. A semantic group can combine distant statements whose relationship is only visible in hidden space. When an answer is wrong, operators need the selected centers, assigned spans, weights, and discarded residuals. Without that trace, SeCo replaces a transparent long prompt with an opaque continuous one. High-stakes deployment should preserve source offsets and permit raw-text fallback.

## 11. Research notes, limitations, and deployment applicability

SeCo is most applicable when every document receives few questions and quality matters more than reusable indexing, because compression is recomputed per query. For repeated queries, cached query-independent summaries plus a lightweight selector may be cheaper.

Continuous vectors are derived data and can retain sensitive text. Deployment needs access control, deletion propagation, inversion testing, model/version identifiers, and source evidence handles. Query-conditioned compression can also suppress evidence that appears irrelevant to a biased or underspecified query, making it unsuitable as the sole record for legal, medical, or compliance retrieval.

The paper’s own limitations omit clustering sign behavior, quadratic scaling, lack of uncertainty, split ambiguity, and non-reusability. Its ethical statement claims no novel risks, but latent omission and weak auditability are specific risks deserving explicit governance.

## 12. Falsifiable hypotheses

1. Replace `r_i s_i,k` with a nonnegative gated score. It should improve at least three OOD datasets and remove double-negative assignments; falsify if quality is unchanged.
2. Full-vector re-encoding after token permutation should change SeCo outputs materially, falsifying end-to-end permutation invariance.
3. Approximate assignment should reduce compression latency by at least 2× at 32K tokens with under one F1 point loss.
4. Multi-vector query pooling should outperform mean pooling on multi-hop and negation subsets.
5. Re-run all main cells with three or more seeds, paired confidence intervals, contamination checks, and released predictions.
6. Tune each baseline independently under equal compute budgets, not identical hyperparameters.
7. Publish scaling curves from 2K to 128K with encoder, assignment, merge, and generation time separated.
8. Report cluster-size distribution, center coverage, evidence recall, and exact-string retention.
9. Compare query-specific SeCo against a cached compressor under repeated-query total cost.
10. Release machine-readable values for every plot and appendix stress test.

## Replication and falsification agenda

The minimum reproduction should pin the anonymous artifact, publish every sampled training and evaluation identifier, and regenerate the two main tables from raw predictions. A stronger replication should run at least three seeds, tune baselines under equal compute budgets, and provide paired bootstrap intervals for EM/F1. The systems track should separate encoder prefill, center selection, assignment, merging, decoder prefill, and token generation at 2K, 8K, 32K, and 128K input lengths. It should report both one-query latency and amortized repeated-query cost.

The falsification track should permute raw token order, shift answer evidence across positions, inject semantically similar distractors, and create double-negative cosine cases. It should then compare SeCo with contiguous grouping, random centers, nonnegative gates, balanced clustering, and approximate assignment. Releasing cluster traces and source offsets would allow independent reviewers to distinguish retrieval failure from decoder failure.

## 14. Durable restatement

SeCo selects query-relevant token states as centers, assigns all remaining states by semantic similarity, and merges each group into one continuous prompt token. This removes physical adjacency from grouping and produces strong author-reported QA results at 16× and 32× compression.

It does not remove position from the encoder, and its key assignment equation has a sign/ranking defect relative to the stated intuition. The public record supports a promising semantic-grouping method, not a proof that context compression has become position free. Reproduction and corrected complexity/provenance reporting are the next requirements.
