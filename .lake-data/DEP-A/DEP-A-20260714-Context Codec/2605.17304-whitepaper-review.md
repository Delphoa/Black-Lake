# A Useful Contract Schema with an Author-Scored Compression Demo

## A full-paper review and independent re-conceptualization of “Compress the Context, Keep the Commitments: A Formal Framework for Verifiable LLM Context Compression”

**Paper:** Natalia Trukhina and Vadim Vashkelis, arXiv:2605.17304v1, 17 May 2026.[^1]  
**Scope:** complete 18-page PDF and full-paper HTML, TeX source, all seven tables, central equation families, algorithm listing, safety rules, limitations, and primary-source vetting.  
**Review activity:** conceptual reconstruction and metric audit. No extractor, decoder, compression result, or downstream task was independently reproduced.

---

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

Context Codec correctly reframes agent-memory compression around typed commitments and provenance, but its evidence is a five-case, author-written, author-annotated, author-scored diagnostic whose own aggregate table contradicts the claim that CCL-Core is more compact than structured prose on average.

### The durable contribution

The paper’s strongest move is to separate five concerns that systems frequently merge: extract commitments from raw conversation, normalize their identity, store a canonical representation, render a compact packet, and verify recovery. It defines a semantic atom with type, subject, predicate, value, modality, scope, evidence, confidence, and criticality. This gives memory systems an explicit place for negation, supersession, source spans, safety boundaries, and exact output contracts.

The error taxonomy is also useful: omission, weakening, mutation, polarity flip, scope error, temporal or decision error, hallucinated commitment, and safety-boundary erasure. These categories are more actionable than a generic semantic-similarity score. A fluent summary can be close in embedding space while deleting “no third-party libraries” or resurrecting a rejected architecture.

### Bottom-line judgment

The paper is a framework proposal, not a validated codec. The authors repeatedly say this, and that restraint is appropriate. The atom schema, conservative fallback rules, and round-trip protocol deserve reuse. CCL—the ASCII-first compact rendering—is secondary. Its five cases do not establish that models reliably decode it or that it improves downstream tasks.

The diagnostic is circular by construction. The same authors create five source cases, annotate 12–15 gold atoms per case, assign integer weights, write every compressed representation, and score recovery using their own normalizer. Structured prose and CCL-Core receive perfect CAR and WAR because the test authors control both encoding and decoding. No independent model sees only the compressed packet, no human judges ambiguous equivalence, and no downstream answer is generated.

The counting protocol further weakens the compression claims. Main tables use a lexical proxy that splits words and punctuation. Only six printed snippets receive `cl100k_base` and `o200k_base` counts; the full five-case representations do not. The paper itself shows why this matters: a JSON Patch diff that looks large under the lexical proxy tokenizes close to or better than CCL under BPE.

There is also a direct table/prose error. Table 5 reports structured prose at 66.4 average tokens and 24.0% gain, versus CCL-Core at 71.2 tokens and 21.8% gain. Structured prose is therefore more compact on average. The text says CCL-Core is “slightly more compact on average.” The claim is reversed by the paper’s own numbers. This must be corrected before CCL’s empirical positioning is reused.

The fair conclusion is that Context Codec is a promising specification and evaluation vocabulary. CCL-Core is one candidate serialization. Evidence for superior compression, recoverability, safety, or model usability remains absent.

## 1. Problem framing and scope

Long-running assistants accumulate goals, constraints, preferences, tool outputs, decisions, open questions, and safety conditions. When history must be shortened, token-level deletion and prose summarization do not say which behavioral obligations must survive.

The paper defines a semantic commitment as any proposition, constraint, decision, preference, state variable, procedure, or safety boundary that can change whether a future answer is correct. Examples include exact files, allowed libraries, rejected choices, deduplication keys, lodging boundaries, output-only requirements, and unresolved questions.

This framing is more appropriate for agents than pure similarity. A system should be able to answer: Which commitment was removed? Which source supported it? Was the new statement equivalent, weaker, contradictory, or superseded? Is omission allowed under the budget? Those are testable questions if a canonical schema exists.

The paper does not claim byte-identical reconstruction, lossless semantics, or a universally superior notation. Its proposed scaling intuition is that if the number of query-relevant commitments `kq` grows more slowly than transcript length, a commitment packet can scale with `kq`. This is a design principle, not a theorem, because the extractor must first identify those commitments.

Two running examples make the intended unit concrete. A Python data-cleaning prompt contains filenames, allowed dependencies, input columns, row-rejection rules, three accepted date formats, country normalization, revenue formatting, deduplication policy, report fields, and a code-only output contract. The compact packet does not preserve sentences; it preserves those acceptance conditions. A Lisbon planning dialogue contains allowed base neighborhoods, rejected heavy nightlife, a no-rental-car constraint, desired food/bookstore/viewpoint activities, rainy-day alternatives, transit notes, and cost ranges. A conventional fluent summary in the paper drops or weakens several of those negative and output commitments. These examples justify the schema’s focus on polarity, scope, and decisions.

They also reveal the framework’s boundary. Some conversation value is not naturally reducible to propositions. Tone, emotional context, narrative sequence, the reason behind a preference, and uncertainty about what a user meant may affect future correctness. An atom codec should therefore select structured obligations while retaining pointers to raw source regions. Treating the atom set as the entire meaning of a dialogue would recreate the loss the framework is designed to expose.

## 2. Formal and technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Compression objective

History `H=(m1,…,mn)` and query `q` are mapped by compressor `C` into packet `z` under token budget `B`. Model `M` answers from `(z,q)`. The conventional objective maximizes downstream utility subject to `tokens(z)≤B`.

The paper adds extractor `A(H)={a1,…,ak}` and critical subset `Ac(H,q)`. This makes semantic preservation measurable, but it shifts the hard problem into extraction and criticality. If `A` misses a commitment, every representation and metric downstream can appear perfect while the system fails.

### 2.2 Atom schema

An atom is `a=(τ,s,p,v,μ,σ,e,c,r)`:

- type `τ`: goal, constraint, decision, preference, state, output contract, safety boundary, verbatim snippet, and related classes;
- subject `s`, predicate `p`, and value `v`;
- modality `μ`: must, should, may, rejected, prefer, forbid;
- scope `σ`;
- evidence `e`, including source spans;
- confidence `c`;
- risk or criticality `r`.

The schema’s key strength is representing polarity and scope explicitly. “No external libraries” is not merely a topic about libraries; it is a negative hard constraint scoped to an artifact. Superseded decisions must remain marked as rejected rather than disappearing, otherwise retrieval can resurrect them.

Normalization is staged: clean surface form, map domain aliases, canonicalize subject and predicate, normalize values and units, preserve modality and negation, attach scope, and retain the evidence span. The paper allows narrow fixed lexicons, learned lexicons, and LLM-assisted lexicons. Each regime has a different failure profile. Fixed lexicons are auditable but brittle; learned mappings can drift; LLM normalization can hallucinate equivalence. A production record should store the normalizer version and original span so later audits can distinguish source mutation from ontology migration.

Canonical identity normalizes aliases, units, counts, dates, polarity, and domain lexicons. Two atoms are equivalent when normalized fields match under allowed transformations. Conflict captures incompatible values or modalities within overlapping scope. These relations need domain-specific rules and are not solved by the notation itself.

### 2.3 Confidence and safety

Confidence is an operational signal, not calibrated truth probability. High-risk or safety atoms require evidence and conservative rendering. The algorithm permits CCL-Min only for low-risk, high-confidence, non-safety atoms. Failed verification triggers raw spans, a larger budget, JSON, or no compression.

This fail-closed structure is sound. However, an LLM extractor’s confidence can be miscalibrated. Production policy must combine raw-source checks, deterministic rules, multiple extractors, and human tags. Safety should not depend on a self-reported scalar alone.

## 3. Metrics reconstruction and audit

### 3.1 Critical Atom Recall

`CAR = |Ac(H,q) ∩ A(z)| / |Ac(H,q)|`.

CAR asks how many required commitments remain recoverable. It measures omission but not false additions. A representation can achieve CAR 1.0 while hallucinating incompatible commitments. Precision and conflict rate must accompany it. The paper’s “perfect recall” is specifically author-scored recovery, not blind model or human decoding.[^4]

### 3.2 Weighted Atom Recall

`WAR = Σa w(a)1[a∈A(z)] / Σa w(a)` over critical atoms.

WAR gives safety or output-contract atoms more weight. The paper’s five-case weights are manually assigned and illustrative. Different granularity changes the denominator: splitting one commitment into five atoms can amplify its influence. An annotation guide and inter-rater agreement are necessary.

### 3.3 Commitment Density

`CD = preserved critical atoms / tokens(z)`.

Density rewards compact faithful packets but is vulnerable to atom fragmentation. If a verbose requirement becomes several tiny atoms, density can improve without preserving more user utility. Reporting utility-weighted density and fixed gold segmentation would reduce gaming.

### 3.4 Recoverability

Decoder `D` recovers an atom set equivalent to `Ac(H,q)` up to explicitly allowed omissions. This is the correct end-to-end metric because the receiver—not the paper author—must interpret the packet. Yet the diagnostic does not run it. The paper specifies an independent-decoder protocol as future work, which should have been the main experiment.

### 3.5 Deployment proxies

Suggested proxies include extractor self-consistency, cross-model agreement, round-trip consistency, schema failures, low-confidence counts, and high-risk coverage. These are useful alarms, not ground truth. They should cause fallback, not certify correctness.

## 4. Context Compression Language and codec pipeline

CCL renders canonical atoms as ASCII key-value forms such as `C={rental_car:false}` and `OUT={codeonly:true}`. CCL-Core preserves readable names; CCL-Min abbreviates aggressively. Canonical storage remains JSON-like and can be validated with JSON Schema. This layered design is important: CCL is serialization, not the semantic model.[^5]

The Budgeted Commitment Codec performs eight steps:

1. Segment system constraints, goals, decisions, artifacts, preferences, safety, and recency regions.
2. Extract atoms with evidence, confidence, and criticality.
3. Normalize aliases, values, negations, counts, scopes, and units.
4. Detect conflicts and supersession.
5. Rank by relevance, recency, specificity, criticality, safety, dependency, and confidence.
6. Encode under budget, preferring CCL-Core and retaining raw evidence for high risk.
7. Decode and verify high-priority atoms, schema, conflicts, and safety coverage.
8. Fall back to raw spans, more budget, JSON, or no compression.

The ranking formula is a checklist rather than a trained or calibrated objective. Term scales, dependencies, and budget optimization are unspecified. A real codec needs an explicit selection problem, for example a constrained submodular or integer program over atom utility, dependency closure, and render cost.

The paper offers falsifiable rejection criteria: weaken CCL if it is not more compact than JSON/YAML, does not preserve more weighted atoms than prose, creates many mutations, decodes worse than structured prose at comparable budgets, or drops more safety atoms. These criteria are valuable, although the current diagnostic does not satisfy the independent-decoder or safety tests.

## 5. Experimental design and evidence reconstructed

Five author-created cases cover an epidemic simulator, React dashboard, Python CSV cleaner, Lisbon trip dialogue, and the paper’s own research state. Each receives 12–15 gold critical atoms and integer weights. Authors write free prose, structured prose, JSON, CCL-Core, and CCL-Min.

The experiment reports full and compressed lexical counts, gain, CAR, and WAR. It does not test model behavior, independent decoding, latency, extraction, user preference, inter-rater agreement, or safety. The cases are short and highly structured, favorable to key-value encodings. Legal, medical, emotional, multilingual, ambiguous, and adversarial contexts are absent.

The error-injection table checks that known removal, mutation, polarity, output-contract, and reset errors lower CAR or create conflict once atoms are normalized. That validates definitions at a trivial level; it does not show that an extractor detects those errors from natural language.

## 6. Results and ablations

Free prose averages 27.6 lexical tokens and 68.3% gain but CAR 0.61 and WAR 0.57. Structured prose averages 66.4 tokens, 24.0% gain, and perfect author-scored CAR/WAR. JSON averages 118.6 tokens, negative 29.3% gain, and perfect recall. CCL-Core averages 71.2 tokens, 21.8% gain, and perfect recall. CCL-Min averages 51.0 tokens, 42.4% gain, CAR 0.94, and WAR 0.93.

Per case, CCL-Core ranges from 13.7% to 27.5% gain. Structured prose ranges from 15.0% to 41.2%. CCL-Min ranges from 36.1% to 48.1% and loses atoms in four cases; the trip case remains perfect, which the authors correctly flag as especially susceptible to circular scoring.

There are no ablations in the machine-learning sense. The format comparison is the ablation: prose removes structure, structured prose adds explicitness, JSON adds standard syntax, CCL-Core changes serialization, and CCL-Min trades names for brevity. No extractor, lexicon, confidence, atom weighting, budget, or decoder is varied.

The aggregate contradiction is decisive. CCL-Core has more tokens and lower gain than structured prose, so it is not “slightly more compact on average.” Its empirical argument must instead rest on diffability, validation, and canonical mapping—none evaluated here.

Tokenizer Table 7 reinforces caution. Epidemic CCL-Core is 115/117 BPE tokens; CCL-Min 74/74. A JSON Patch diff is 74/78, close to or better than the CCL diff examples at 65/65 and 79/78 depending on which state is compared. Full-representation BPE counts are still missing.

## 8. External primary-source vetting

The canonical arXiv record confirms v1, both authors, subject classes, title, and abstract.[^1] No official code or reproducibility repository is linked. JSON Schema’s core and validation specifications support the paper’s choice to keep canonical data separate from compact rendering.[^2]

The same authors released SemanticZip shortly afterward as a more aggressive, LLM-decompressible follow-up. Its canonical record reports independent-decoder experiments and more explicit compression/recovery tradeoffs, making it a direct follow-up rather than independent confirmation.[^3]

No same-paper or directly corresponding DEP-A, DEP-E, or Delphoa-Labs record was verified. Existing memory-compression DEPs are broad conceptual context only.

No official implementation was found either.[^6] That prevents inspection of the lexical counter, normalization code, complete five-case representations, or tokenizer script. The paper prints enough examples to understand the method, but not enough machine-readable material to reproduce Table 4 end to end. A minimal release would include original contexts, every encoding, gold atoms, weights, normalizer, metric code, and full tokenizer counts.

The external evidence also sharpens the prior-work boundary. JSON Schema validates record structure, not semantic truth or source entailment. SemanticZip adds a learned or prompted decoder and reports a larger compression/recoverability trade space, but it shares authors and diagnostic lineage. Neither source independently establishes that commitment atoms cover real production histories.

## 9. Research notes and critical considerations

### 9.1 Commitments should be versioned events

Instead of storing only current atom state, keep an append-only event ledger: asserted, rejected, superseded, scoped, and expired. A materialized compact state can be regenerated while provenance remains recoverable.

### 9.2 Dependencies need closure

Atoms are not independent. “Use ISO dates” may depend on accepted input formats. A budget selector that keeps a child without its qualifiers creates weakening. The codec should encode a dependency graph and select closed subsets.

### 9.3 Verification must include precision and contradiction

Recall alone rewards adding every possible commitment. Required metrics are atom precision, conflict rate, polarity errors, scope errors, unsupported-atom rate, and evidence entailment.

### 9.4 Receiver diversity matters

A notation may decode well for one model family and fail for another, or drift after model upgrades. Versioned CCL grammar, conformance tests, and per-model decoder certification are necessary.

## 10. Limitations, implications, and deployment applicability

The authors acknowledge extractor quality, annotation burden, tokenizer dependence, model familiarity with JSON, and the small author-scored diagnostic. Additional limitations include no released code, no independent annotators, undefined atom granularity, no precision/conflict aggregate, no query distribution, no downstream utility, and no longitudinal update study.

The framework is most applicable to structured agent state, code specifications, project decisions, and workflows with explicit contracts. It is least appropriate as a replacement for nuanced medical, legal, emotional, literary, or safety-critical source text. In those settings, compact atoms should index and accompany raw evidence, not replace it.

Deployment should treat canonical atoms as private structured records with source access controls. Evidence spans can contain personal or confidential data. Deletion and correction must propagate through summaries and derived packets. Compression must never turn “unknown” into an asserted fact.

## 11. Independent re-conceptualization and falsifiable hypotheses

### Hypothesis 1: versioned atom graphs outperform flat packets

On multi-turn tasks with reversals and supersession, a versioned dependency graph should reduce outdated-decision errors by at least half versus flat CCL at equal token budget.

### Hypothesis 2: structured prose wins for unfamiliar receivers

Across five model families, structured prose should have higher atom recall than CCL-Core before demonstrations, while CCL closes the gap after a short grammar primer. Falsify if CCL wins zero-shot consistently.

### Hypothesis 3: BPE-aware rendering changes the format frontier

Optimize field names and punctuation for target tokenizers. JSON/JSONL should narrow or reverse lexical-proxy gaps on short records. Falsify if CCL keeps a >15% advantage across full cases and tokenizers.

### Hypothesis 4: dependency-closed selection improves safety

At fixed tokens, selecting atom dependency closures should reduce weakening and scope errors versus independent ranking, even if raw CAR falls slightly.

## 12. Replication and falsification agenda

1. Release all five full source and compressed representations plus gold atom schemas.
2. Correct the structured-prose versus CCL-Core aggregate statement.
3. Count complete cases with at least two current tokenizers.
4. Recruit independent annotators and report agreement on atom boundaries, equivalence, conflict, and weights.
5. Run blind independent decoders across multiple model families.
6. Report precision, recall, WAR, conflict, polarity, scope, unsupported atoms, and evidence entailment.
7. Generate downstream answers and measure contract satisfaction.
8. Add multilingual, ambiguous, adversarial, legal, medical, safety, and tool-agent histories.
9. Sweep budgets and plot Pareto frontiers for structured prose, JSON, CCL-Core, CCL-Min, and raw retrieval.
10. Test update, supersession, deletion, and provenance over hundreds of turns.

The CCL hypothesis should be rejected if it fails to beat structured prose or JSON on recoverability at matched BPE budget, or if safety-critical atom errors rise despite fallback rules.

## 13. Durable restatement

Context compression for agents should preserve typed, sourced, versioned commitments rather than merely similar prose. Extraction, normalization, canonical storage, compact rendering, and independent verification are distinct stages. A compact notation is useful only if diverse receivers recover the same obligations and contradictions under measured token budgets. Context Codec provides the right vocabulary and fail-closed posture; its current CCL evidence is a self-scored design probe, not a benchmark result.
