# SpectrumKV Finds the Right Design Space with the Wrong Headline Baseline

## A full-paper review and independent re-conceptualization of “SpectrumKV: Per-Token Mixed-Precision KV Cache Transfer for Prefill–Decode Disaggregated LLM Serving”

**Paper:** Yang Pengju, arXiv:2606.08635v1, 7 June 2026.[^1]
**Scope:** complete PDF and full-paper HTML, TeX source, all main and appendix sections, equations, lemmas, proposition, conjecture, pipeline, every table and figure, raw numerical appendices, bug log, limitations, and primary-source vetting.
**Review activity:** full-source reconstruction, budget/algebra audit, result and hardware consistency review, and public-artifact search. No model, quantizer, GPU timing, network transfer, or NIAH trial was independently reproduced.

---

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

SpectrumKV correctly reframes disaggregated KV transfer as a precision-allocation problem and openly records important implementation corrections, but its main 50% result is exactly uniform INT8 rather than per-token allocation, its adaptive low-budget policy drops tokens despite the “retain every token” story, and its hardware and softmax-theory claims contain material defects.

### Durable contribution

The durable insight is that PD disaggregation turns the cache into a network representation, so keep/drop is not the only choice. A token may be transmitted as FP16, INT8, INT4, or—if unavoidable—not transmitted. Importance and model tolerance can determine fidelity. This design space is broader than eviction and composes with placement, lossless compression, and per-channel quantization.

The paper is also unusually explicit about negative evidence. It says Qwen catastrophically fails under INT4, reports that random tier assignment can match or beat the proposed ranking, marks obsolete measurements after fixing five implementation bugs, states that the latency test is not a production PD runtime, and identifies missing state-of-the-art baselines. Those disclosures materially improve the reviewability of a single-author preprint.

### Bottom-line judgment

The strongest empirical fact is narrower than the branding. At normalized budget 0.5, every token is INT8 under SpectrumKV Greedy and Uniform-INT8, so the methods are identical. Reported PPL changes are +1.97% on Qwen, −0.06% on Mistral, and −0.44% on Gemma, while binary PDTrim degrades 25.85%, 22.07%, and 35.63%. This establishes that uniform INT8 is much better than deleting half the positions for these models and test data. It does not establish that importance-ranked per-token precision is responsible.

Below budget 0.5, genuine tiering appears, but the ranking signal is weak: random assignment is sometimes better than Greedy on Mistral. Qwen cannot tolerate INT4, so an FP16+INT8 policy at budget 0.3 cannot retain all tokens—the cheapest retained tier costs 0.5. The paper eventually acknowledges that 40% or more must be dropped, contradicting repeated statements that every token retains an approximate representation.

The NIAH probe is useful as a safety circuit, but it is only three trials from the same constructed retrieval family used for evaluation. Qwen 2-tier reaches 52.6% at budget 0.3 versus PDTrim 26.3%, while Mistral/Gemma report 100%. With only 19 insertion depths, these percentages are coarse and highly protocol dependent.

The latency headline is not end-to-end serving evidence. The paper reports 50–62% TTFT reductions but later says measurements isolate the transfer path and omit scheduler, network contention, placement, continuous batching, and cold fetches. More seriously, it names “RTX 4080 SUPER 32GB” and “RTX 4090 48GB”; standard NVIDIA specifications are 16 GB and 24 GB respectively.[^2] Unless the author used nonstandard modified/cloud-labeled devices, the hardware record is impossible as written.

The theoretical section should be revised. The elementary value-error and tail-mass bounds are sound under their assumptions. The low-entropy softmax “amplification” lemma and layer-propagation proposition are not. Their proofs do not derive the displayed expressions, use uncontrolled approximations, and omit the complete Transformer Jacobian. They should be labeled hypotheses, not formal results.

## 1. Problem framing and scope

Prefill/decode disaggregation computes prompt KV on one worker and transfers it to another. Full FP16 payload is `2 L H_kv d_h n s16` bytes: keys and values for every layer, head, dimension, and token. SpectrumKV normalizes cost per element as FP16=1, INT8=0.5, INT4=0.25.

A budget `b` is average cost over tokens. Binary selection fits the same accounting by assigning retained tokens cost one and dropped tokens zero. Mixed precision instead assigns a quantized vector to a token, preserving approximate contribution.

The paper covers transfer representation, not cache capacity during subsequent decoding in a fully integrated runtime. It assumes per-token quantization at prefill, transfer of quantized payload plus a precision map, and dequantization before attention on the decode worker.

## 2. Technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Value approximation and deletion

For attention output `y=Σp_j v_j`, quantized values `v_j+e_j` with norm error bounded by `δ(q_j)` yield:

`||y−ỹ|| ≤ Σ p_j δ(q_j)`.

This is a valid triangle-inequality bound if probabilities are treated as fixed. It ignores key quantization changing those probabilities. Its useful message is that precision should track attention mass.

For deletion with removed mass `ε`, renormalized retained output differs by at most `2εV` when value norms are bounded by `V`. This lemma is correct. It does not say mixed precision always dominates: quantization changes keys and all downstream layers, while a well-chosen dropped tail may have negligible mass.

### 2.2 Sorted assignment

Given scalar importance `I_j` and tier perturbations, an exchange argument proves higher-importance tokens should not receive worse precision for fixed tier counts. The proof is correct for the separable surrogate `ΣI_jδ(q_j)`. It does not validate the chosen importance, independent token errors, or tier counts.

Actual importance is `I_j=A_j exp(−λΔ_j)`, combining observed/approximated attention with recency. Sinks are optionally pinned. Qwen may fall back to KV norms. Aggregate scoring mixes layers and heads, which the random-vs-Greedy ablation shows can miss functional positions.

### 2.3 Budget mapping

For three tiers and `0.25≤b≤0.5`, fractions are `f8=4b−1`, `f4=2−4b`, `f16=0`. At `b=0.3`, this means 20% INT8 and 80% INT4. For `0.5≤b≤1`, fractions are `f16=2b−1`, `f8=2−2b`.

At exactly 0.5, all tokens are INT8. At exactly 0.25, all tokens are INT4; the appendix later says Greedy uses 20% INT8 at 0.25, contradicting the formula and budget arithmetic. A 20/80 INT8/INT4 mixture costs `0.2×0.5+0.8×0.25=0.3`, not 0.25.

For a two-tier FP16/INT8 policy, no all-token assignment can reach `b<0.5`. The policy must introduce a zero-cost dropped tier, violating the method’s advertised distinction from selection. The paper admits this in the Qwen probe ablation but does not update the formal policy or headline claims.

### 2.4 INT4 probe

Three aggressive NIAH trials at `b=0.3` enable INT4 if at least two succeed. Qwen fails 0/3; Mistral and Gemma pass 3/3. This is a practical smoke test, not a robust tolerance characterization. It can miss code, continuation, multilingual, or domain-specific failure, as the paper acknowledges.

### 2.5 Key perturbation and softmax theory

If every logit perturbation is bounded by `η`, probabilities lie within multiplicative factors `e^(±2η)`. This standard bound is distribution independent.

The later lemma defines a near-max set `S` and claims low entropy amplifies error roughly as `e^(2η)/|S|`. The proof introduces an invalid expression for the perturbed partition function, then replaces it with an approximation. The displayed bound is not derived from those steps. Low entropy can make a wrong top key consequential, but the universal softmax Jacobian does not yield the paper’s claimed monotonic `1/|S|` law without stronger margin and value assumptions.

The layer-wise proposition multiplies `(1+Λ_k α_k)` factors but omits MLPs, residual normalization details, query/key/value projections, nonlinear activation, and the dependence of later caches on earlier perturbations. `α_k` is borrowed from the invalid lemma. This is intuition, not a Transformer error bound.

## 3. Architecture and pipeline

Figures 1 and 2 show prefill scoring, one-time probe, tier map, per-tier quantization, transfer, dequantization, and decode attention. The cold-upgrade idea is attractive: if INT4 is already present, upgrading to FP16 transfers 1.75 additional bytes per element rather than two.

No cold-fetch experiment implements that path. The approximate KV is available, but fetching a residual or replacement also requires identifiers, synchronization, scheduling, and deciding that a cold token matters. The paper’s arithmetic excludes metadata and quantization scales/zero points, so normalized budgets are idealized payload costs.

## 4. Experimental design

Models are Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3, and Gemma-2-9B-it; Qwen2.5-14B appears only in locality characterization. WikiText-2 measures PPL. A constructed 4,096-token NIAH test uses 19 depths. Small code and science scans test domain robustness.

Baselines are PDTrim, SWS, uniform quantization, random tiering, and internal variants. Missing comparisons include KIVI/KVQuant-style quantizers, production PD systems, lossless compression, reuse, and layer-aware placement. At equal bit budget, a tuned uniform/per-channel quantizer is the essential baseline.

The reported software is Python 3.12, PyTorch 2.11.0+cu130, and Transformers 5.9.0. No code repository, environment lock, seeds for deterministic policies, model revisions, quantization kernel description, group size, scale format, or calibration protocol is publicly linked by arXiv.

The source’s bug log records fixes to attention hooks, deletion masking, the NIAH question, adaptive control flow, and Gemma chat templating. This transparency is commendable. It also means raw logs before the final correction are unreliable; exact scripts and JSON hashes are necessary.

## 5. Results

### 5.1 Perplexity

At `b=0.5`, all-INT8 SpectrumKV is close to FP16 on all three models. PDTrim and SWS are much worse. At `b=0.3`, Mistral Greedy is +5.41% PPL and Gemma +2.45%; Qwen three-tier is unsafe. Across 2K–8K, Mistral remains within 0.3% at `b=0.5`, Qwen degrades 1.97–2.90%, and Gemma 8K is missing.

Negative deltas are correctly treated as noise or perturbation effects, not improvement. Raw absolute PPL tables are provided in the appendix.

The small domain scan is less favorable: SpectrumKV PPL change reaches +33.9% for Mistral science, +32.7% Gemma science, and +30.5% Qwen science. It still beats PDTrim but is not near-lossless outside WikiText.

### 5.2 Retrieval

Qwen adaptive two-tier reports NIAH 52.6/74.7/100/100% for budgets 0.3/0.4/0.5/0.7. Because the low-budget variant drops tokens, its superiority over PDTrim reflects a different selection strategy plus quantization, not preservation of every token.

Mistral Greedy is a constant 89.5% across budgets while an “adaptive verification” line is 100% everywhere. The distinction is not operationally defined well enough to reproduce. Gemma is 100% in all displayed mixed-precision cells. With 19 trials, 89.5% corresponds to 17/19 and 52.6% to 10/19; confidence intervals would be wide.

The depth heatmap shows the intended sink-and-recency U-shape for Qwen. It appears manually encoded in the TeX source rather than generated from machine data, increasing transcription risk.

### 5.3 Latency

Reported `b=0.5` timing reduces “TTFT” 50–62% at 2K–8K. Decode throughput ranges from −11% to +33%. These results are labeled end-to-end in the table but explicitly described as transfer-path evidence without a networked PD scheduler. The apparent contradiction should be resolved by renaming the metric and publishing the timing harness.

The stated GPU memory capacities make the hardware record unreliable. Exact cloud instance type, `nvidia-smi`, device UUID/model, power mode, and raw timing logs are required.

## 6. Ablations

Sink pinning changes PPL by less than 0.6 points in tested cells. Balanced tiering causes Qwen +7,506.84% PPL, demonstrating INT4 intolerance rather than a general ranking benefit.

Random tiering matches or beats Greedy in several Mistral low-budget cells. The paper appropriately concludes aggregate importance is imperfect. At 0.5 ranking is mathematically irrelevant because all tokens are INT8.

The probe is the most consequential component: Qwen NIAH rises from 4.2% three-tier to 52.6% adaptive. But this also changes the action space to include dropping, so it is not a clean probe-only ablation.

PDTrim’s first/last ratio varies widely by model. The baseline’s main table uses a fixed ratio that is not optimal for every model, weakening fairness.

## 8. Code, data, and reproducibility status

No official repository or raw JSON log was found.[^5] Reproduction needs final corrected scripts, commit hash, quantizer definition, scale metadata, exact attention hooks, model revisions, prompts, WikiText split, NIAH generator, seed list, obsolete-log manifest, and timing environment.

Unit tests should enforce budget arithmetic, verify no dropped tokens when that is claimed, compare uniform INT8 equivalence at 0.5, and reproduce every fractional percentage from integer NIAH counts.

### Quantization semantics that must be pinned

“INT8” and “INT4” do not uniquely identify a representation. Reproduction needs to know whether keys and values are quantized per tensor, token, head, channel, or group; whether scales are symmetric or asymmetric; whether zero points exist; the group width; clipping policy; rounding; accumulation precision; and whether RoPE is applied before quantization. Scale and zero-point bytes must enter the payload budget. For small groups, metadata can materially raise the effective cost above the ideal 0.5 or 0.25 ratios.

Keys and values should not automatically share a tier. Key error changes attention probabilities, while value error perturbs a weighted sum. A two-dimensional policy could protect keys at higher precision and compress values more aggressively. The paper’s own Qwen result suggests key sensitivity dominates, but no K-only/V-only ablation establishes that mechanism.

### Baseline conditioning

PDTrim’s first/last ratio is shown to have different optima by model, yet headline tables do not always use each optimum. A fair comparison must either tune on a held-out set for every model/budget or report an oracle envelope. Selection methods should also be allowed the same sink protection and recency information. Uniform INT8 is mandatory at 0.5 because it is exactly the proposed assignment; at other budgets, mixtures with random, position-aware, and learned allocation separate quantizer quality from ranking quality.

### Statistical unit

WikiText perplexity aggregates many tokens, but NIAH uses only 19 depths and the probe only three trials. Reporting percentages to one decimal creates false precision. Each retrieval cell should include integer successes, binomial intervals, multiple needle strings, prompt templates, insertion seeds, and exact decoding rules. Probe trials must be disjoint from evaluation trials to avoid selecting a policy on the test.

## 9. Independent re-conceptualization

SpectrumKV should become a **four-action rate allocator**: FP16, INT8, INT4, and absent. The absent action must be explicit because model intolerance and budgets below the cheapest safe tier require it. Allocation should minimize estimated downstream loss under payload, scale-metadata, and latency constraints.

Instead of a binary three-trial probe, learn a model/workload tolerance profile across layers, heads, keys, values, and tasks. A safe allocator can reserve precision for fragile early layers or high-margin keys and use calibrated uncertainty to fall back.

### Progressive residual coding

Cold upgrades become cleaner if low tiers are prefixes of a progressive code. INT4 would carry the high-order information and an upgrade would send only residual bits that exactly reconstruct INT8 or FP16. Standard independent quantizers do not guarantee that subtracting 1.75 bytes recreates the full vector; sending the difference in nominal bit width is only arithmetic unless the formats are nested. A production design should define the codec, residual payload, reconstruction error, and cache-coherence protocol.

### Versioned tolerance certificates

Probe outcomes should be stored with model hash, tokenizer, attention implementation, RoPE settings, quantization kernel, driver, and workload suite. Any change invalidates the certificate. Rather than “INT4 safe,” the certificate should report a tolerated error envelope by layer/task/budget and an abstention policy when live telemetry drifts.

## 10. Implications

The paper convincingly shows that deletion is an unnecessarily harsh baseline when INT8 KV is almost lossless. Serving systems should expose precision as a first-class placement dimension. The negative result is equally important: 4-bit tolerance is model dependent, and vector cosine is not a safety metric.

The work does not yet show production goodput. Network compression, quantization kernels, continuous batching, and cache upgrades interact with scheduler behavior. A representation win can disappear if conversion overhead or metadata dominates.

## 11. Research notes, limitations, and deployment applicability

Applicable deployments are network-bound PD systems with stable model revisions and repeated workloads that justify profiling. Every model update, RoPE change, attention kernel, or fine-tune can invalidate tolerance decisions. Certification must be versioned.

Additional limitations are small model/task coverage, constructed NIAH, impossible hardware labels, absent code, manual plot data, incomplete baseline tuning, invalid formal results, and no security analysis. Quantized KV may still leak prompt content; lower precision is not privacy protection.

## 12. Falsifiable hypotheses

1. Tuned uniform INT8 will reproduce all `b=0.5` SpectrumKV results exactly.
2. Per-head/per-layer ranking will beat random tiering below 0.5 on Mistral and Gemma.
3. A workload-diverse probe will predict INT4 failures better than three NIAH trials.
4. Explicit four-action allocation will dominate the hidden-drop adaptive policy at matched budgets.
5. Production networked goodput gains will be smaller than isolated transfer-path “TTFT” reductions.

## Replication and falsification agenda

Release corrected code/logs and reproduce PPL, NIAH, timing, and raw appendix tables on standard 24/16 GB device variants or accurately named cloud GPUs. Compare uniform INT8/INT4, KIVI, KVQuant, PDTrim tuned per model, SWS, lossless compression, and mixed precision under actual byte accounting including scales and maps.

Integrate with a networked PD runtime; report p50/p95/p99 TTFT, inter-token latency, throughput, goodput, quantize/dequantize time, bytes, energy, and cold-upgrade frequency. Evaluate code, science, multilingual, agents, and 70B models. Replace or formally prove the softmax/layer claims.

## 13. Durable restatement

SpectrumKV shows that KV transfer should allow intermediate precision rather than only retain/drop. Uniform INT8 already preserves far more quality than deleting half the cache on the tested models. INT4 can add savings for tolerant models, while Qwen requires fallback.

The current record does not establish that per-token importance is the source of gains, that every token is retained, or that production TTFT falls by the headline amount. The next version should correct hardware, budget, and theory defects and release the final bug-fixed implementation.
