# Video memory as a searchable pyramid rather than a frame ledger

## A detailed review, technical reconstruction, and independent re-conceptualization of “MuKV: Multi-Grained KV Cache Compression for Long Streaming Video Question-Answering”

**Source paper:** Junbin Xiao, Jiajun Chen, Tianxiang Sun, Xun Yang, and Angela Yao, “MuKV: Multi-Grained KV Cache Compression for Long Streaming Video Question-Answering,” arXiv:2605.22269v1, 2026.[^source]

**Artifact prepared:** 2026-07-15  
**Local source reviewed:** complete 14-page PDF and matching full-paper HTML, including the post-reference dataset and sensitivity appendices  
**Review scope:** full method and experiment reconstruction, quantitative and claim audit, independent re-conceptualization, and replication plan  
**Review status:** paper and public arXiv record inspected; no official runnable MuKV repository was verified and no experiment was reproduced

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

MuKV stores streaming-video transformer keys and values at patch, frame, and segment resolutions, compresses each resolution with a combined attention/frequency score, and retrieves them through a coarse-to-fine query procedure intended to preserve both local visual evidence and long temporal context.

### The deeper idea

The paper’s lasting idea is to replace a chronological frame ledger with a multiresolution evidence pyramid. A stream does not need equal-fidelity storage everywhere: objects and small regions require patch detail, stable appearances can be represented at frame level, and events or actions require segment context. MuKV makes those levels explicit in the cache and postpones the final fidelity choice until a question arrives. Its compression and retrieval stages are therefore coupled: compression decides what evidence can ever be recalled; retrieval decides which resolution should answer this particular query.

### Bottom-line judgment

The paper presents a coherent architecture and broad within-paper evaluation. It compares streaming accuracy, online latency, and cache storage; ablates granularity, compression, and retrieval; reports deployment-oriented seconds-per-question and gigabytes-per-hour; and extends analysis to offline long-video QA, retention ratios, retrieval mixing, and sampling rates. The evidence supports that the proposed multigrain representation is useful under the tested LLaVA-OV configurations and benchmarks. The strongest limitation is attribution: MuKV changes representation, pruning, and retrieval together, while some headline gains depend on an LLM judge whose deprecated version was replaced. The experiments do not prove that frequency is a general salience measure, that stored KV remains stable for arbitrarily long streams, or that the same trade-off holds for other multimodal backbones and production video distributions.

### Principal strengths

- Patch, frame, and segment caches address distinct spatial and temporal failure modes rather than applying one undifferentiated token budget.
- The dual-signal compressor is lightweight enough to be available during prefill and is evaluated separately from the complete system.
- The semi-hierarchical retriever recognizes that searching every token across every granularity in one flat ranking can amplify cross-resolution noise.
- The evaluation covers three streaming benchmarks, two model scales, controlled storage/latency measures, component ablations, and extensive appendices.

### Principal qualifications

1. Results use LLaVA-OV 0.5B and 7B, with major experiments on A5000 GPUs; cross-backbone evidence is limited.
2. RVSEgo/RVSMovie answers use an LLM judge, and a GPT-3.5-turbo version change makes copied baseline values and new scores imperfectly comparable.
3. Some hyperparameters are greedily selected on RVSEgo and transferred, so RVSEgo is not a fully untouched evaluation distribution.
4. Compression quality is evaluated through downstream questions; unretrieved but important events, rare-object loss, and calibration of retrieval confidence remain weakly observed.
5. No independent code execution or result reproduction was performed.

## 1. The problem the paper is actually solving

### 1.1 Problem and stakes

Streaming video question answering differs from offline video QA because the stream may not have a known endpoint, questions arrive at different times, and each answer may depend on any earlier moment. A system that repeatedly feeds all observed frames to a multimodal model eventually exceeds its context and incurs growing prefill cost. A text-memory or caption agent can reduce storage but requires online language-model work and may discard visual details before a future question reveals their importance. A KV-memory system performs visual encoding and transformer prefill offline, then retrieves cached keys and values for later questions, reducing online computation.

The baseline weakness identified by MuKV is not just “too many tokens.” Per-frame caches overrepresent redundant frames while underrepresenting two kinds of structure: small spatial details within a frame and temporal relations spanning multiple frames. Uniform sampling can miss both. The target is a memory that keeps enough patch, frame, and segment evidence to answer future questions while maintaining bounded storage and low online latency.

### 1.2 Prior method families

The paper groups approaches into end-to-end long-context models, Socratic/agentic memories, and KV-cache memories. End-to-end systems trade large context and computation for direct access to visual tokens. Agentic systems store descriptions or embeddings but perform substantial model work online. ReKV and related KV approaches amortize past visual computation but use principally frame-level storage and flat retrieval.

For KV compression, the paper distinguishes quantization and pruning. It chooses pruning and notes that attention and similarity are common importance signals. MuKV adds a frequency-domain signal, arguing that recurring or structured spatial/temporal patterns can reveal salience not captured by self-attention alone. For retrieval, it contrasts flat parallel ranking with hierarchical search.

### 1.3 The proposed gap

MuKV claims that a single temporal resolution cannot simultaneously preserve region detail, frame semantics, and cross-frame dynamics. Its answer is not merely a bigger cache. It encodes each arriving segment in three forms, compresses them separately, and retrieves with an initial granularity-aware screen followed by cross-grain adjustment. This makes information resolution an explicit decision variable.

## 2. Formal and technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Definitions and assumptions

Let a video stream contain frames arriving through time and let questions (q_i) occur at timestamps (t_i). Only video observed up to (t_i) may be used. The system groups sampled frames into segments. For each segment it derives patch-level, frame-level, and segment-level token sequences, runs them through the multimodal language model, and stores compressed KV tensors at all transformer layers.

The formulation assumes questions are unknown during offline storage, so compression is query-agnostic. It assumes the visual backbone and language model produce reusable keys/values whose semantics remain comparable across segments, and that pooling key vectors yields a useful retrieval embedding.

### 2.2 Multi-grained representation

At a given segment, the patch representation preserves local regions, the frame representation summarizes a representative middle frame, and the segment representation encodes a short sequence of continuous frames. The paper’s default stream sampling is 0.5 FPS, with four sampled frames spanning eight seconds per segment. Granularity-specific retention ratios allocate different budgets because the informational roles differ.

This design multiplies candidate memory before compression. It is beneficial only if pruning removes enough redundancy and retrieval can prevent irrelevant cross-grain tokens from flooding the answer context.

### 2.3 Dual-signal compression

For each granularity, MuKV computes an attention importance vector from last-layer self-attention and a frequency signal using a Fourier transform over token features. The attention term is averaged over heads and relevant positions. The frequency tensor is reduced to a token-level score. After normalization, a convex combination forms the final importance:

\[
I_t = \alpha_t\widehat I_{att} + (1-\alpha_t)\widehat I_{fft}.
\]

Tokens with higher combined importance are retained according to the granularity budget. The exact semantic interpretation of “frequency” changes with the token axis and representation. It is best understood as a structured-variation proxy, not a proof of temporal importance. The paper’s frequency ablation supports usefulness but does not isolate why the signal works.

### 2.4 Semi-hierarchical retrieval

MuKV pools stored key tokens to produce retrieval vectors and pools question keys to obtain a query vector. The first stage performs retrieval within each granularity, preserving the three specialized candidate pools. A second stage adjusts candidates across grains, combining an initial similarity score (s_j) with a cross-grain term \(\gamma_j\):

\[
\widetilde s_j=(1-\lambda_g)s_j+\lambda_g\gamma_j.
\]

The “semi” qualifier is apt: the procedure is not a strict tree in which coarse retrieval deterministically gates fine nodes. It first retrieves in parallel within levels, then uses cross-level evidence to re-rank or refine. That protects specialization while permitting a question to shift the final mix.

### 2.5 Training process

MuKV is largely an inference/memory architecture built on pretrained LLaVA-OV. The paper does not present a new end-to-end model-training objective. Hyperparameters—sampling, segment size, granularity budgets, attention/frequency mixing, and retrieval weights—are selected empirically. This lowers training cost but moves burden to configuration and benchmark tuning.

### 2.6 Inference process

Offline, video segments are sampled, transformed into three granularities, prefetched through the model, scored, pruned, and appended to separate cache memories. Online, a question is encoded, timestamp eligibility is enforced, candidates are retrieved within each granularity, cross-grain scores adjust the portfolio, and selected KV tensors are supplied to autoregressive answer generation. Later questions repeat retrieval against the enlarged memory without recomputing all earlier video features.

### 2.7 Notation and implementation audit

The paper’s equations define normalized signal fusion and pooled key/query retrieval clearly. However, reproducibility depends on details not recoverable from equations alone: which layer supplies retrieval keys, normalization across segments, tie breaking, per-grain top-k allocation, cache layout, batching, and how selected KV from different segments is positionally composed. Without public code execution these remain paper-reported.

## 3. Architecture and information flow

### 3.1 Representation

The memory is a time-indexed set of three synchronized but differently compressed KV streams. Patch caches emphasize local objects, frame caches offer economical appearance summaries, and segment caches represent motion/event context. The architecture is closer to a multiresolution database than a rolling context window.

### 3.2 Decision modules

There are two irreversible decisions. DCP decides which evidence is never stored. Semi-hierarchical retrieval decides which stored evidence is exposed for a question. Errors compound: retrieval cannot recover pruned evidence, and perfect compression is wasted if retrieval selects the wrong granularity.

### 3.3 Dependencies and post-processing

The system depends on LLaVA-OV, stream sampling, segment boundaries, per-grain tokenization, transformer KV compatibility, and an autoregressive decoder. The answer judge is external to the deployed architecture but material to evaluation.

## 4. The complete operational pipeline

### 4.1 End-to-end flow

Frames arrive and are sampled. Four-frame chunks form segments. Each chunk yields patch, middle-frame, and segment encodings. Transformer prefill creates KV tensors; DCP scores and retains tokens; metadata records time and granularity. When a question arrives, its representation queries eligible caches, first within each grain and then across grains. Retrieved tensors plus generated tokens drive answer decoding.

### 4.2 Resource allocation

Storage is reported as memory tokens and as gigabytes per hour; online cost is inference visual tokens and seconds per question. Reporting per 300 frames/ten minutes in the main table and per 30 frames/minute in offline appendices requires care when comparing. The paper’s sensitivity results favor more segment retention, indicating that the optimal budget is not uniform.

### 4.3 Guarantee boundary

Timestamp filtering enforces a causal availability constraint. It does not guarantee full coverage of past events, correct retrieval, or faithful answers. Compression and retrieval are approximate, and LLM judging introduces another layer of uncertainty.

## 5. Independent re-conceptualization

### 5.1 A resolution-on-demand evidence store

MuKV can be understood as storing evidence at several resolutions before knowing the query, then purchasing resolution at retrieval time. A spatial-detail query should draw patches; a scene query may need frames; an event query needs segments.

**Boundary of the analogy:** unlike an image pyramid, the three representations are generated by a multimodal transformer and are not lossless downsamplings of one common signal. Cross-level consistency is learned or emergent rather than guaranteed.

### 5.2 Two-stage lossy memory

The design is a composition of write-time loss and read-time selection. This explains why evaluating only cache size or only retrieval recall is insufficient. The correct unit is end-to-end answerability under a fixed storage and latency budget.

### 5.3 Transferable design principle

For open-ended future queries, diversify stored abstractions and delay the final fidelity decision, but instrument both irrecoverable write loss and recoverable read-selection error separately.

## 6. Experimental design reconstructed

### 6.1 Data

The main streaming benchmarks are RVSEgo (about 1.4K questions over eleven roughly thirty-minute egocentric videos), RVSMovie (about 1.9K questions over twenty roughly one-hour movies), and a StreamingBench subset drawn from about 4.5K questions over 900 videos. Appendices evaluate offline long-video benchmarks and analyze answer-span distributions. These datasets differ materially: RVSEgo has longer relative answer spans, making uniform sampling less redundant than on RVSMovie.

### 6.2 Models and hardware

MuKV is instantiated with LLaVA-OV 0.5B and 7B. Major experiments use NVIDIA A5000 24 GB GPUs. Default sampling is 0.5 FPS and four sampled frames per segment. Hyperparameters are greedily searched on RVSEgo and then reused.

### 6.3 Baselines

Comparisons include end-to-end and memory methods such as LongVA, FVStream, and ReKV. InfinitPot-V appears in a controlled KV-compression comparison. Some baseline numbers are copied from prior work, and the deprecated GPT-3.5-turbo-0613 judge is greyed where it prevents a direct comparison with newly judged results.

### 6.4 Metrics

Accuracy is judged by GPT-3.5-turbo for RVSEgo/RVSMovie and by standard multiple-choice accuracy for StreamingBench. Efficiency includes inference visual tokens, memory tokens, seconds per question, and cache gigabytes per hour. These metrics are complementary; none alone defines a deployment optimum.

### 6.5 Runtime semantics

“Online time” includes retrieval and answer generation, while offline cache construction is amortized. A fair service comparison must state video ingestion rate, question arrival rate, batch size, storage horizon, and whether offline compute keeps pace with real time. The paper provides useful point measurements but not a full cost model.

## 7. Results: what is reported and what it means

### 7.1 Streaming performance

Table 1 reports MuKV as consistently stronger than recent competitors on RVSEgo and StreamingBench, while FVStream leads RVSMovie but generalizes poorly to StreamingBench. Relative to ReKV, MuKV improves answer accuracy without increasing the reported online QA time and offline storage. Figure 1 indicates that the advantage grows as streams lengthen.

**Interpretation:** structured multigrain memory becomes more valuable as a flat per-frame ledger accumulates redundancy.

**Qualification:** judge-version differences and multiple simultaneous architectural changes limit precise attribution.

### 7.2 Category-level behavior

On StreamingBench, MuKV improves higher-level categories such as causal reasoning, clip summarization, event understanding, and prospective reasoning, but is weaker on counting. This pattern fits the design: segment representations preserve global semantics, while pruning repeated instances can damage exact enumeration.

### 7.3 Compression and deployment

The DCP ablation compares half-rate frame sampling, random 50% dropping, and attention/frequency pruning. The paper reports that combined signals improve accuracy, memory, and QA efficiency. Tables 4 and 5 ground the analysis in seconds per question and gigabytes per hour and compare an existing video KV compressor under controlled conditions.

### 7.4 Generalization and sensitivity

Appendix results extend to offline long-video QA and alternative backbones. Granularity sensitivity shows higher segment retention improves performance. Retrieval-weight sensitivity differs by dataset: smaller cross-grain dependence can help RVSEgo but hurt RVS-Movie. That is evidence against a universal retrieval mixture.

## 8. Ablations and causal evidence

### 8.1 Granularity

Table 2 compares granularity variants under parallel retrieval and equal online efficiency. The full multigrain configuration performs best, supporting complementarity. Stronger evidence would equalize stored bytes, not only online retrieval cost, because representations may differ in KV footprint.

### 8.2 Compression signal

Random dropping and half-rate sampling are useful controls. The frequency-only/high-versus-low pruning analysis and signal distributions make the DCP story plausible. Missing controls include shuffled frequency axes and frequency computed after temporally permuting frames.

### 8.3 Retrieval

Table 7 compares retrieval strategies, while Table 11 sweeps the cross-grain weight. These show that hierarchical structure contributes beyond storage. Recall of answer-bearing spans would make the causal link clearer than answer accuracy alone.

### 8.4 Randomness and statistics

The paper does not foreground multiple seeds, confidence intervals, or judge agreement. Reported differences should be treated as point estimates. Video/question correlation also means questions are not necessarily independent statistical units.[^statistics]

## 10. External views and field context

### 10.1 Predecessor evidence

The paper positions ReKV as the closest per-frame KV-memory baseline and InfinitPot-V as a compression comparator. The reviewed evidence supports a different representation/retrieval design; it does not prove all per-frame memories are inferior.

### 10.2 Benchmark perspective

StreamingBench, RVSEgo, and RVSMovie emphasize different temporal and answer types. Cross-dataset variation in retrieval weight and category results is a useful warning that benchmark averages should not be collapsed into one universal setting.[^benchmark]

### 10.3 Contemporaneous alternatives

Alternatives include long-context end-to-end MLLMs, textual episodic memories, embedding stores, and quantized KV memory. They trade online compute, storage, visual fidelity, and recoverability differently. A complete comparison should use a common ingest/question workload.

### 10.4 Publication, code, and reproducibility status

The primary source is an arXiv v1 preprint. No official repository or immutable implementation revision was verified through the paper record during this review. Consequently, artifact availability is unresolved and result reproduction was not attempted.[^code]

## 11. Research notes and critical considerations

### 11.1 Internal consistency

Main claims align with the ablations and appendices. The paper clearly marks deprecated-judge baseline values, which is good practice. The title’s “compression” covers both write-time pruning and read-time retrieval, so readers should avoid attributing full-system gains to DCP alone.

### 11.2 Metric and baseline concerns

LLM judging can drift and introduce preference bias. Storage in tokens does not always equal bytes across layers/dtypes, while G/h is hardware/configuration-specific. Copied baselines may use different inference libraries or judging versions.

### 11.3 Unsupported or overbroad claims

“Without sacrificing” should be scoped to reported metrics and comparators. Infinite or unconstrained streams remain untested. Efficiency excludes some offline and operational costs.

### 11.4 Alternative explanations

Gains may come from increased representational diversity or total candidate tokens rather than hierarchical semantics. Segment tokens may simply align better with benchmark answer spans. Retrieval may act as regularization by limiting distracting frame candidates.

### 11.5 Unresolved questions

- How fast does cache storage grow over days, and when should old evidence be consolidated or deleted?
- Can the system estimate that required evidence was pruned and abstain?
- How do retrieval errors and compression errors contribute separately?
- Does frequency scoring preserve rare safety-critical events or preferentially delete them?

## 12. Potential implications

### 12.1 Scientific implications

Long-video memory should be evaluated as a representation-and-retrieval problem, not just context extension. Multiresolution state may offer a better inductive bias than uniformly sampled tokens.

### 12.2 System-design implications

Deployments need storage budgets per granularity, horizon policies, event-level retrieval telemetry, and replayable provenance connecting answers to source timestamps and retained patches.

### 12.3 Broader implications

The resolution-on-demand pattern may transfer to multimodal agent memory, robotics logs, and sensor archives. Transfer remains hypothetical until tested with non-video state and action consequences.

## 13. New hypotheses derived from the paper

These are reviewer-derived proposals.

### Hypothesis 1: optimal grain is predictable from question type

**Proposition:** query intent predicts the useful patch/frame/segment allocation better than a fixed mixture.  
**Predicted observation:** a calibrated query router reduces retrieved tokens at equal accuracy.  
**Falsifying observation:** adaptive routing performs no better than the fixed semi-hierarchy.  
**Minimum test:** stratify questions by counting, object, scene, and temporal relation under equal budgets.

### Hypothesis 2: frequency scoring suppresses rare-event recall

**Proposition:** DCP’s frequency component improves common-event accuracy but disproportionately loses one-off events.  
**Predicted observation:** rare, brief perturbations have lower retained-token recall than repeated actions.  
**Falsifying observation:** recall is equal after controlling for attention.  
**Minimum test:** insert timestamped rare events into long streams and audit token retention.

### Hypothesis 3: write/read error decomposition predicts scaling

**Proposition:** long-horizon degradation is determined by the product of evidence survival at write time and retrieval recall at read time.  
**Predicted observation:** measured component recalls predict answer accuracy better than stream length.  
**Falsifying observation:** generation errors dominate despite correct evidence.  
**Minimum test:** oracle-cache and oracle-retrieval factorial evaluation.

## 14. Deployment and governance considerations

### 14.1 Appropriate use

MuKV is appropriate for exploratory streaming QA where occasional retrieval errors are acceptable and the model can cite timestamps. It is not validated for safety-critical surveillance, medical monitoring, or autonomous control.

### 14.2 Poor fit and failure modes

Poor fits include exact counting, rare-event detection, legal retention, and settings requiring exhaustive recall. Failures include irreversible pruning, cross-grain score mismatch, positional incompatibility, LLM hallucination, privacy accumulation, and judge/benchmark drift.

### 14.3 Required safeguards

1. Preserve source-video references and timestamp provenance for every answer.
2. Log write-time retention and read-time retrieval separately.
3. Support abstention and raw-video fallback when confidence or coverage is low.
4. Enforce retention, privacy, and tenant-isolation policies on cached KV.
5. Revalidate after backbone, tokenizer, or positional-encoding changes.

## 15. Replication and falsification agenda

### 15.1 Highest-priority result

Reproduce Table 1 and Table 4 with pinned LLaVA-OV, judge prompts, API model version, sampling, dtype, GPU, and repeated runs. Report confidence intervals by video rather than treating questions as independent.

### 15.2 Full pipeline

Archive frame sampling, segment construction, per-grain token counts, DCP scores, retained indices, KV dtype/layout, retrieval candidates, final selected tensors, prompts, answers, and judgments.

### 15.3 Normalize evaluation

Compare MuKV, ReKV, textual memory, embedding memory, and long-context baselines at equal ingest compute, storage bytes/hour, online latency, and answer-token budget.

### 15.4 Test the mechanism

Use oracle write and oracle retrieval variants. Evaluate frequency with shuffled time/patch axes and explicitly measure answer-span survival.

### 15.5 Test realistic shifts

Add longer horizons, variable frame rates, scene cuts, rare events, multilingual questions, noisy/low-light video, multiple concurrent streams, and different multimodal backbones.

## 16. Durable restatement

> MuKV treats long streaming video memory as a multiresolution KV evidence store. Patch, frame, and segment representations are pruned with attention/frequency signals and queried through a semi-hierarchical retriever. The paper reports favorable accuracy/storage/latency trade-offs on its benchmarks, but the write-time and read-time approximations remain empirical and configuration-dependent.

The key lesson is to make information granularity explicit and to evaluate memory by answerability under joint storage and latency budgets.
