# Conditional Gain Control for Faithful Uncertainty

## A detailed review, technical reconstruction, and independent re-conceptualization of “Reinforcement Learning with Metacognitive Feedback Elicits Faithful Uncertainty Expression in LLMs”

**Source paper:** Gabrielle Kaili-May Liu, Avi Caciularu, Gal Yona, Idan Szpektor, and Arman Cohan, “Reinforcement Learning with Metacognitive Feedback Elicits Faithful Uncertainty Expression in LLMs,” arXiv preprint, arXiv:2606.32032v1.[^source-paper]

**Artifact prepared:** 2026-07-14
**Local source reviewed:** complete 62-page PDF, full-paper HTML, metadata record, TeX source, appendices, and figures; private archive path withheld
**Review scope:** full-paper reconstruction, claim vetting, official-code audit, external primary-source contextualization, independent mechanism interpretation, implications, and replication agenda
**Review status:** official MIT-licensed code inspected at commit `a087e7a1e49f52aaa701add19cd80699b709fdef`; dependencies were not installed and experiments were not rerun

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

The paper modifies group-relative reinforcement learning so that accurate self-assessment increases the learning weight of completions that are already above average on faithful uncertainty expression, then converts the resulting numerical confidence into contextual natural-language hedges.

### The deeper idea

RLMF is best understood as **conditional gain control over a task-specific advantage**, not as a general reward for introspection. The policy produces a completion, an external procedure estimates whether its stated confidence matches sampled behavioral consistency, and the policy predicts how well it achieved that alignment. This second-order prediction does not replace task performance. It changes the gain applied to the positive faithfulness component after that component has already cleared a group-relative threshold.

That restriction is the paper's most important design decision. A completion cannot earn stronger reinforcement merely by recognizing that it performed poorly. Accurate self-diagnosis matters only among completions that are already comparatively faithful. The method therefore separates “doing the task well” from “knowing how well the task was done,” while using the latter to prioritize the former.

### Bottom-line judgment

The paper offers a concrete, well-documented, and empirically promising mechanism for training uncertainty expression. The reported faithful-calibration gains are large across the tested Llama and Qwen families, the appendix contains unusually broad ablations, and the official implementation exposes the central mechanism clearly. The two-stage design also solves a real product problem: numerical uncertainty can be calibrated once, while linguistic realization can later adapt to audience and context.

The evidence does not establish broad metacognition, latent self-knowledge, or universal state of the art. The quantity called intrinsic confidence is estimated from sampled-response consistency and an NLI judge. RLMF may therefore improve alignment to a behavioral proxy and the evaluation machinery that defines it. Main results lack multi-seed uncertainty, checkpoint selection uses in-domain test faithful calibration, and several stages share judges, prompts, and metrics. The correct interpretation is “strong preprint evidence for conditional self-evaluation as a faithful-calibration training signal,” pending held-out, multi-seed, cross-estimator replication.

### Principal strengths

- The metacognitive signal is subordinate to primary task performance rather than added as an unconstrained reward.
- Experiments span multiple model sizes, ten datasets, several training tasks, extensive mechanism ablations, and a structured human study.
- The official repository is public, pinned dependencies and sample commands are provided, and the central trainer implementation matches the paper closely.
- Numerical calibration and linguistic realization are decoupled, enabling contextual adaptation without repeating reinforcement learning.

### Principal qualifications

1. “Intrinsic confidence” is a sampled-consistency proxy shaped by decoding and a Qwen3-32B judge, not verified access to a model's internal belief.
2. One reported seed, missing confidence intervals, and test-set checkpoint selection weaken the causal and generalization claims.
3. The human study validates linguistic preference under a specific rubric, not factual preservation or calibrated real-user reliance.
4. The pipeline is expensive and evaluator dependent, with no complete wall-clock, token, energy, or monetary cost ledger.

## 1. The problem the paper is actually solving

### 1.1 Problem and stakes

Language models routinely produce answers whose verbal confidence is poorly related to their own behavioral stability. A model may answer assertively while alternate samples disagree, or hedge despite repeatedly producing the same claim. This can miscalibrate user reliance even when aggregate correctness is acceptable.

The paper distinguishes two targets. **Factual calibration** aligns confidence with correctness: among answers assigned 0.8 confidence, roughly 80 percent should be correct. **Faithful calibration** aligns expressed confidence with an estimate of the model's own confidence. The targets can diverge. A model can consistently believe a false fact and express that high confidence faithfully; it can also be factually calibrated in aggregate while using confidence language that is disconnected from its response process.

The practical stake is not simply better scores. Uncertainty language affects whether users verify, defer, or act. In research, professional, and educational settings, a model that states uncertainty in proportion to its own instability can expose where additional checking is warranted. Yet faithful uncertainty cannot replace factual verification: a faithfully expressed mistake remains a mistake.

### 1.2 Prior method families

The paper positions three prior families as incomplete.

Prompting methods such as MetaFaith ask models to reflect on and express internal uncertainty. MetaFaith established that LLMs often fail faithful linguistic calibration and that metacognitive prompting can improve it.[^metafaith] Prompting is cheap and model agnostic, but it depends on instruction following and can affect task accuracy.

Supervised fine-tuning methods such as FUT teach models to emit predefined linguistic uncertainty expressions.[^fut] They can improve alignment but risk formulaic language, limited hedge diversity, and generalization tied to the training task and phrase inventory.

Reinforcement-learning methods have optimized factual confidence, entropy, self-certainty, and calibration-related rewards. The paper argues that these methods operate directly on output confidence rather than on the accuracy of a model's assessment of its own task performance. RLMF moves one level upward: the auxiliary signal is whether the model correctly predicts its faithful-calibration performance.

Sampling-consistency methods such as SelfCheckGPT provide a practical black-box uncertainty estimator by comparing generated content with additional samples.[^selfcheck] RLMF inherits that convenience, but also inherits estimator dependence: semantic-equivalence handling, sample count, temperature, and judge behavior define the target.

### 1.3 The paper's proposed gap

The authors claim that no prior method provides an end-to-end pipeline for both numerical and natural linguistic faithful calibration, and that metacognitive performance has not previously been used to scale LLM reinforcement-learning advantages in this way. The paper proposes four linked contributions:

- RLMF, which uses self-assessment accuracy to scale positive faithfulness advantages;
- metacognitive data selection, which chooses high- and low-self-rated examples;
- cMFG-star, a revised faithful-calibration metric using equal-mass bins and confidence-axis-width weighting; and
- targeted rewriting from numerical confidence to contextual linguistic uncertainty.

The combination is novel and coherent. The “first” and “state-of-the-art” wording remains an author claim in a fast-moving 2026 literature. This review examined direct predecessors and the official implementation, not every contemporaneous paper.

## 2. Formal and technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Definitions and assumptions

For input query `q`, policy model `M_theta` samples a group of `G` completions. Completion `r_g` contains `N_g` sentence-confidence pairs:

```text
r_g = {(s_1, c_1), ..., (s_Ng, c_Ng)}
```

The expressed confidence `c_i` lies in `[0,1]`. The target `g_i`, called intrinsic confidence, is estimated from consistency between sentence `s_i` and a set of sampled responses. In the main setup, `K=20` sampled responses are compared using Qwen3-32B as a semantic-consistency judge.

The core assumptions are:

- sampled response agreement is a useful proxy for the policy's confidence;
- the judge can assess semantic support consistently;
- sentence boundaries are an acceptable granularity;
- the policy can meaningfully predict its completion-level faithful calibration;
- group-relative reward comparisons produce informative gradients; and
- improving alignment to this proxy transfers across tasks and confidence-expression modes.

The first two assumptions define the construct. If sampling or the judge changes, `g_i` may change even when the policy does not.

### 2.2 Objective and central equations

The paper computes several rewards. Strict and soft format rewards enforce parseable sentence-confidence tags. A correctness reward protects task accuracy. A factual-calibration reward protects confidence-correctness alignment. The main faithfulness reward is quadratic:

```text
r_faith(i) = 1 - (c_i - g_i)^2
```

Sentence rewards are averaged within the completion. The appendix reports default reward weights of 3 for strict format, 3 for soft format, 1 for factual calibration, 1 for correctness, and 12 for faithfulness.

The completion's computed faithful-calibration level is thresholded:

```text
F_gold(g) = (1 / N_g) * sum_i I(|c_i - g_i| < tau)
```

The policy is separately prompted to predict this completion-level value, giving `F_pred(g)`. Metacognitive performance is:

```text
Z_g = 1 - (F_pred(g) - F_gold(g))^2
```

With values in `[0,1]`, `Z_g=1` denotes exact agreement. It measures accuracy in judging faithful-calibration performance, not faithful calibration itself.

RLMF separates the faithfulness reward `f_g` from all other reward terms `o_g`. With group means `mean(f)` and `mean(o)`, additive constant `k=1`, and standard-deviation normalization removed, the advantage is:

```text
A_RLMF(g) = (o_g - mean(o)) +
  { (f_g - mean(f)) * (k + Z_g), if f_g > mean(f)
  { (f_g - mean(f)),             otherwise
```

This makes `Z_g` a multiplier only on positive faithfulness deviations. Accessory objectives remain centered but unscaled.

### 2.3 Components

The complete system has six functional components:

1. A pre-SFT formatter teaches sentence/confidence tags.
2. The policy model generates 32 grouped completions per prompt.
3. A consistency pipeline estimates sentence-level intrinsic confidence from sampled responses.
4. Composite rewards measure format, correctness, factual calibration, and faithful calibration.
5. A metacognitive prompt obtains `F_pred`, and the RLMF trainer computes `Z_g` and the modified advantage.
6. A rewriting model maps confidence bins to diverse, context-appropriate linguistic hedges.

Metacognitive data selection is an offline companion component. It produces linguistic answers, asks the same model to score alignment between linguistic and internal confidence, and selects equal numbers from the highest and lowest scores.

### 2.4 Training process

1. Run pre-SFT on 1,800 examples with 200 validation examples for five epochs. The reported configuration uses LoRA rank 32, alpha 64, dropout 0.05, and learning rate `3e-5`.
2. Score the candidate training pool with the model's own metacognitive ratings. Select 1,000 high-score and 1,000 low-score examples.
3. For each GRPO prompt, generate 32 completions. Parse sentence-confidence pairs, estimate intrinsic confidence, compute composite rewards, and obtain a predicted faithful-calibration level.
4. Compute `Z_g`, center faithfulness and accessory reward components, and amplify above-mean faithfulness advantages by `1 + Z_g`.
5. Optimize LoRA parameters for up to 1,500 steps with BF16, AdamW, `beta=0.1`, effective batch size 64, and a learning-rate sweep over `5e-6`, `1e-5`, and `2e-5`.
6. Save checkpoints every 100 steps and select the best using in-domain faithful-calibration performance, then evaluate that checkpoint across all tasks.

The last step is an evaluation weakness. In-domain test data should be replaced by a dedicated validation split for model selection.

### 2.5 Inference process

1. The trained policy answers a query with sentence-level numerical confidence tags.
2. Each confidence value is mapped to a width-0.05 bin containing human-rated hedge candidates.
3. A rewriting model receives the numerical response, 20 candidate hedges per bin, and user or task context.
4. The model performs a one-pass rewrite intended to preserve content while adapting uncertainty expression.
5. The rewritten output is evaluated for linguistic faithful calibration, accuracy, factual calibration, and human-rated communication quality.

RLMF training is not repeated when context changes. Only the rewriting prompt and context change.

### 2.6 Notation and implementation audit

The official trainer at pinned commit `a087e7a` subclasses TRL 0.23.0 `GRPOTrainer`. It retrieves an auxiliary metacognitive score from the faithfulness reward, separates the last reward function as faithfulness, computes group means, and applies the piecewise multiplier exactly as described. The sample configuration sets `advantage_form='mf'`, `advantage_piecewise=False`, `advantage_k=1`, `mc_threshold=0.10`, 32 generations, and `scale_rewards='none'`.[^code]

One minor mismatch exists. The displayed paper equation uses strict `< tau`; the reward code counts a sentence as close when `abs(p-g) <= mc_threshold`. Only exact-boundary cases differ. The mismatch is unlikely to explain the effect, but specification and tests should agree.

The code also documents a clamp at zero for the positive scaling factor. With official `Z_g` in `[0,1]` and `k=1`, the clamp is inactive, but it protects alternative score formulations. No larger equation/code discrepancy was found in the inspected central files.

## 3. Architecture and information flow

### 3.1 Representation

The policy's basic representation is an answer segmented into sentences with explicit confidence values. This makes the reward parseable and allows long responses to assign different confidence to different claims. Sentence-level scoring is a compromise: response-level confidence is too coarse, while atomic-claim extraction is more expensive and itself judge dependent.

Three confidence-like representations coexist:

- `c_i`: confidence expressed by the policy for sentence `i`;
- `g_i`: sampled-consistency estimate treated as intrinsic confidence; and
- `F_pred`: the policy's prediction of how well all `c_i` values match their `g_i` targets.

The metacognitive score compares the third representation with a thresholded aggregate computed from the first two. Schemas and dashboards must keep these names separate. Calling all three “confidence” invites serious interpretation errors.

### 3.2 Decision or action modules

The policy decides answer content and numerical confidence. The consistency judge decides whether alternate samples support each sentence. The correctness judge decides whether an answer matches the task target. The trainer decides how much gradient weight each completion receives. The rewriting model decides which hedge phrase and sentence structure express the numerical score in context.

No one module is authoritative. A failure in sentence parsing, semantic consistency, correctness judging, self-assessment, or rewriting can affect the final output. The architecture is therefore an evaluator graph, not a single calibrated model.

### 3.3 Dependencies and post-processing

The main setup depends on Llama 3.1 or Qwen3 policy weights, Qwen3-32B for consistency judgments, TRL/vLLM/Transformers/PEFT/PyTorch, multiple public benchmarks, and Gemini-2.5-Flash-Lite for accuracy judging and rewriting. GPT-5-Mini and Qwen3-8B are tested as alternative rewriters.

Post-processing includes tag parsing, confidence-bin assignment, human-rated hedge lookup, one-pass rewriting, score aggregation, and plot/table generation. These stages create reproducibility obligations beyond model weights: prompts, templates, bin maps, model API versions, parsing rules, and dependency versions all need pins.

## 4. The complete operational pipeline

### 4.1 End-to-end flow

The pipeline begins with an uncalibrated instruction model. Pre-SFT teaches output structure. Metacognitive data selection chooses a model-specific training subset. During RLMF, grouped responses provide both policy candidates and samples for estimating behavioral consistency. Composite rewards protect output format and task quality while directly optimizing expressed/proxy alignment. The policy predicts its completion-level alignment, and the accuracy of that prediction changes how strongly positive faithfulness evidence is learned.

After training, numerical confidence is stable enough to serve as an intermediate interface. The rewriting stage turns that interface into language appropriate to an educational, research, professional, or other stated context. This division makes the numerical layer a control surface and the linguistic layer a presentation adapter.

### 4.2 Resource allocation

The paper reports four GPUs for distributed training, one GPU for the rollout server, and one GPU for the Qwen3-32B judge. Hardware includes A6000, A100, and H100 devices. The official README says at least three GPUs are needed in the default setup, demonstrates six, and optionally uses two more to evaluate checkpoints while training.

Each training prompt uses 32 completions. Intrinsic-confidence estimation depends on repeated samples and semantic judgments. Metacognitive data selection also runs inference and scoring before training. Stage 2 invokes a separate rewriting model. The paper reports configuration but not a complete accounting of generated tokens, judge tokens, GPU hours, wall time, failures, energy, or API spend.

The architecture is computationally rational for a research demonstration but not lightweight. A practical reproduction should start with a small model and synthetic or reduced public task, measure every stage, and avoid downloading unused checkpoints or duplicating datasets.

### 4.3 Guarantee boundary

RLMF provides no formal guarantee that expressed confidence equals latent belief, that outputs are correct, or that users will rely appropriately. `Z_g` is exact only relative to the computed `F_gold` and prompted `F_pred` in that run. cMFG-star is an empirical metric relative to the chosen estimator and test samples. Human preference is empirical under a given rubric and annotator pool.

Validation occurs at several boundaries: parser validity, reward computation, checkpoint selection, cross-task metrics, rewriting metrics, and human ratings. The strongest safe claim is that the reported selected checkpoints align expressed uncertainty with the paper's sampled-consistency estimator better than the listed baselines on the tested datasets.

## 5. Re-conceptualization

### 5.1 Metacognitive gain control

RLMF can be generalized as a controller with a primary signal and a diagnostic signal. The primary signal says whether an action is good. The diagnostic signal says whether the actor accurately understands the quality of its action. The controller increases gain only when both are favorable.

This is different from rewarding confidence, punishing entropy, or adding a self-critique score. The diagnostic signal does not decide direction; it changes magnitude after direction has been established. That pattern can apply to tool-use agents, compression systems, retrieval selection, or planning, provided the primary predicate is independently verifiable.

**Boundary of the analogy:** In RLMF, the diagnostic target is generated within the same broad model-and-judge ecosystem. In safety-critical control, an independent sensor or executable validator may be required. Self-assessment alone should not authorize consequential actions.

### 5.2 Numerical confidence as an intermediate representation

The two-stage pipeline treats numerical confidence as an intermediate representation between model behavior and language. This is analogous to separating semantic state from rendering. The model first produces a structured epistemic estimate; a context adapter later renders it as “likely,” “my best guess,” or a domain-appropriate qualification.

The separation is useful because user communication styles change faster than model training. It also supports auditing: evaluators can inspect whether linguistic intensity matches the numerical value. The risk is that the rewriter becomes a lossy compiler. It may change facts, scope, or recommendation strength while preserving a plausible hedge.

**Boundary of the analogy:** A numerical score is not a complete semantic state. Different uncertainty sources, distributions, and stakes may require different language even at the same scalar value.

### 5.3 Transferable design principle

**Use self-evaluation to prioritize verified success, never to redefine success.** Keep the primary task predicate, diagnostic score, support evidence, and transformation checks in separate fields. Only amplify behavior that has passed the primary predicate. When the diagnostic channel is unsupported, shifted, or in disagreement, route to review rather than silently substituting confidence for evidence.

## 6. Experimental design reconstructed

### 6.1 Data

The evaluation covers ten datasets, generally capped at 1,000 test examples each: PopQA, SelfAware, SimpleQA, HaluEval, MMLU, SciQ, MATH, UMWP, ARC-Challenge, and SuperGLUE. Main training uses 2,000 PopQA examples selected by the metacognitive high-plus-low strategy. Additional training runs use SelfAware, HaluEval, and UMWP to test training-task robustness.

The mix is broad in task format but still mostly benchmark QA and reasoning. It does not directly represent long-running agents, retrieval systems with changing corpora, tool execution, dialogue over many turns, or real high-stakes users. Dataset-version and contamination questions are not the paper's focus.

The human study draws 20 examples each from Natural Questions, SciQ, and SelfAware for Llama3.1-8B and Qwen3-8B, yielding 120 query-model cases. Each case shows three responses from FUT and three from RLMF-plus-rewriting. Contexts are educational assistance, research/fact checking, and high-stakes professional assistance.

### 6.2 Training and hardware

RLMF uses LoRA rank 64, alpha 64, dropout 0.05 over linear projections, BF16, AdamW, 1,500 steps, learning-rate sweep `5e-6`, `1e-5`, `2e-5`, warmup, cosine schedule, `beta=0.1`, and effective batch size 64. The reported seed is 42. Pre-SFT uses rank 32, five epochs, and `3e-5`.

The paper reports mixed A6000, A100, and H100 hardware and a six-GPU main topology. Hardware heterogeneity matters for runtime comparisons, but the paper's central claim is metric quality rather than speed. No total training cost is given.

### 6.3 Baselines

The main baselines are the unmodified model, MetaFaith prompting, FUT supervised tuning, and standard RL without metacognitive advantage scaling. Proprietary comparisons apply MetaFaith to Gemini 3.1 Pro, Gemini 3 Flash, and GPT-5. Smaller Qwen models test scale transfer.

Standard RL is the most important causal baseline because it shares the reward and training framework while removing the metacognitive scaling. Pre-SFT, data selection, and many hyperparameters still interact, so the appendix variants are necessary to attribute gains.

Comparisons with proprietary models are less controlled. The models differ in size, training, API behavior, prompting, and evaluator relationships. Specialized small-model performance on cMFG-star does not imply general capability superiority.

### 6.4 Metrics

Instance faithful calibration is one minus the average absolute difference between expressed and intrinsic confidence. cMFG-star groups examples into equal-mass bins by intrinsic confidence and weights mean bin faithfulness by each bin's width on the confidence axis. Dividing by total empirical support width produces a uniform-over-support average.

This addresses two problems in prior cMFG: population concentration can dominate, and equal-width bins may be empty for models with narrow confidence ranges. Equal-mass bins improve estimation stability. Width weighting prevents dense regions from dominating.

The remaining concern is support. A model whose intrinsic confidence occupies `[0.45,0.60]` is scored uniformly over that range, not penalized because it lacks examples near zero or one. That may be appropriate for within-support faithfulness, but cross-model reports should include support width, bin counts, and curves.

Accuracy is scored by an LLM judge. Brier score measures factual calibration. Human metrics cover diversity, naturalness, helpfulness, and contextual suitability. No metric directly measures whether users make better decisions after reading the output.

### 6.5 Runtime semantics

The paper does not make a universal runtime-speedup claim. Runtime burden appears in training topology, generation count, repeated consistency scoring, and rewriting calls. The 32 completions used for GRPO also contribute to confidence estimation, which reuses computation conceptually, but judge scoring remains expensive.

A production evaluation should report p50/p95 latency for numerical inference and rewriting separately, tokens per request, judge batching, failed parse rate, and cost by stage. The one-time-training argument for Stage 2 adaptation is valid only if rewriting quality remains stable across contexts and API versions.

## 7. Results: what is reported and what it means

### 7.1 Llama3.1-8B-Instruct

Average cMFG-star/accuracy/Brier results are:

| Method | cMFG-star | Accuracy | Brier |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Base | 0.60 | 0.31 | 0.33 |
| MetaFaith | 0.67 | 0.28 | 0.36 |
| FUT | 0.66 | 0.31 | 0.29 |
| Standard RL | 0.77 | 0.40 | 0.20 |
| RLMF | 0.84 | 0.41 | 0.26 |
| RLMF plus rewriting | 0.82 | 0.41 | 0.26 |

**Interpretation:** RLMF improves the target faithful-calibration metric substantially over prior linguistic methods and standard RL while retaining the accuracy gain. Rewriting preserves most of the numerical gain.

**Qualification:** Standard RL has a better Brier score than RLMF. The method therefore trades among faithful calibration, factual calibration, and task accuracy rather than uniformly dominating. No seed variance is reported.

### 7.2 Qwen3-8B and smaller Qwen models

Qwen3-8B results are:

| Method | cMFG-star | Accuracy | Brier |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Base | 0.54 | 0.55 | 0.31 |
| MetaFaith | 0.63 | 0.51 | 0.29 |
| FUT | 0.67 | 0.38 | 0.41 |
| Standard RL | 0.51 | 0.59 | 0.26 |
| RLMF | 0.83 | 0.57 | 0.19 |
| RLMF plus rewriting | 0.83 | 0.57 | 0.19 |

**Interpretation:** The Qwen comparison is especially favorable to RLMF. Standard RL improves accuracy but fails faithful calibration on several tasks; RLMF improves both the target metric and Brier score relative to base.

Qwen3-4B reaches 0.83 numerically and 0.85 after rewriting. Qwen3-1.7B reaches 0.82 and 0.83. These results suggest the mechanism is not limited to 8B scale.

**Qualification:** All Qwen results share the paper's estimator and selection framework. Similar numbers across sizes could reflect a robust method, a saturated metric, or shared adaptation to the same judge-defined target. Cross-estimator testing is needed.

### 7.3 Generalization, robustness, and human preference

Training on PopQA produces 0.84 for Llama and 0.83 for Qwen. SelfAware, HaluEval, and UMWP training produce 0.80-0.81 for Llama and 0.81 for Qwen. The cross-task table supports robustness to the chosen training task within the tested set.

Metacognitive selection improves over random and active selection. For Llama, the cMFG-star values are 0.84, 0.80, and 0.79. For Qwen, they are 0.83, 0.76, and 0.72. The accuracy columns show why faithful calibration cannot be reviewed alone: Qwen active selection reports 0.36 accuracy, and the random row in the compact table contains an accuracy value that should be checked carefully against the full table and pipeline.

The human study reports 98 percent wins for diversity and naturalness, 95 percent for helpfulness, and 96 percent for contextual suitability. Inter-annotator agreement is 0.93. These are striking preferences under the rubric and examples. They should not be converted into a claim that 96 percent of all users make better decisions.

## 8. Ablations and causal evidence

### 8.1 Reward and optimization components

The full reward reaches 0.65 cMFG-star in the preliminary reward ablation setting. Removing strict format drops to 0.48; removing soft format to 0.55; removing factual calibration to 0.62; and removing accuracy to 0.61. Faithfulness weights 5, 8, and 25 yield 0.50, 0.52, and 0.57, indicating that both under- and over-weighting can hurt.

Quadratic faithfulness reward reaches 0.65 versus 0.62 for stretched cross entropy, 0.61 for binned cross entropy, and 0.57 for linear. The selected system prompt reaches 0.72 in its comparison. Removing advantage standard-deviation normalization reaches 0.78 versus 0.72 with normalization.

These results show a tuned system rather than a parameter-free principle. The mechanism works with a specific reward mixture, prompt, and normalization strategy. A multi-seed factorial design would show which interactions are stable.

### 8.2 Metacognitive mechanism and data selection

The raw quadratic `Z_g` reaches 0.84, outperforming linear and root alternatives. Using unnormalized `Z_g` reaches 0.84 versus 0.80 for mean centering and 0.71 for z-scoring. Scaling the faithfulness component only reaches 0.84; scaling the whole advantage reaches 0.71, and scaling the whole group reaches 0.69.

`k=1` reaches 0.84, compared with 0.69 for `k=0`, 0.76 for `k=2`, and 0.71 for `k=4`. `tau=0.10` reaches 0.84 versus 0.74 for 0.05. A direct metacognitive-reward variant is weaker and exposes reward-gaming complexity. These ablations directly support conditional advantage scaling as the preferred implementation.

Training-data size is non-monotonic: 2,000 examples reaches 0.84, while 1,000 reaches 0.80 and 4,000 reaches 0.80. High-plus-low metacognitive selection is strongest among tested strategies. This suggests the selected examples alter reward diversity and gradient signal, not merely sample count.

### 8.3 Randomness and statistical evidence

The paper reports seed 42 for training details but does not provide seed-level main tables, standard deviations, confidence intervals, or significance tests. RL with sampled completions is stochastic at data selection, rollout, judge, optimization, and checkpoint levels. One seed cannot distinguish a robust mechanism from a favorable trajectory.

Human evaluation reports inter-annotator agreement but not confidence intervals on preference rates. The 120 cases are a useful study but not independent at every level: multiple responses come from the same models and datasets, and the annotators are three graduate students familiar with LLMs.

The appropriate next experiment is not another broad benchmark sweep. It is a narrower pre-registered study with at least five seeds, a fixed validation protocol, untouched test sets, paired bootstrap intervals, and public aggregate predictions.

## 10. External views and field context

### 10.1 Predecessor evidence

MetaFaith formalized faithful natural-language uncertainty expression and found that prompting could improve it while exposing persistent gaps. RLMF uses MetaFaith as both conceptual predecessor and strong prompting baseline. This means the new paper does not originate the faithful-calibration problem; it extends the intervention from prompting toward training and numerical confidence.[^metafaith]

FUT demonstrates that supervised fine-tuning can teach linguistic uncertainty. RLMF's human-study advantage over FUT is therefore best understood as evidence for decoupled numerical calibration plus contextual rewriting over fixed or formulaic linguistic tuning, not evidence that supervised tuning is generally ineffective.[^fut]

SelfCheckGPT and semantic-uncertainty work establish that repeated generations and semantic equivalence can provide useful uncertainty signals without model-internal logits. They also make the proxy boundary unavoidable: sampling-based confidence measures behavior under a generation distribution.[^selfcheck]

### 10.2 Independent evaluation perspective

Calibration literature has long shown that scalar expected calibration error can obscure bin support, class structure, and distribution shift. The paper's cMFG-star addresses a specific density and empty-bin problem with equal-mass bins and width weighting. A complete evaluation card should still report support, counts, confidence curves, accuracy, Brier score, and shift slices.

The strongest methodological critique is leakage through model selection. Evaluating every checkpoint on the in-domain test set and selecting the best converts that test set into validation data. Cross-task datasets remain less directly selected, but the claimed in-domain result is optimistic and hyperparameters can indirectly favor the shared metric pipeline.

### 10.3 Contemporaneous or later alternatives

Semantic entropy groups generations by meaning and measures uncertainty over semantic clusters. It provides an alternative target for the cross-proxy experiment proposed here. Logit confidence, when available, and verifier-based correctness probabilities offer other channels. None is universally “intrinsic”; each encodes different assumptions.

Direct abstention training is another alternative when the product objective is safe deferral rather than faithful self-expression. Conformal or PAC-style prediction can add finite-sample coverage under stated assumptions. These methods answer different questions: RLMF asks whether language matches a behavioral confidence proxy, while abstention and coverage methods ask whether decisions meet risk constraints.

### 10.4 Publication, code, and reproducibility status

The paper is arXiv v1, submitted 30 June 2026, with no verified conference publication at the cutoff. The official `yale-nlp/RLMF` repository is public under MIT, has a clear file map, and provides commands for baseline scoring, MetaFaith, RLMF, data selection, checkpoint evaluation, and rewriting. It requires API keys for proprietary services, Hugging Face access, external UMWP data, and substantial GPU resources.[^code]

The repository makes reproduction materially more feasible but not turnkey. No tagged release, container, published checkpoints, frozen dataset bundle, raw predictions, or seed-level result archive was identified. Dependency pins include rapidly changing ML libraries. Exact API model versions may become unavailable.

## 11. Research notes and critical considerations

### 11.1 Internal consistency

The main equations, appendix, and pinned code agree on the core mechanism. The strict `< tau` versus inclusive `<= tau` comparison is the one concrete implementation/specification difference found. The README's sample commands and config are consistent with the six-GPU architecture and central hyperparameters.

The compact data-selection table reports Qwen3-8B random-selection accuracy as 0.23, while the full appendix data-selection table reports 0.53 for the same row; both print cMFG-star 0.76 and Brier 0.18. The source does not resolve which accuracy is intended. This discrepancy should be corrected against released aggregate predictions before the row is reused.

The paper sometimes moves from “improved self-assessment on faithful calibration” to broader “metacognitive awareness” and alignment implications. A footnote correctly notes that better self-assessment on one task is not equivalent to broad metacognition. That footnote should govern the interpretation of the abstract and conclusion.

### 11.2 Metric and baseline concerns

cMFG-star is conditional on the intrinsic-confidence estimator. If RLMF changes sampling diversity, response style, or compatibility with the NLI judge, both target and score can move. Evaluating against a second estimator is necessary to show transfer.

The proprietary baseline comparison uses specialized prompting but remains asymmetrical. Open models receive task-specific training; frontier systems do not. The result shows specialized calibration can beat prompted general models on this metric, not that the small models are generally more reliable.

Checkpoint and hyperparameter selection should use validation data. Main results should report every seed and the selection rule, not only the best checkpoint per experiment. The broad ablation suite also raises multiple-comparison risk.

### 11.3 Unsupported or overbroad claims

“State of the art” is supported against the listed baselines but not against every concurrent 2026 method. “First end-to-end pipeline” is plausible but cannot be certified by this review. “Metacognitive awareness” is broader than the measured prediction of faithful-calibration performance. “Self-directed learning” is a speculative implication of model-specific data selection, not a demonstrated autonomous capability.

The paper's alignment language should be read as direction, not established safety. Better uncertainty expression can help, but it can also make a model more persuasive and socially fluent. Alignment requires correctness, robustness, oversight, and user-impact evidence.

### 11.4 Alternative explanations

RLMF may improve because `Z_g` correlates with easy-to-parse, stable, or stylistically regular completions rather than because the model develops a general capacity to monitor itself. The high-plus-low data strategy may increase diversity or curriculum contrast independent of metacognition. Removing advantage normalization and tuning the reward mixture may account for some gains. Shared judge prompts may reward outputs that are easier for Qwen3-32B to compare.

Stage 2's human preference may come from a strong proprietary rewriter's general stylistic ability rather than the numerical hedge map alone. A blinded ablation should compare the same rewriter with true scores, shuffled scores, and no scores.

### 11.5 Unresolved questions

- Does RLMF improve alignment to logit confidence, semantic entropy, and human consistency judgments when trained only on the Qwen3-32B sampling proxy?
- Are gains stable across seeds, model versions, decoding temperatures, and judge families?
- How often does rewriting alter entities, values, negation, scope, or recommendation strength?
- Do users verify more appropriately, or merely prefer the more natural outputs?
- Can a smaller verifier or cached estimator preserve gains at practical cost?
- What happens under distribution shift where the model's confidence support changes?

## 12. Potential implications

### 12.1 Scientific implications

The results support a specific hypothesis: second-order performance prediction can be useful for optimizing first-order behavior when applied conditionally. This suggests experiments beyond uncertainty calibration, such as tool-use validity, retrieval sufficiency, or code-test prediction, where the model estimates whether an independent validator will pass and that estimate scales only already-valid behavior.

The work also clarifies that “metacognition” in machine learning should be decomposed. Monitoring, prediction, regulation, confidence expression, and task selection are different capabilities. RLMF measures monitoring of one calibration objective and uses it for regulation of gradient strength.

### 12.2 System-design implications

Numerical confidence can serve as an auditable intermediate representation before user-facing language. Systems can validate numbers, support, and correctness separately, then allow multiple context adapters. Original numerical responses should be retained so rewritten text can be diffed and reverted.

Evaluator provenance should be a first-class artifact. Every result should record policy version, decoding settings, sample count, judge version, prompts, confidence estimator, split, and checkpoint-selection rule. Judge disagreement should be preserved rather than averaged away.

### 12.3 Broader or cross-domain implications

Conditional gain control applies wherever an agent can predict whether it satisfied a verifiable objective. In code repair, a model's prediction that tests will pass could scale learning only after tests actually pass. In retrieval, predicted sufficiency could scale examples only when citations support claims. In planning, predicted constraint satisfaction could scale only trajectories that a formal checker accepts.

The boundary is critical: self-prediction should never replace the external predicate. Otherwise the system optimizes confidence about success rather than success.

## 13. New hypotheses derived from the paper

These are reviewer inferences, not established findings.

### Hypothesis 1: Cross-proxy transfer separates monitoring from proxy fitting

**Proposition:** If RLMF teaches transferable performance monitoring, gains trained with sampled consistency will also appear when faithful calibration is measured using semantic entropy, open-model logits, or independent human consistency labels.
**Motivating evidence:** RLMF improves across tasks and model sizes, but every main evaluation uses closely related proxy machinery.
**Predicted observation:** RLMF checkpoints outperform standard RL on at least two held-out confidence estimators without new tuning.
**Falsifying observation:** Gains disappear or reverse whenever the Qwen3-32B consistency target is replaced.
**Minimum test:** Train three seeds on a reduced public task, then evaluate the same frozen outputs with three independent estimators and paired bootstrap intervals.

### Hypothesis 2: Conditional scaling is robust because it blocks diagnostic reward hacking

**Proposition:** RLMF's piecewise gate works better than a direct metacognitive reward because it prevents accurate self-description of poor behavior from earning positive optimization pressure.
**Motivating evidence:** Whole-advantage, whole-group, and direct-reward variants underperform the selected formulation.
**Predicted observation:** Under adversarial synthetic fixtures where self-assessment is easy but task performance is poor, direct reward increases self-description while conditional scaling preserves task quality.
**Falsifying observation:** Direct reward matches conditional scaling once reward weights and compute are tuned fairly.
**Minimum test:** Use a synthetic grouped-bandit task with independently controlled task quality and diagnostic accuracy, then measure gradient allocation and final behavior.

### Hypothesis 3: Rewriting preference is partly independent of calibrated scores

**Proposition:** A strong rewriter contributes substantial naturalness gains even when confidence-bin assignments are shuffled, while true assignments are required for faithful-calibration gains.
**Motivating evidence:** Human ratings favor style and context, and multiple strong rewriters perform similarly.
**Predicted observation:** Shuffled-score rewrites retain some naturalness preference but lose expressed/proxy alignment.
**Falsifying observation:** Shuffling eliminates both style and calibration gains, indicating the hedge map drives the entire effect.
**Minimum test:** Blindly compare true-score, shuffled-score, no-score, and FUT conditions with identical rewriting model and prompt budget.

## 14. Deployment and governance considerations

### 14.1 Appropriate use

Near-term use is appropriate in offline model evaluation, supervised research assistants, and user interfaces where uncertainty is one of several evidence channels. Teams can use the method to compare uncertainty-expression behavior, identify examples for review, or prototype contextual hedge rendering.

Any deployment should preserve the numerical response, show source or verification options, and monitor correctness separately. The method is especially useful when users can choose to verify and the system can abstain or escalate.

### 14.2 Poor fit and failure modes

RLMF is a poor fit when repeated sampling is impossible, the judge is untrusted, latency is strict, confidence cannot be validated, or mistakes cause immediate high-impact action. It is not a standalone medical, legal, financial, employment, security, or physical-control safeguard.

Failure modes include proxy gaming, judge collusion, confidence-range collapse, malformed tags, test leakage, checkpoint over-selection, correlated samples, API version drift, rewrite factual changes, polished uncertainty that increases persuasion, and reviewer overload.

### 14.3 Required safeguards

1. Keep task correctness, factual calibration, faithful calibration, support width, judge disagreement, and shift status as independent release gates.
2. Use separate training, validation, and test data; pin model, judge, prompt, estimator, dependency, and dataset versions.
3. Retain original outputs and run semantic-preservation checks before accepting rewritten text.
4. Require human approval for high-impact communication and provide source verification or abstention paths.
5. Monitor user reliance and adverse outcomes, not only preference or hedge naturalness.

## 15. Replication and falsification agenda

### 15.1 Highest-priority unresolved result

The highest-priority result is the gap between RLMF and standard RL under independent evaluation. Reproduce one 1.7B or 4B configuration across at least five seeds, select checkpoints on validation data, and evaluate untouched test data with the original Qwen3-32B consistency proxy plus two independent estimators. Success requires a positive paired effect on faithful calibration without accuracy loss across most seeds. Failure on independent estimators falsifies the broad monitoring interpretation.

Archive configs, commit hashes, dataset revisions, prompt hashes, checkpoints, aggregate predictions, per-seed metrics, support curves, compute logs, and failed runs.

### 15.2 Reproduce the full pipeline

Reproduce pre-SFT parse validity first. Then generate and freeze metacognitive-selection scores. Train standard RL and RLMF with equal compute. Evaluate numerical confidence before invoking a rewriter. Only after numerical replication should Stage 2 be tested with true, shuffled, and absent score conditions.

Each stage needs an acceptance artifact: format test report, selection manifest, reward-unit tests, seed-level learning curves, untouched test metrics, rewrite semantic diffs, and human-study protocol.

### 15.3 Normalize evaluation

Use the same examples, generation budgets, decoding settings, judges, and metric implementations for all baselines. Report confidence support, bin counts, cMFG-star, original cMFG, accuracy, Brier score, refusal/abstention, parse failures, and cost. Proprietary comparisons should be labeled prompting-only and should not imply equivalent training access.

### 15.4 Test the proposed mechanism directly

Measure the correlation among `Z_g`, task difficulty, output length, parse quality, intrinsic-confidence support, correctness, and gradient magnitude. Replace `Z_g` with matched random multipliers, difficulty estimates, and judge-confidence controls. Test adversarial completions that accurately predict poor performance. These experiments distinguish diagnostic accuracy from generic example weighting.

### 15.5 Test realistic shifts and alternatives

Vary decoding temperature, sample count, NLI judge, model family, domain, and confidence support. Add long-form retrieval, tool-use, and multi-turn tasks with executable validators. Compare direct abstention, semantic entropy, logit confidence, verifier probabilities, MetaFaith, FUT, standard RL, and RLMF under a common split and compute budget.

For rewriting, test cultural and domain-specific communication norms, factual preservation, accessibility, and calibrated user reliance. Any high-stakes simulation should remain offline and supervised.

## 16. Durable restatement

> RLMF is a reinforcement-learning modification in which a model's accuracy at predicting its own faithful-calibration performance increases the learning weight of completions that are already above average on faithful uncertainty expression. The method reports large gains across tested Llama and Qwen models and preserves most gains after a separate language-rewriting stage. Its evidence is strongest as a demonstration of conditional self-evaluation for a sampled-consistency proxy; it does not establish direct access to latent belief, broad metacognition, or safety. Independent estimators, multiple seeds, clean validation, support-aware metrics, and rewrite-preservation tests are the decisive next checks.

What should be remembered is the control pattern. Self-assessment is valuable when it modifies how strongly verified success is learned, while remaining unable to redefine success. Numerical confidence should be treated as a versioned intermediate artifact, and linguistic uncertainty as a transformation that requires its own integrity check.
