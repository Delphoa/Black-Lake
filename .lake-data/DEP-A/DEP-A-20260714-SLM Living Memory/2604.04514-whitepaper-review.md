# SuperLocalMemory V3.3 Is a Lifecycle-Oriented Memory Stack, but Its Evidence Does Not Yet Validate a Cognitive Theory

## A detailed review, technical reconstruction, and independent re-conceptualization of “SuperLocalMemory V3.3: The Living Brain — Biologically-Inspired Forgetting, Cognitive Quantization, and Multi-Channel Retrieval for Zero-LLM Agent Memory Systems”

**Source paper:** Varun Pratap Bhardwaj, arXiv:2604.04514v1, submitted 6 April 2026.[^source-paper]

**Artifact prepared:** 2026-07-14
**Local source reviewed:** complete 19-page PDF and complete arXiv full-paper HTML; 4 numbered figures, 9 numbered tables, 12 central displayed equations, references, acknowledgments, and availability statement
**Review scope:** full-paper reconstruction, equation and claim audit, quantitative evaluation, external primary-source context, code/reproducibility assessment, independent re-conceptualization, and replication agenda
**Review status:** the paper and current official repository were inspected; the paper's experiments, package installation, benchmarks, and code were not independently rerun or reproduced

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

SuperLocalMemory V3.3 assembles local persistent storage, multi-channel retrieval, lifecycle-dependent forgetting and quantization, soft-prompt generation, and automatic developer-tool hooks into one agent-memory stack, but its strongest claims rest on small, author-run benchmarks and several definitions that encode the desired outcome rather than demonstrating general memory quality.

### The deeper idea

The durable contribution is a *resource-governed memory lifecycle*. Instead of treating every stored embedding as permanent and equally precise, the system assigns a strength, retention, state, precision tier, and trust-adjusted decay rate to each memory. Retrieval combines seven heterogeneous views, while consolidation converts repeated explicit records into compact prompts. In reviewer terms, this is a control plane over a local retrieval system: it decides which representations remain expensive and visible, which become compressed, which are promoted into behavioral context, and which are removed.

That framing is more defensible than the paper's “living brain” analogy. The implementation borrows names from cognitive psychology, but its transitions are engineered thresholds and schedules, not a validated model of biological memory. The analogy is useful for organizing functions—rehearsal, decay, consolidation, associative recall—but it should not be treated as evidence that the system has reproduced human memory mechanisms.

### Bottom-line judgment

The paper is unusually broad for a systems preprint. It specifies an operational architecture, exposes central formulas, reports resource measurements, acknowledges a 4.4 percentage-point regression against its predecessor, and gives concrete failure modes. The best-supported result is narrow: in an author-created mixed-precision test containing 18,840 query–fact pairs, the proposed Fisher–Rao Quantization-Aware Distance (FRQAD) always ranks the float32 copy ahead of its 4-bit copy, while cosine does so 85.6% of the time. That establishes the behavior of the scoring rule under its designed preference test. It does not establish better relevance retrieval: cosine has the higher reported top-50 Spearman correlation, 0.908 versus 0.806 for FRQAD.

The main LoCoMo headline is materially weaker than the abstract implies. The evaluation uses 2 of the benchmark's 10 conversations, 304 question–answer pairs, an Azure GPT-5.4-mini judge, and the best of five rounds. V3.3 reaches 70.4% in that setting, up from its own 62.8% baseline but below the predecessor's reported 74.8%. The gains are concentrated in multi-hop, temporal, and adversarial categories; single-hop performance remains 14.9 points below the predecessor. Because the benchmark's official publication describes a much larger long-conversation evaluation setting, the two-conversation subset and best-round selection limit generalization.[^locomo]

Several claims need sharper boundaries. “Memory parameterization” produces natural-language prompts; it does not update model parameters. “Provable convergence” applies only under the stated confining-potential, gradient-drift, and positive-temperature assumptions, not automatically to the discrete production scheduler. “100% precision” means a full-precision copy is deliberately preferred over a quantized copy, not that retrieval answers are perfectly relevant. “Complete cognitive taxonomy” is an architectural mapping, not a cognitive validation. The current public repository confirms an active implementation lineage, but it has advanced beyond the paper and currently reports a different version and license, so it cannot by itself reproduce v3.3.26.[^code]

### Principal strengths

- The paper connects storage precision, memory decay, retrieval weighting, and trust into one explicit lifecycle rather than presenting isolated retrieval tricks.
- It reports adverse results, including lower overall LoCoMo performance than V3.2, 68% mixed-precision recall@10, severe 2-bit degradation, and a 1.12 GB total process footprint despite a small main process.
- The architecture is concrete: local SQLite stores, seven channels, weighted reciprocal-rank fusion, ONNX reranking, daemonized embeddings, hooks, and explicit retention/precision states.
- The evaluation covers preference behavior, recall under quantization, memory footprint, simulated forgetting, persistence across sessions, and long-conversation QA.

### Principal qualifications

1. The empirical suite is author-run, mostly small, and often tests properties built into the definitions; there are no confidence intervals, independent training seeds, or external replications.
2. FRQAD's perfect fidelity preference is not a relevance metric, and its own rank-correlation result favors cosine.
3. LoCoMo uses only 2 of 10 conversations and a best-of-five round, so the 70.4% headline is not a full-benchmark estimate.
4. Key contributions—soft-prompt parameterization, seven-channel fusion as a whole, automatic hooks, compliance, and claimed production maturity—lack direct controlled evaluations.
5. The paper says 11 tables in arXiv comments, while the complete PDF/HTML contains 9 numbered table captions; this review follows the primary artifacts rather than the comment field.

## 1. The problem the paper is actually solving

### 1.1 Problem and stakes

The practical problem is cross-session memory for developer agents. A model may have a long context window during one interaction yet lose project conventions, prior decisions, and user preferences when the session ends. A persistent memory layer must capture observations, retrieve relevant records later, control growth, reject or down-weight unreliable content, and integrate with tools without forcing users to invoke memory commands manually.

The paper argues that common agent-memory systems treat this as static text storage plus vector search. In that model, every record remains at full precision, relevance is mostly semantic similarity, and stale facts accumulate. V3.3 instead treats memory as an evolving inventory. Access, importance, confirmation, emotion, and trust affect retention; retention determines lifecycle state; lifecycle state determines embedding precision; multiple retrieval channels expose semantic, lexical, graph, temporal, consolidated, and associative views.

This is a legitimate systems gap. Persistent memory needs retention policy and failure handling, not only nearest-neighbor search. The stronger claim—that no existing system implements any comparable forgetting, consolidation, or learning—requires a systematic review broader than the paper supplies. The paper reports a literature search but does not publish its query, databases, screening criteria, dates, or exclusion ledger.

### 1.2 Prior method families

The work inherits at least five families. Local vector and graph memories supply the basic storage and retrieval substrate. ACT-R and spreading-activation models motivate access- and association-sensitive recall. Data-oblivious vector quantization supplies random rotation and scalar codebooks. Modern Hopfield networks supply content-addressable pattern completion. Prompt-based personalization supplies the mechanism called parameterization.

TurboQuant is especially important. Its primary paper proposes random orthogonal rotation followed by scalar quantization, with near-optimal distortion bounds and experiments on KV cache and nearest-neighbor search.[^turboquant] V3.3's local contribution is not the underlying quantizer; it is the adaptation to persistent, mixed-precision memories and the coupling of precision to lifecycle state. The paper's cognitive taxonomy also relies on a survey that distinguishes text, KV-cache, parameter, and hidden-state memory families.[^cognitive-survey]

### 1.3 The paper's proposed gap

The paper claims five contributions: FRQAD and persistent TurboQuant; Ebbinghaus-style forgetting coupled to precision; seven-channel retrieval; conversion of consolidated patterns to soft prompts; and automatic lifecycle hooks. Of those, FRQAD and the retention-to-precision coupling are the clearest new formal proposals. Seven-channel retrieval is primarily integration: the paper says channels 1–5 appeared in earlier papers, consolidation was already built in V3.2 but unpublished, and Hopfield retrieval is new here. Soft prompts and hooks are product mechanisms whose novelty should be scoped to this stack.

## 2. Formal and technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Definitions and assumptions

A memory `m` has an embedding mean `theta`, a bit-width `b`, an observation variance, access count `a`, importance `iota`, confirmation count `gamma`, emotional salience `epsilon`, and source trust `tau`. The system assumes that quantization uncertainty can be represented as isotropic variance inflation around the embedding mean. It also assumes that a scalar strength function and exponential retention curve are suitable control signals for all record types.

These are engineering abstractions. Embedding error is not generally isotropic, record importance is not objectively observed, confirmation counts can reinforce coordinated falsehoods, and emotional salience is domain-dependent. The formulas are nevertheless useful because they make policy explicit and testable.

### 2.2 Objective and central equations

TurboQuant rotates a unit vector `x` with a stored random orthogonal matrix `Pi`, quantizes each rotated coordinate against a bit-width-specific Lloyd–Max codebook, then rotates the reconstruction back. The inherited mean-squared-error bound is

`D_mse <= sqrt(3*pi/2) * 4^(-b)`.

FRQAD represents memory `m_k` as `N(theta_k, sigma_eff,k^2 I)`, where

`sigma_eff,k^2 = sigma_obs^2(m_k) * (32 / b_k)^kappa`.

Distance is the Fisher–Rao geodesic between the two diagonal Gaussians. The full form combines mean separation and variance mismatch through an `arccosh` term. In plain language, a lower bit-width expands uncertainty; the metric is designed so that mismatched precision carries a penalty unavailable to cosine similarity.

The paper's Proposition 4.3 asserts monotonic degradation: reducing either memory's bit-width strictly increases FRQAD distance. The manuscript gives the statement but not a complete proof. Because Gaussian Fisher–Rao distance contains interacting mean and variance terms, the scope should be interpreted as the paper's parameterization and assumptions, not an established property of every quantization-noise model.

Memory strength is

`S(m) = max(S_min, alpha*log(1+a(m)) + beta*iota(m) + gamma_c*gamma(m) + delta*epsilon(m))`,

and retention is `R(m,t)=exp(-t/S(m))`. Retention thresholds map a memory to Active, Warm, Cold, Archive, or Forgotten. Precision then maps Active to 32-bit, Warm to 8-bit, Cold to 4-bit, and Archive to 2-bit. This creates a deterministic bridge from usage history to storage cost.

The continuous lifecycle extension adds a forgetting drift to a Riemannian stochastic differential equation. The stationary-density claim requires a confining combined potential, gradient-derived forgetting force, and positive effective temperature. Those are sufficient-condition statements. The production system still uses discrete batch schedules and thresholds, so the theorem does not prove that a deployed database will converge to a desirable retention distribution.

Trust modifies decay as `lambda_eff = lambda * (1 + kappa*(1-tau_source))`, with default `kappa=2`. A source with trust 1 decays normally; trust 0 decays three times faster. This reduces persistence but does not prevent initial retrieval, coordinated confirmation, or poisoning of high-trust sources.

Seven-channel outputs are fused with weighted reciprocal-rank fusion:

`score(m)=sum_c w_c / (k + rank_c(m))`, with `k=15`.

The paper says standard RRF commonly uses 60 and that 15 is optimized for candidate pools of 50–200 facts. It does not report the optimization protocol or held-out data, leaving open whether `k` and channel weights were tuned on the same LoCoMo subset used for reporting.

Modern Hopfield retrieval uses `xi_new = X^T softmax(beta X xi)` with `beta=1/sqrt(d)`. Pattern completion is complementary to semantic search, but the paper provides no isolated Hopfield ablation or false-association analysis.

Soft-prompt confidence is an evidence-saturation factor multiplied by the absolute deviation of an observed rate from 0.5. Patterns need confidence at least 0.7 and five observations. The resulting natural-language prompt is capped at 1,500 tokens and injected at session start. This is prompt conditioning, not a change to the language model's parameters.

### 2.3 Notation and implementation audit

The paper is readable but several notational issues matter. The stochastic update writes a square-root term containing `dt` and also a noise differential; whether `d eta_t` denotes a standard normal sample or a Wiener increment is ambiguous. The convergence sketch assumes a gradient-derived force without demonstrating that the implemented scheduler realizes that field. The FRQAD preference proposition lacks a detailed proof. Several equations disappear from arXiv's rendered text extraction but are present in the full HTML `alttext` and PDF.

The paper also states that all embeddings are float32 by default in the current release, making FRQAD and standard Fisher–Rao identical until mixed-precision scheduling is enabled. Therefore the headline FRQAD advantage applies to an optional lifecycle state, not necessarily the default path.

## 3. Architecture and information flow

### 3.1 Representation

The storage layer uses SQLite databases. `memory.db` holds facts, graph structure, embeddings, time fields, quantized representations, prompts, and forgetting schedules. `learning.db` records behavioral signals. `code_graph.db` represents code entities and relations. A sentence-transformer produces 768-dimensional embeddings, and sqlite-vec supplies semantic KNN.

Figure 1 divides the system into interface, engine, and storage layers. The interface exposes MCP, CLI, dashboard, and developer hooks. The engine organizes encoding, retrieval, lifecycle, and learning pipelines. The paper reports 17 packages, 215 source modules, 60 MCP tools, and a 27-file code-graph module with 385 tests. These are paper-reported inventory counts, not independently reconstructed from the exact v3.3.26 tag.

### 3.2 Decision or action modules

The retrieval channels are semantic KNN (weight 1.2), BM25 (1.0), entity graph (1.0), temporal (1.0), spreading activation (1.0), consolidation (0.8), and Hopfield (0.8). Multi-hop queries trigger an intersection between entity and temporal results before RRF, followed by an ONNX cross-encoder. This conditional intersection is presented as the mechanism behind the multi-hop gain.

The lifecycle controller computes decay, changes precision tiers, consolidates related memories, generates prompts, and garbage-collects forgotten records. Trust scores modulate the decay rate. The automatic hook pipeline recalls at startup, observes file changes with rate limits, saves at session end, and runs maintenance between sessions.

### 3.3 Dependencies and post-processing

The system depends on sentence-transformers, ONNX runtime, SQLite/sqlite-vec, tree-sitter, rustworkx, and platform-specific hooks. The cross-encoder is `ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2`. The paper's local-first claim is credible for Mode A's inference topology, but model downloads, package installation, updates, and optional modes still create supply-chain and network considerations.

The current official repository is inspectable and active, but it now reports a later package version, an AGPL-3.0-or-later license, and evolved channel/tool counts. The paper reports v3.3.26 under Elastic License 2.0. Without an immutable, inspected v3.3.26 release artifact, current-main code supports project continuity but not exact reproduction.[^code]

## 4. The complete operational pipeline

### 4.1 End-to-end flow

At session start, hooks load project context, retrieved memories, consolidated patterns, and prompts. During work, observations enter an encoding pipeline that extracts facts, resolves entities, applies an entropy gate, attaches emotion and provenance, updates graph structure, and chooses an ADD/UPDATE/SUPERSEDE/NOOP action. Retrieval runs channels in parallel, fuses ranks, optionally applies multi-hop intersection, reranks candidates, and returns context. At session end, a summary is stored. Between sessions, maintenance recomputes retention, changes precision, consolidates clusters, generates prompts, and removes or archives cold records.

### 4.2 Resource allocation

The daemon keeps a warm engine on localhost and shuts down after 30 minutes of idleness. Table 2 reports cold CLI recall falling from 19 seconds to 0.6 seconds, warm recall from 1.0 to 0.6 seconds, asynchronous remember from 0.3 to 0.1 seconds, and cold MCP recall from 23 seconds to 1 second. These are large author-reported speedups, but hardware, repetition count, dispersion, and load are not supplied in the table.

Table 6 reports 63.3 MB RSS for the main process and 1,058.9 MB for the embedding worker, totaling 1,122.2 MB. The main-process framing is therefore accurate but incomplete: Mode A still needs roughly 1.1 GB when the worker is live. Auto-kill after two minutes can reduce idle cost while reintroducing warm-up latency.

### 4.3 Guarantee boundary

SQLite persistence provides deterministic durability only to the extent that transactions, filesystems, migrations, and backups behave correctly. Session continuity's 10/10 test is a smoke test, not a durability guarantee. The stochastic convergence theorem is conditional. Quantization bounds are inherited for vector distortion, not end-to-end answer quality. Silent hook failure preserves developer workflow but can make memory capture incomplete without visible alarms.

## 5. Re-conceptualization

### 5.1 A retention-and-fidelity control plane

The most useful generalization is to view V3.3 as a control plane that allocates representational fidelity according to expected future value. A memory's lifecycle state simultaneously changes its retrieval prior, precision, and deletion risk. This resembles a cache hierarchy with semantic admission and trust-aware eviction more than a biological brain.

**Boundary of the analogy:** hardware caches have explicit hit costs and reproducible objects; semantic memories are lossy, mutable, and socially sourced. A low-access memory may be legally or operationally critical. Therefore access frequency cannot stand alone as value.

### 5.2 Retrieval as a portfolio of correlated estimators

The seven channels can be read as estimators with different biases. Semantic KNN favors paraphrase, BM25 exact vocabulary, graph traversal relations, temporal scoring recency, consolidation abstraction, and Hopfield completion partial patterns. RRF is a portfolio rule that limits domination by any one score scale.

**Boundary of the analogy:** the channels are not independent. Entity, semantic, spreading-activation, and consolidation outputs can share the same source facts. Counting agreement as separate evidence can amplify one erroneous record. Fusion should model correlation and provenance, not only rank.

### 5.3 Transferable design principle

The transferable principle is: *separate evidence retention from representation cost*. Keep an auditable source record even when its retrieval representation is compressed or regenerated. V3.3's public description couples forgetting to representation precision, but high-stakes deployments should preserve source provenance in a protected ledger and treat embeddings, prompts, and graph summaries as disposable indexes.

## 6. Experimental design reconstructed

### 6.1 Data

FRQAD uses 943 facts, 768-dimensional embeddings, and 18,840 query–fact comparisons where each fact appears in float32 and 4-bit form. Mixed-precision recall uses 929 facts, split 50% float32, 30% 4-bit, and 20% 2-bit, with 20 queries. Forgetting simulates 170 facts over 30 days: 20 hot, 50 warm, and 100 cold. Session continuity stores 10 diverse facts across an engine restart.

LoCoMo is the only external task-level benchmark. The paper uses 2 of 10 conversations, 304 QA pairs, and 1,585 ingested facts. Five-turn chunks are ingested. An Azure GPT-5.4-mini judge scores answers on a 1–5 Likert scale, with 4 as the pass threshold. The reported Round 3 is the best of five rounds.

### 6.2 Training and hardware

The work is primarily a system evaluation, not a newly trained foundation model. The paper does not provide a unified hardware table, repeated-run timing statistics, energy use, or total tuning cost. Mode A is CPU-oriented, but the exact machine behind each benchmark and whether caches were warm are not consistently specified.

### 6.3 Baselines

FRQAD is compared with cosine and standard Fisher–Rao under a preference label defined as choosing float32 over 4-bit. Mixed-precision recall compares against an all-float32 result. LoCoMo compares a V3.3 retrieval baseline, V3.3 Round 3, V3.2, and reported scores for Mem0, Letta, and Zep.

The cross-system table is not controlled: cloud use, splits, judges, prompts, and model backends differ. The current project README itself now warns that such scores are not apples-to-apples. The most defensible comparison is within the same V3.3 run setup, and even there best-of-five selection needs a mean and variance.

### 6.4 Metrics

“Prefers f32” measures fidelity bias, not relevance. Spearman correlation captures ranking agreement with an inner-product ground truth. Recall@10 compares retrieved sets. Cosine fidelity measures reconstruction direction. RSS reports process memory. Retention `R` and strength `S` are outputs of the designed simulator. LoCoMo accuracy is judge-conditioned and should include judge-repeat or human-agreement uncertainty.

### 6.5 Runtime semantics

Daemon timings are per operation under an unspecified measurement protocol. Memory RSS separates the main process from the model worker but reports their total. No p50/p95 latency, concurrent load, database size scaling, or long-running fragmentation result appears. The claim “entirely on CPU” describes device placement, not low total resource demand.

## 7. Results: what is reported and what it means

### 7.1 Quantization and retrieval fidelity

Table 4 reports float32 preference of 16,127/18,840 (85.6%) for cosine, 13,316/18,840 (70.7%) for standard Fisher–Rao, and 18,840/18,840 (100%) for FRQAD. Figure 2 visualizes the same result. Top-50 Spearman correlation is 0.908 for cosine, 0.173 for standard Fisher–Rao, and 0.806 for FRQAD. Mean 4-bit MSE is `1.603e-5`, and mean cosine degradation is 0.006.

**Interpretation:** FRQAD reliably enforces the chosen policy that lower-precision copies should be penalized.

**Qualification:** a relevant quantized memory may be better than an irrelevant full-precision memory. The benchmark duplicates facts across precisions, so it does not measure mixed-content answer relevance or whether the penalty is calibrated.

Table 5 reports all-float32 recall@10 of 1.000 and mixed recall@10 of 0.680. Four-bit cosine fidelity is 0.994; 2-bit is 0.801. Calling the 68% result “without meaningful recall loss” is too strong without a task utility threshold. A 32-point recall decrease may be unacceptable for critical facts.

### 7.2 Forgetting, persistence, and resource use

At simulated day 30, hot facts have mean retention 0.345 and strength 11.28, warm facts 0.165 and 6.67, and cold facts 0.000 and 1.69. Hot facts land at 4-bit, warm at 2-bit, and cold are deleted. Figure 3 shows the designed curves.

**Interpretation:** the formula separates the three synthetic access schedules.

**Qualification:** the 6.7 ratio is produced by parameterized behavior on constructed groups. It does not demonstrate that the schedule improves downstream retrieval, avoids deletion of rare critical facts, or matches human forgetting.

The session test stores and recalls 10 facts across a restart, all at rank 1. This confirms a basic persistence path. It does not test corruption, migration, simultaneous writers, partial failure, or month-scale drift.

### 7.3 LoCoMo

V3.3's baseline versus Round 3 is: single-hop 60.5% to 65.1%; multi-hop 25.4% to 49.2%; temporal 38.5% to 53.8%; open-domain 86.8% to 82.5%; adversarial 63.4% to 76.1%; overall 62.8% to 70.4%. Figure 4 visualizes category differences.

The multi-hop and temporal improvements fit the proposed entity–temporal intersection mechanism. Open-domain declines by 4.3 points, and the predecessor remains better overall at 74.8%. The paper attributes the gap to fusion complexity, which is plausible but not causally established without matched routing ablations.

## 8. Ablations and causal evidence

The paper does not provide a full component-ablation table for all five claimed contributions. FRQAD is compared as a scoring rule, but no downstream LoCoMo ablation replaces it with cosine under mixed precision. The seven-channel stack lacks isolated leave-one-channel-out results. Soft-prompt parameterization has no behavioral benchmark. Automatic hooks have no adoption, loss, or failure-rate study. Trust-weighted decay has no poisoning experiment.

The strongest causal-looking evidence is the category improvement after the Round 3 retrieval changes and the designed fidelity preference. Yet Round 3 bundles changes, and best-round selection obscures expected performance. A rigorous ablation would fix facts, queries, embeddings, judge, and seed while varying one channel, fusion rule, bit penalty, or intersection policy at a time.

### Randomness and statistical evidence

No confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, or multi-seed distributions are reported for the central benchmarks. The FRQAD pair count is large, but pairs share facts and queries, so they are not necessarily independent observations. LoCoMo's unit of generalization should include conversations, not only 304 questions. Reporting two conversation-level scores is essential; averaging question outcomes can understate between-conversation variance.

## 10. External views and field context

### 10.1 Predecessor evidence

The official V3 predecessor record claims Fisher–Rao retrieval, stochastic lifecycle dynamics, contradiction detection, and 74.8% Mode A performance.[^predecessor] V3.3 is therefore an extension and product integration, not the origin of every mathematical component. The predecessor relation is explicit and should anchor novelty claims.

TurboQuant establishes the random-rotation/scalar-quantization basis and its near-optimal distortion result.[^turboquant] V3.3's differentiator is applying a related quantizer to persistent stores and tying bit-width to retention. The memory taxonomy survey confirms that parameter-based memory is normally associated with methods such as LoRA, test-time training, or mixture-of-experts changes; this weakens the paper's use of “parameterization” for natural-language templates.[^cognitive-survey]

### 10.2 Independent evaluation perspective

The official LoCoMo publication describes 10 long conversations of roughly 600 turns and 16K tokens on average, with QA, summarization, and multimodal tasks.[^locomo] V3.3 evaluates two conversations and QA only. The paper's result is useful as a development probe but not equivalent to the benchmark's complete evaluation surface.

### 10.3 Contemporaneous or later alternatives

The paper cites memory consolidation, graph retrieval, and prompt-based systems but does not compare against a common-backbone, common-split suite. The most informative future comparison would hold the embedder, database, conversation subset, judge, and token budget constant across vector-only, graph, learned routing, text summarization, and V3.3 lifecycle variants.

### 10.4 Publication, code, and reproducibility status

The work is an arXiv v1 preprint with an arXiv DOI and a linked Zenodo record; no conference or journal acceptance was verified. The official repository is public and currently active. Current main reports version 3.5.7 and AGPL-3.0-or-later, while the paper reports v3.3.26 under Elastic License 2.0. The repository README also now gives different feature/channel counts and explicitly cautions that cross-system LoCoMo scores are not directly comparable.[^code]

The project is therefore **artifact available and inspectable at current main**, but the exact paper release was **not independently reproduced**. No installation, tests, package execution, benchmark run, or result regeneration occurred in this review.

## 11. Research notes and critical considerations

### 11.1 Internal consistency

The arXiv comment says “4 figures, 11 tables,” while the complete PDF/HTML exposes 4 numbered figures and 9 numbered tables. The abstract says seven channels, while sections identify only Hopfield as newly introduced here and consolidation as previously built. The paper says current default embeddings remain float32, which means lifecycle quantization and FRQAD's distinct behavior may be opt-in rather than baseline.

The license and version differ from current repository main. That may reflect legitimate later relicensing and development, but it makes immutable release provenance important. Claims about 3,000+ tests, exact tool counts, and release behavior should be tied to a tag and commit.

### 11.2 Metric and baseline concerns

Preference for float32 is a policy target, not a relevance ground truth. The 68% mixed recall result needs task-level impact. The forgetting ratio is parameter-generated. Session continuity is only 10 facts. Cross-system LoCoMo scores mix configurations. Within V3.3, best-of-five Round 3 should be accompanied by all-round mean, standard deviation, and predeclared selection.

### 11.3 Unsupported or overbroad claims

“Zero prior art,” “first,” “complete taxonomy,” “provable convergence,” “production-grade,” and compliance statements are broader than the supplied evidence. The paper's engineering may be valuable without those superlatives. Rephrasing them as scoped proposals would improve credibility.

### 11.4 Alternative explanations

Multi-hop gains may come from query classification, entity–temporal intersection, judge variation, or tuning on the selected conversations rather than from seven-channel cognitive architecture generally. FRQAD's 100% result may be driven by explicit variance penalty, not better semantic geometry. Forgetting separation follows directly from access schedules and chosen coefficients. Soft prompts may help because they repeatedly restate preferences, not because explicit memory became implicit.

### 11.5 Unresolved questions

- How were the two LoCoMo conversations selected, and what are per-conversation and all-five-round scores?
- Which v3.3.26 commit, lockfile, dataset snapshot, and benchmark script produced each table?
- How often does a low-bit relevant memory lose to a full-precision distractor under FRQAD?
- What is the calibration cost of strength coefficients and lifecycle thresholds across users and domains?
- Can deletion be proven to remove information from consolidated prompts, graphs, backups, and derived embeddings?
- What happens when multiple retrieval channels agree because they descend from the same poisoned fact?

## 12. Potential implications

### 12.1 Scientific implications

The paper motivates evaluation of memory systems as controlled lifecycles rather than static stores. That is a productive direction: retention, representation precision, trust, retrieval, and consolidation should be measured jointly. It also exposes a useful tension between complex-query recall and single-hop precision.

### 12.2 System-design implications

Multi-channel fusion should include provenance diversity and channel correlation. Retention policies should protect rare, high-consequence facts. Mixed precision should be calibrated against downstream task loss, not only vector fidelity. Background maintenance should report visible health, not fail silently without an audit trail.

### 12.3 Broader or cross-domain implications

The lifecycle pattern transfers to retrieval systems, personal knowledge bases, incident archives, and edge databases. The risk also transfers: an access-frequency policy can erase low-frequency minority evidence, while confirmation counts can reward repeated misinformation. Governance needs protected classes of memory and reversible compression.

## 13. New hypotheses derived from the paper

These are reviewer inferences, not established findings.

### Hypothesis 1: Provenance-diverse fusion will outperform rank-only fusion under poisoning

**Proposition:** discounting correlated channels that descend from the same source record will reduce poisoned-memory amplification without materially reducing clean recall.
**Motivating evidence:** V3.3 fuses seven channels, several derived from shared facts, but evaluates no poisoning case.
**Predicted observation:** source-diversity-aware fusion retains clean LoCoMo accuracy while improving adversarial source injection tests.
**Falsifying observation:** correlated-channel discounting consistently lowers clean and adversarial accuracy with no poisoning benefit.
**Minimum test:** create matched clean/poisoned stores, track source lineage per result, compare weighted RRF with lineage-aware fusion over five seeds.

### Hypothesis 2: FRQAD needs relevance-conditioned precision penalties

**Proposition:** a fixed monotonic penalty for low bit-width will reduce recall when relevant old memories compete with high-precision recent distractors.
**Motivating evidence:** FRQAD perfectly enforces precision preference, while cosine has higher rank correlation and mixed recall falls to 0.680.
**Predicted observation:** query- or uncertainty-conditioned penalties outperform a fixed penalty on mixed-age relevance tasks.
**Falsifying observation:** fixed FRQAD dominates calibrated alternatives across relevance, age, and bit-width shifts.
**Minimum test:** construct queries where relevance and precision are independently varied, report nDCG, recall, calibration, and failure cost.

### Hypothesis 3: Dynamic channel routing will recover single-hop precision

**Proposition:** simple queries should use fewer channels and complex queries more, reducing the reported single-hop regression without losing multi-hop gains.
**Motivating evidence:** V3.3 improves multi-hop and temporal categories but trails V3.2 by 14.9 points on single-hop.
**Predicted observation:** predeclared routing improves single-hop while keeping multi-hop within one confidence interval of the full stack.
**Falsifying observation:** routing adds classifier error and lowers overall performance across conversation-level resampling.
**Minimum test:** compare fixed seven-channel, oracle, heuristic, and learned routing on all ten LoCoMo conversations.

## 14. Deployment and governance considerations

### 14.1 Appropriate use

The design is appropriate for local developer assistance where records are low-to-moderate risk, users control the machine, memory can be inspected and reset, and loss of a stale fact is recoverable. It is also useful as an experimental platform for lifecycle policies.

### 14.2 Poor fit and failure modes

It is a poor fit for medical, legal, financial, security, or personnel decisions unless every derived memory has provenance, retention is policy-controlled, and outputs are independently verified. Failure modes include rare-fact deletion, stale prompt injection, semantic collision, graph poisoning, channel double counting, quantization bias, silent hook loss, model-download supply-chain risk, and incomplete erasure of derived artifacts.

### 14.3 Required safeguards

1. Maintain an append-only, access-controlled provenance ledger separate from disposable embeddings and prompts.
2. Make deletion and correction propagate through graph edges, consolidated prompts, quantized copies, backups, and caches, with auditable verification.
3. Expose per-channel results, source identities, retention state, precision, and trust to operators.
4. Use protected-memory policies for rare high-consequence facts and explicit legal holds.
5. Monitor retrieval quality by lifecycle tier, bit-width, source trust, and query class; fall back to readable source records when confidence is low.

## 15. Replication and falsification agenda

### 15.1 Highest-priority unresolved result

Reproduce LoCoMo on all ten conversations with a frozen v3.3.26 commit, immutable data snapshot, predeclared channel configuration, all five rounds, repeated judge calls, and human audit. Report per-conversation means, confidence intervals, category counts, token/runtime cost, and raw predictions. Success means the 70.4% subset result is not an outlier and complex-query gains persist; falsification is a large collapse or reversal on the remaining conversations.

### 15.2 Reproduce the full pipeline

Archive the exact package tag, dependency lock, database schema, embedder, cross-encoder, seeds, benchmark scripts, generated facts, judge prompts, and outputs. Run installation, unit tests, session capture, decay, quantization, consolidation, prompt injection, restart, deletion, and backup/restore. This review did not execute those steps.

### 15.3 Normalize evaluation

Use common LoCoMo conversations, common judge and repeat policy, equal ingest chunks, equal token budgets, and identical backends for vector-only, BM25, graph, four-channel predecessor, seven-channel V3.3, and routed variants. For compression, vary relevance and precision independently. For forgetting, include rare critical facts and distribution shifts.

### 15.4 Test the proposed mechanism directly

Measure whether entity–temporal intersection causes multi-hop gains; whether Hopfield retrieval completes partial cues without hallucinated associations; whether trust-weighted decay suppresses poisoned sources; whether consolidation preserves corrections; and whether low-bit embeddings lose relevance disproportionately. Leave-one-component-out and interaction ablations should use at least five seeds.

### 15.5 Test realistic shifts and alternatives

Evaluate multilingual projects, codebase migrations, renamed entities, contradictory requirements, long inactivity, multiple users, clock errors, database corruption, version upgrades, model changes, and adversarial tool outputs. Report storage growth, p50/p95 latency, worker warm-up, energy, backup size, and deletion completeness over simulated months.

## 16. Durable restatement

> SuperLocalMemory V3.3 is best understood as a local retrieval system with an explicit retention-and-fidelity control plane. It computes memory strength from usage and metadata, maps retention to lifecycle and precision tiers, fuses several retrieval views, converts consolidated patterns into prompts, and automates these operations through developer hooks. The paper shows that these mechanisms can be implemented and that selected subcomponents behave as designed. It does not yet show that the lifecycle is optimal, biologically faithful, fully reproducible, or generally superior across the complete benchmark.

What should endure is the design question the paper raises: agent memory quality depends not only on what is stored and retrieved, but on how evidence ages, how representation cost changes, how trust affects retention, and how derived memories preserve provenance. The next step is to replace superlative claims with frozen artifacts, complete benchmarks, causal ablations, calibrated deletion policies, and source-aware fusion.
