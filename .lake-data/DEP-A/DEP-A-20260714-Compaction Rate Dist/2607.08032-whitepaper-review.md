# Memory Compaction Needs an Access Model, Not Only a Rate–Distortion Metaphor

## A full-paper review and independent re-conceptualization of “What to Keep, What to Forget: A Rate–Distortion View of Memory Compaction in LLMs and Agents”

**Paper:** Ashwin Gerard Colaco and Nada Lahjouji, arXiv:2607.08032v1, 9 July 2026.[^1]  
**Scope:** complete 24-page paper, all main sections and limitations, six numbered tables, six numbered figures, two numbered equations, appendix master table, references, and public-source vetting.  
**Review activity:** source reconstruction and claim auditing. No experiment was independently rerun.

---

## Executive assessment

### One-sentence assessment

The paper supplies a useful cross-layer vocabulary for memory compaction and a genuinely valuable repeated-compaction test, but its formal lower bound, query-entropy penalty, and “reversibility dominates” language are substantially broader than the demonstrated assumptions and experiments support.

### What the paper contributes

The paper surveys four historically separate families: KV-cache compression, prompt/context compression, bounded architectural state, and agent or semantic memory. It argues that every family implements the same abstract pair: a compaction operator maps history into a smaller representation, and a usage operator produces task output from that representation and the query. It then classifies methods along seven axes, highlights reversibility and query-conditioning, transfers mechanisms across memory tiers, proposes COMPACT-Bench, and reports two small reference experiments.

That synthesis is the strongest part. A single budget unit such as bytes per token of original history forces comparisons that the literature often evades. The paper is also right to insist that an agent repeatedly summarizing its own summaries is not evaluated by a one-shot long-context benchmark. Its second reference experiment makes the gap vivid: archival retrieval remains near 0.95 fact recall while repeated irreversible summaries retain only 0.33–0.56.

### Bottom-line judgment

As a survey and design manifesto, the work is high-value. Its seven-axis table, three-tier bridge, and repeated-compaction protocol are reusable. As a theoretical paper, it needs qualification. Equation 2 is a coarse Fano-style statement for a particular answer-space and information accounting setup, not a directly operational lower bound for heterogeneous cache, prompt, recurrent, and agent systems. The budget is alternately physical bytes, token count, state dimension, or store size, yet the proof treats it like an information capacity in bits. Those currencies are not interchangeable without a coding or channel model.

The claimed query-agnostic penalty of “roughly `B - H(Q)`” is presented as a consequence but is not derived with the precision implied. Query entropy can be enormous while the information about which history bits matter is small, or vice versa. The relevant quantity is closer to conditional rate–distortion or value of query side information, not query entropy by itself. Reversibility also has a price: archive storage, retrieval latency, index error, stale or unsafe records, and privacy obligations. A reversible operator can dominate an irreversible one only under an expanded budget that includes those costs and only when retrieval succeeds.

Finally, the experiments are illustrative rather than validating the full synthesis. One uses a 1.5B model on one 8 GB GPU and a synthetic needle task; the other compares two deliberately different memory regimes. Neither crosses all four layers, and the paper provides no discoverable code link despite saying code and configurations accompany the survey. The appropriate status is a strong conceptual framework with a promising benchmark seed, not a verified universal law.

## 1. Problem framing and research questions

The motivating problem is that context-derived memory grows at several timescales. During decoding, KV tensors grow with sequence length. Before inference, prompts can exceed model or serving budgets. Architectures with recurrence must squeeze history into fixed state. Agents accumulate tool traces, dialogue, and cross-session facts. Different communities describe different mechanisms—eviction, quantization, gisting, recurrence, summarization, retrieval—but every mechanism decides which information survives and at what fidelity.

The paper asks five useful questions:

1. Can these mechanisms be expressed through one constrained objective?
2. Which axes distinguish their behavior despite different substrates?
3. Which failure modes recur across layers?
4. Can mechanisms be transferred between inference memory and agent memory?
5. What benchmark would compare them on a common budget and measure compounding loss?

The scope excludes weight compression and unconstrained long-context training. This is sensible, although parametric memory re-enters the theory section as a comparison. The survey emphasizes work from 2023–2026 and includes preprints, making provenance and reproduction boundaries particularly important.

The central research claim is not that every representation is identical. It is that each can be evaluated by downstream task loss under a resource constraint. That abstraction is sound. The harder question is whether the same numeric rate and the same theorem can span physical memory, token budgets, state dimension, archived storage, and retrieval compute. The paper assumes more unity at this level than it establishes.

## 2. Formal and technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Compaction and usage operators

Let `H` contain all usable history: prompt tokens, activations, KV entries, recurrent state, and prior-session memory. A compaction operator `Cθ` maps `H` to representation `Z`. A usage operator `U` consumes `Z` and query `Q` to predict `Ŷ`. Equation 1 is:

`minθ E[ℓ(U(Cθ(H), Q), Y)]  subject to rate(Z) ≤ B`.

In plain language, choose the compaction policy that minimizes expected task loss while keeping the compact state within budget `B`. This is a good engineering objective because it makes the downstream task—not reconstruction fidelity alone—the criterion. It also exposes that the decoder or retrieval mechanism is part of the scheme. A token summary and an archived raw transcript cannot be compared by summary size alone if the latter pays index and retrieval costs.

The information-bottleneck restatement seeks to maximize `I(Z;Y|Q)` while limiting information retained about `H`. The classic Information Bottleneck primary paper indeed defines a short code that preserves information relevant to another variable, supporting this conceptual lineage.[^2] But mutual information is not physical bytes unless the representation and coding distribution are specified. An fp16 KV cache, a discrete prompt, and a vector database entry can have the same nominal storage while different effective capacity, decoder complexity, and noise.

### 2.2 Task-conditioned information content

The paper defines `I*(Q) = I(Y;H|Q)` as the amount of history information needed for the answer. This is useful shorthand but should not be described as an instance-specific number of required bits without care: mutual information is distributional, conditional on how `H`, `Q`, and `Y` are sampled. It may also include answer information already present in model weights unless the conditional variables cleanly separate parametric knowledge from context evidence.

The paper’s strongest practical consequence survives this qualification: compressibility is task-dependent. Redundant prose for a low-entropy classification task can be compressed more aggressively than exact multi-key retrieval. A single average score can conceal severe tail failures on exact identifiers, negations, safety constraints, or tool arguments.

### 2.3 The Fano-style lower bound

Equation 2 states:

`Pe ≥ [H(Y|Q) - B - 1] / log |𝒴|`, when `B < I*(Q)`.

The intended chain is that `Ŷ` is a function of `(Z,Q)`, data processing bounds answer information carried through `Z`, and Fano’s inequality converts insufficient mutual information into classification error. The direction is familiar, but four conditions matter.

First, `B` must upper-bound information in `Z`, not merely count tokens, dimensions, or allocated bytes. Second, `𝒴` must be a finite answer space with a defined error event; free-form generation and graded losses require a different formulation. Third, the Markov assumptions depend on what is included in model weights, tools, and retrieval infrastructure. Fourth, Fano bounds are often loose and do not predict the empirical collapse point without estimating the relevant distribution.

Thus Equation 2 supports the qualitative statement “insufficient answer-relevant capacity forces error.” It does not prove that every layer has the same practical error curve or that a quarter-budget collapse in one needle test measures `I*(Q)`.

### 2.4 Query-conditioning penalty

The paper argues that a query-agnostic operator spreads its budget over possible queries and pays a gap related to `H(Q)`. This captures a real phenomenon: once the query is known, compression can retain query-relevant evidence. LongLLMLingua-style query-aware selection and dynamic sparse retrieval exploit that advantage.

The quantitative statement needs repair. Entropy of the raw query is not generally the price of ignorance. Many differently worded queries select the same evidence; then `H(Q)` can be high while the relevant side-information value is low. Conversely, a one-bit query can select between two disjoint, high-volume evidence regions. A safer formulation is that query-conditioned and query-agnostic rate–distortion functions differ by the value of query side information under the joint task distribution.

### 2.5 Seven-axis taxonomy

The seven axes cover mechanism, granularity, lifecycle stage, reversibility, query adaptivity, fidelity profile, and optimization signal. Tables 2 and 6 apply them across layers. The taxonomy’s practical value is that it separates frequently conflated decisions. Quantization changes fidelity but normally retains addressability. Eviction removes addressability. Sparse retrieval may keep the complete cache but read selectively. Summarization changes both content and provenance. These mechanisms can occupy the same byte point while producing different tail risks.

The axes should add at least two columns in future versions: access cost and externalized storage. “Reversible” is not binary if restoration requires a slow remote archive or an imperfect embedding retriever. Privacy, retention, and authorization are also missing from a taxonomy that extends to long-term agent memory.

## 3. Architecture and pipeline across memory tiers

The survey’s pipeline has three conceptual tiers.

At the inference tier, KV methods evict tokens, retrieve blocks dynamically, quantize elements, factorize hidden dimensions, share across layers, or merge tokens. The important distinction is between deleting content and retaining it in a cheaper or selectively accessed form. Table 3 lists representative savings but warns that heterogeneous setups are not directly comparable.

At the context tier, hard compaction extracts or rewrites text, while soft compaction learns latent tokens. Hard methods remain inspectable but can lose dependencies and qualifications. Soft representations can be compact and task-trained but are decoder-specific and difficult to audit. Both can be query-agnostic or query-aware.

At the architectural tier, recurrent or state-space systems bound memory by construction. They exchange an expanding addressable store for a fixed-dimensional state. The survey correctly connects this to exact-recall limits: bounded state is excellent when the task admits a sufficient statistic and risky when arbitrary past items must remain individually addressable.

At the agent tier, working memory is periodically trimmed and cross-session memories are consolidated. Here, lifecycle and governance become central. An agent may preserve a claim but lose its source, time, uncertainty, or superseding correction. Repeated compaction makes this damage cumulative.

The bridge section maps five knobs across tiers: salience signal, retain/drop action, fidelity level, recovery path, and stopping criterion. This is a productive design device. For example, error-bound stopping rules from KV allocation could inspire a summary policy that stops deleting once a validation set of commitments would be lost. Conversely, an archival agent-memory design suggests keeping evicted inference blocks on a slower tier rather than deleting them.

## 4. Experimental design and evidence reconstructed

### 4.1 COMPACT-Bench proposal

The paper proposes one bytes-per-token-of-history axis and three evaluation requirements: sweep multiple budgets, compare mechanisms across layers, and test repeated compaction. Table 5 argues that existing benchmarks satisfy at most parts of this combination.

Bytes per original token is more informative than nominal compression ratio, but it remains incomplete. The benchmark also needs decode latency distribution, prefill cost, archive bytes, index bytes, network transfer, energy, and accuracy conditioned on answer type and evidence position. For soft prompts or recurrent state, the conversion to “retained KV bytes” can imply a common currency while hiding different computation.

### 4.2 Experiment 1: accuracy–budget frontier

The first experiment uses Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct on an RTX 4060 with 8 GB. Natural Wikitext filler contains a unique key–value needle at depths from 10% to 90% and lengths from 2k to 8k tokens. Six KV strategies—SnapKV, StreamingLLM, TOVA, K-norm, expected-attention scoring, and random eviction—are evaluated at five budgets with three trials. The paper reports 1,395 generations.

The full cache reaches 1.00 accuracy. All methods approach zero below about one-quarter of the full budget. Random eviction is competitive at high budget and collapses fastest as the budget tightens. This is credible evidence that learned or heuristic scoring matters under pressure and that one BPT axis can plot heterogeneous KV choices.

It is not a direct measurement of an information-theoretic threshold. A needle occupies few tokens, so a good query-conditioned retriever could retain it at far below 25% if it knows the question. The collapse may reflect the selected scorers, prompt format, observation window, or small-model attention behavior. Exact per-method plotted values are not stated in prose, uncertainty intervals are absent, and the code/configuration link is missing.

### 4.3 Experiment 2: repeated compaction

An agent reads a document in chunks containing twelve key–value facts. One operator repeatedly overwrites working memory with a model-generated summary. The other archives every chunk and retrieves relevant raw material at query time. At the end, all facts are queried.

Retrieval-backed recall remains near 0.95. Irreversible-summary recall lies between 0.33 and 0.56 and is weakest under the most frequent compaction. This is the paper’s most important empirical result because it measures a lifecycle absent from typical long-context tests.

The comparison is also confounded by total storage. The retrieval system keeps every raw chunk, while the summary system discards it. Their active-context budgets may be similar, but total retained bytes and privacy exposure are not. The result supports “archival recovery beats deletion when archive cost is excluded,” not unconditional dominance of reversibility.

## 5. Results reconstruction

The paper’s demonstrated results are narrow and should be stated exactly:

- One small open model achieves perfect needle accuracy with its full cache in the reported setup.
- Six compression/scoring choices lose nearly all needle accuracy below roughly 25% full-cache budget.
- Random eviction falls fastest at tight budgets, showing value in structured scoring.
- Retrieval from a retained archive holds twelve-fact recall around 0.95 across tested compaction frequencies.
- Repeated summary-over-summary retains 0.33–0.56, with greater loss at more frequent compaction.

The survey-level conclusions—that all layers share an objective, that query-agnostic irreversible deletion is a recurring risk, and that repeated compaction is undermeasured—are supported conceptually and by literature organization. The universal quantitative claims are not established by two reference experiments.

## 6. Ablations and controls audit

The random-eviction line is the principal control in Experiment 1, while the full cache is the upper anchor. The multiple context lengths, needle depths, budgets, and three trials provide some variation. Experiment 2 sweeps compaction frequency and contrasts recovery-capable versus destructive memory.

Missing ablations include model size and family, quantization precision, scorer hyperparameters, exact budget values, retrieval-index failure, archive latency, summary prompt, summarizer temperature, fact type, contradictory updates, evidence ordering, and source attribution. There are no error bars, seed-level results, energy figures, or throughput measurements. The “same average budget” claim requires separate reporting for active accelerator state, CPU/archive storage, index overhead, and retrieval compute.

## 8. External primary-source vetting

The canonical arXiv record confirms v1, both authors, the submission date, title, and four subject classes.[^1] The Information Bottleneck paper supports the use of relevance-preserving compression as intellectual ancestry, but it does not itself validate the reviewed paper’s physical-memory conversions or query-entropy penalty.[^2]

The paper cites and links NVIDIA’s KVPress repository as an implementation framework for KV compression; the repository is a relevant primary implementation source, not evidence that the paper’s specific experiment is reproducible.[^3] The public Needle-in-a-Haystack repository verifies the benchmark family’s purpose but not the paper’s exact natural-filler protocol.[^4] No official repository attributable to these authors and this arXiv record was discoverable during review, so experiment code, dependency versions, prompts, seeds, and figure data remain unverified.

A same-paper raw research-context record exists in Delphoa-Labs/Black-Lake-Data as `DEP-20260711-Tech Intel 1311`; it is associated provenance, not a substitute for this full-paper review.[^5] Existing DEP-A records for CompressKV Semantic Heads and LCLM Context Compression are additional conceptual neighbors, not independent validation of this survey.

## 9. Research notes and critical considerations

### 9.1 Reversibility is a vector, not a Boolean

A raw archive is recoverable only if the system can find, authorize, load, and interpret the right record. Retrieval recall, archive retention, schema evolution, encryption keys, and source integrity determine effective reversibility.[^6] The taxonomy should record recovery probability and cost.

### 9.2 Provenance is part of distortion

For agents, preserving the semantic gist while losing the source may be unacceptable. A memory compacted from “the user prefers X under condition Y” to “the user prefers X” can create durable policy error. Distortion should include provenance, temporality, scope, and uncertainty, not only final-answer accuracy.

### 9.3 Budgets require a resource vector

One scalar hides trade-offs. A deployable benchmark should use a vector: accelerator bytes, host bytes, archival bytes, transfer bytes, prefill time, decode time, retrieval tail latency, energy, and privacy retention. Pareto frontiers can then replace a falsely universal compression ratio.

### 9.4 The experiment tests deletion versus storage

Experiment 2’s large gap is meaningful, but its interpretation is closer to storage theory than codec theory. If one method retains raw chunks and the other deletes them, recovery should win. The difficult comparison is between equal total storage with different fidelity tiers and recovery indexes.

## 10. Limitations, implications, and deployment applicability

The authors acknowledge that this is a survey, `I*(Q)` is unestimated, composition laws are empirical, the model and GPU are small, and many sources are recent preprints. Additional limitations are missing public code, incomplete resource accounting, weak statistical reporting, and the gap between finite-label Fano error and open-ended language tasks.

For deployment, the paper supports three practices. First, preserve a recovery path for high-consequence evidence. Second, evaluate memory over repeated updates rather than one turn. Third, report accuracy over a budget sweep. It does not justify replacing current serving policies without workload-specific measurement.

Governance implications are substantial. Long-term archives improve reversibility but expand privacy and retention risk. A system needs expiration, deletion propagation, access control, source attribution, and audit logs. Irreversible summaries reduce stored detail but can fossilize errors. The choice is not simply quality versus memory; it is quality, cost, privacy, and accountability.

## 11. Independent re-conceptualization and falsifiable hypotheses

### Hypothesis 1: recovery-adjusted rate predicts long-horizon quality

Define effective rate as active memory plus archived bytes weighted by probability and cost of successful recovery. Across equal effective rates, long-horizon recall should correlate better with this measure than with active BPT alone. Falsify it if active BPT predicts recall equally well after archive and retrieval costs are included.

### Hypothesis 2: provenance-aware distortion changes the frontier

Score not only answer correctness but whether each answer cites the correct source, time, and scope. Summarization methods should lose provenance earlier than semantic answer accuracy, while extractive and archival methods should retain it. Falsify if provenance metrics track ordinary accuracy with no systematic separation.

### Hypothesis 3: the value of query side information is not `H(Q)`

Construct many paraphrased queries that select the same evidence and a low-entropy binary query that selects disjoint evidence. The conditioned/unconditioned rate gap should follow evidence-selection information, not surface query entropy. Falsify if the gap scales with raw `H(Q)` across both constructions.

### Hypothesis 4: equal-total-storage tiers narrow the reported gap

Give both repeated-summary and retrieval systems the same total byte budget. Permit the summary system a compact raw-evidence reserve and charge the retrieval system for indexes. The 0.5 recall gap should shrink but remain positive on exact facts. Falsify if retrieval retains the full gap after total-cost equalization.

## 12. Replication and falsification agenda

1. Release the exact experiment repository, commit, prompts, packages, seeds, and raw figure data.
2. Reproduce both experiments on at least three model families and sizes, including a larger instruction model.
3. Report every tested budget, per-depth needle accuracy, confidence intervals, and run failures.
4. Replace scalar BPT with active GPU, host, archive, index, transfer, latency, and energy measures.
5. Compare query-aware retrieval, irreversible eviction, quantization, soft-prompt compression, bounded recurrent state, and agent summarization on at least one shared task.
6. Equalize total retained information in the repeated-compaction experiment.
7. Add contradiction, supersession, negation, structured identifier, and source-attribution tests.
8. Measure repeated compaction for 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and 32 events with raw-memory checkpoints.
9. Empirically estimate conditional rate–distortion curves rather than labeling a budget collapse as `I*(Q)`.
10. Treat failure to preserve exact commitments or deletion requests as a stopping criterion, not merely average recall loss.

The framework is falsified as a universal predictor if normalized frontiers do not transfer across layers, if query-conditioning provides no controlled advantage, or if repeated irreversible compression does not compound under equal-total-cost experiments.

## 13. Durable restatement

Memory compaction is best evaluated as a downstream-utility problem over a resource vector and an access contract. Every scheme decides what evidence remains directly addressable, what becomes approximate, what moves to a slower tier, and what is destroyed. Query information and recovery paths can improve the frontier, but their benefits must be charged for storage, latency, failure, privacy, and provenance. The paper’s lasting contribution is to make the common decision visible and to demand repeated-compaction evaluation. Its theory is a qualitative guide; its benchmark is a seed requiring larger, cost-equalized, reproducible validation.
