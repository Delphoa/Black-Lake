# Turn scratch work into disposable state by preserving explicit outcomes

## A detailed review, technical reconstruction, and independent re-conceptualization of “Structured Thoughts For Improved Reasoning And Context Pruning”

**Source paper:** Zain Sarwar, Supriyo Chakraborty, Berkcan Kapusuzoglu, Chia-Hsuan Lee, Anirban Das, Stephen Rawls, Kartik Balasubramaniam, and Sambit Sahu, “Structured Thoughts For Improved Reasoning And Context Pruning,” arXiv:2607.10386v1, 2026.[^source]

**Artifact prepared:** 2026-07-15  
**Local source reviewed:** complete 13-page PDF and matching full-paper HTML, including ethics, reproducibility, LLM-use disclosure, prompt appendix, all three tables, and all three figures  
**Review scope:** full method and experiment reconstruction, quantitative and claim audit, independent re-conceptualization, deployment considerations, and replication agenda  
**Review status:** paper and public arXiv record inspected; no official implementation was verified, and neither fine-tuning nor pruning experiments were rerun

## Executive assessment

### The paper in one sentence

The paper reformats mathematical reasoning traces into alternating exploratory `<try>` blocks and concise `<outcome>` blocks, fine-tunes 7B–8B models on that syntax, then masks or removes completed scratch blocks so later reasoning retains only their distilled conclusions.

### The deeper idea

Structured Thoughts introduces an explicit call–return boundary inside chain-of-thought. A `<try>` block is temporary working state; its paired `<outcome>` is the durable return value. Once the return value exists, later steps should not need the complete execution trace. This turns context pruning from a token-importance guess into a semantic lifecycle rule created by the model’s own output format.

### Bottom-line judgment

The paper supports two distinct conclusions. First, structured supervision improves average math-reasoning accuracy over raw-trace SFT: reported relative gains are 3.66% for Llama-Nemotron-8B and 8.08% for Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct across seven benchmarks. A compute-matched control with uninformative masked outcomes gains only 1.69%, suggesting outcome content matters beyond added sequence length, though other formatting effects remain. Second, pruning is technically feasible but not yet deployment-ready: removing `<try>` blocks yields about 85% context/KV reduction from the measured block-length ratio and an average relative accuracy loss reported around 8.76% (the abstract says 8.67%, a small internal discrepancy).[^discrepancy] The authors acknowledge a slow Hugging Face implementation, single-model pruning evidence, heuristic segmentation, and no optimized dynamic-KV engine. The work is promising as a learned state-management protocol, not proof that reasoning scratch is generally dispensable.

### Principal strengths

- The syntax supplies natural, model-generated pruning boundaries instead of applying an external salience score to arbitrary tokens.
- Pruning-aware attention masks simulate the intended inference visibility during training.
- Two model families and seven math benchmarks support the structured-SFT result.
- The compute-matched masked-outcome control addresses the simple “more tokens/compute” explanation.
- The paper reports block counts and lengths, hardware, dataset scale, ethics, reproducibility, and an explicit summarization prompt.

### Principal qualifications

1. Pruning is evaluated only for Llama-Nemotron-8B, while the structured-SFT claim covers two models.
2. The generated `<outcome>` may omit premises needed later; pruning makes any summarization error irreversible.
3. Segmentation uses heuristic decision cues and an LLM summarizer, so data construction quality and model-provider behavior are important hidden variables.
4. Training uses 64 A100 40 GB GPUs across eight nodes, while deployment speed is not demonstrated in an optimized runtime.
5. Evaluation is confined to mathematical reasoning and primarily accuracy, not faithfulness, verifier agreement, or hidden error propagation.

## 1. The problem the paper is actually solving

### 1.1 Problem and stakes

Reasoning models generate long chains containing algebra, case analysis, corrections, and verification. Keeping every token preserves information but grows attention work and the KV cache. External token pruning risks deleting a premise because ordinary traces do not mark which details are temporary and which conclusions later steps depend on.

The paper’s strategy is to change the trace format before compression. Each reasoning step is divided into scratch (`<try>`) and a short durable conclusion (`<outcome>`). If training teaches later tokens to rely on outcomes, completed scratch can be masked during training and physically removed during inference.

### 1.2 Prior method families

Chain-of-thought supervision teaches explicit intermediate steps; self-consistency and tree search increase test-time exploration; process supervision evaluates steps; context-compression methods prune or summarize earlier state. PENCIL is the closest call–return-style system named by the paper. Structured Thoughts differs by studying foundation-scale 7B–8B models, million-example supervised fine-tuning, standard math benchmarks, and accuracy gains from the format itself.

### 1.3 The proposed gap

Most reasoning traces have only a global `<think>` block and final answer. They do not distinguish temporary derivation from durable state. The proposed gap is a scalable syntax and training mask that makes this distinction machine-actionable.

## 2. Formal and technical reconstruction

### 2.1 Definitions and assumptions

A raw solution is segmented into step-sized spans using cues such as “Wait” or “Hmm.” Each span becomes `<try> T_i </try>`. A larger instruction-tuned model summarizes the span into `<outcome> O_i </outcome>`. The question and final answer remain unchanged. The resulting sequence alternates (T_i,O_i).

The central assumption is a sufficient-state property: (O_i), together with earlier outcomes and current context, contains enough information for future steps even when (T_i) is hidden. This is empirical and approximate, not guaranteed.

### 2.2 Structured supervised fine-tuning

Stage 1 uses ordinary token-level cross-entropy on reformatted traces. Models learn the tags, generate exploratory work, and emit a conclusion for each step. The syntax may improve reasoning because it forces periodic consolidation and makes decisive information easier to attend to.

### 2.3 Pruning-aware masking

Stage 2 modifies the causal attention mask. Tokens in a `<try>` block remain visible while its paired `<outcome>` is generated. After the outcome closes, future query positions cannot attend to that try block but can attend to the outcome. This simulates the information graph expected during physical pruning while retaining a dense training sequence.

The mask is semantically asymmetric: outcomes may read scratch, but later scratch may read only prior outcomes. Correct implementation must preserve ordinary causal order inside all visible regions and avoid attention to future tokens.

### 2.4 Inference-time pruning

Generation proceeds until a complete try/outcome pair appears. The runtime removes the preceding try tokens from active context/KV, retains the outcome, and continues. Repetition creates a compact state consisting of the prompt, durable outcomes, active scratch, and final response tokens.

The paper’s proof-of-concept uses Hugging Face Transformers because vLLM does not support the required dynamic KV eviction. Removing tokens also requires correct positional handling; otherwise later positions and rotary embeddings can diverge from the training mask semantics.

### 2.5 Training process

The dataset starts from roughly two million Llama-Nemotron Math v1.1 examples; the experiments select the first one million. Structured and baseline variants use matched examples. Models include Llama-Nemotron-8B and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (s1). Training runs two epochs at learning rate (10^{-4}), maximum context 25,000, with sequence packing, using veRL and LLaMA-Factory on eight nodes of eight A100 40 GB GPUs.[^training]

### 2.6 Inference process

Unpruned structured evaluation generates the tagged trace normally. Pruned evaluation uses a model trained with the visibility mask and removes completed scratch blocks. The final answer is scored by benchmark correctness. The paper does not describe a deterministic verifier that checks whether an outcome faithfully covers its try block.

### 2.7 Notation and implementation audit

The method is mostly algorithmic rather than equation-heavy. The key object is the attention mask in Figure 3. Correctness requires exact tag parsing, handling malformed or nested tags, deciding when a block is complete, preserving special/system tokens, and managing positional indices after eviction. Without code, those edge cases are not independently audited.

## 3. Architecture and information flow

### 3.1 Representation

Reasoning state is split into ephemeral computation and durable summaries. Outcomes form an append-only ledger of conclusions; only the current try is high-volume temporary state.

### 3.2 Decision or action modules

Offline construction decides step boundaries and summaries. The model then decides the content and timing of its outcomes. The runtime’s action is deterministic conditional on well-formed tags: remove the completed try.

### 3.3 Dependencies and post-processing

Dependencies include source traces, cue-based segmentation, an external summarizer model/prompt, SFT infrastructure, custom attention masks, a parser, and a mutable KV runtime. There is no post-generation correction specific to pruning.

## 4. The complete operational pipeline

### 4.1 End-to-end flow

Raw traces are segmented; each segment is wrapped and summarized; baseline, structured, masked, and compute-matched datasets are constructed; models are fine-tuned; unpruned and pruned generation runs across seven benchmarks; accuracy, token/context savings, and overhead are compared.

### 4.2 Resource allocation

Structured examples contain on average 7.34 try blocks. A typical try is 688.5 tokens and an outcome 103.2 tokens, an outcome-to-try ratio near 0.15. Removing completed tries therefore suggests roughly 85% reduction in historical reasoning tokens and corresponding KV/attention state. This arithmetic is a structural estimate; actual peak savings depend on active block length, tags, prompt, outputs, batch shape, and allocator behavior.

### 4.3 Guarantee boundary

Masking enforces visibility during training, and parsing can enforce deletion. Neither guarantees summary sufficiency, correct reasoning, stable tag generation, nor wall-clock speedup. The reported accuracy cost confirms information is sometimes lost.

## 5. Independent re-conceptualization

### 5.1 Learned call–return memory

Each try resembles a function body and each outcome its return value. Once returned, the body can be discarded while callers retain the value.

**Boundary of the analogy:** ordinary functions have explicit inputs and semantics; natural-language outcomes are lossy, untyped, and can omit hidden dependencies.

### 5.2 Semantic garbage collection

The tags act as lifetime annotations. The runtime collects scratch only after the model emits a durable replacement. This is safer than time-based eviction because disposal is tied to a semantic event.

### 5.3 Transferable design principle

If an agent must forget intermediate state, train it to publish a compact handoff before deletion and make that handoff’s sufficiency testable.

## 6. Experimental design reconstructed

### 6.1 Data

Training uses the first one million examples of Llama-Nemotron Math v1.1. Evaluation covers MATH-500, MinervaMath, GSM8K, AMC23, AIME24, TheoremQA, and OlympiadBench. The tasks range from grade-school arithmetic to competition and theorem-oriented reasoning.

### 6.2 Models and hardware

Llama-Nemotron-8B and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (s1) are fine-tuned for structured-versus-raw comparison. Pruning uses Llama-Nemotron-8B only. Hardware totals 64 A100 40 GB GPUs connected across eight nodes, a substantial budget that matters to reproducibility.

### 6.3 Baselines

Baseline SFT uses raw traces. Structured SFT uses try/outcome traces. The compute-matched control retains sequence length but replaces outcome content with mask tokens, isolating part of the content/compute effect. Pruned structured evaluation compares the same structured model with and without context deletion.

The controls do not isolate every factor: tags, segmentation, extra supervision, summarizer style, and changed token distribution remain bundled.

### 6.4 Metrics

Benchmark accuracy is reported, often as relative improvement. Context savings derive from token counts, and pruning cost is relative accuracy degradation. The paper does not report calibration, pass@k, response length distributions, verifier scores, or confidence intervals across independent fine-tuning seeds.

### 6.5 Runtime semantics

The method should reduce KV memory and attention FLOPs, but the prototype is slower because it runs in an unoptimized Transformers loop with deletion overhead. Thus theoretical resource reduction and achieved latency diverge. The paper states this limitation clearly.

## 7. Results: what is reported and what it means

### 7.1 Structured SFT

Table 1 reports average relative gains of 3.66% for Llama-Nemotron-8B and 8.08% for Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. Individual tasks vary: examples include Llama MATH-500 90.2 to 93.2 and Qwen AIME24 33.3 to 53.3, while some tasks slightly decline.

**Interpretation:** the format/supervision package improves average reasoning for both tested models.

**Qualification:** relative percentages can magnify low baselines, and one training seed does not establish stability.

### 7.2 Pruning

Table 2 reports an average relative degradation of 8.76% for pruned versus unpruned structured Llama-Nemotron-8B. The abstract states 8.67%, which appears to be a transposition or version inconsistency. Context/KV saving is about 85% by measured block-length ratio.

**Interpretation:** outcomes retain much, but not all, useful scratch information.

**Qualification:** only one model and an unoptimized runtime are tested; average loss may hide catastrophic task/example failures.

### 7.3 Compute-matched control

Table 3 reports raw SFT baseline, structured SFT (+3.66%), and masked-outcome compute match (+1.69%) for Llama-Nemotron-8B. Added length/compute explains part but not all of the gain.

**Interpretation:** outcome content or meaningful structure contributes beyond extra tokens.

**Qualification:** mask tokens do not match the linguistic or optimization properties of real outcomes, so the control is informative but incomplete.

## 8. Ablations and causal evidence

### 8.1 Structured versus raw supervision

The two-model comparison supports a format-level effect. A stronger factorial would compare tags without summaries, summaries without tags, random boundaries, human boundaries, and several summarizers.

### 8.2 Compute-matched outcomes

The masked-outcome control is the main causal ablation. It weakens the “longer sequence alone” hypothesis. It does not isolate whether gains come from periodic repetition, shorter local targets, or the semantics of conclusions.

### 8.3 Pruning-aware training

The mask matches the intended visibility graph. The paper would benefit from pruning a model trained without the mask, and from evaluating masking without physical deletion, to separate training adaptation from runtime mechanics.

### 8.4 Randomness and statistics

No independent fine-tuning-seed distribution is central to the report. Dataset order (“first one million”), summarizer randomness, and generation settings can affect results. Claims should remain point-estimate, tested-condition statements.

## 10. External views and field context

### 10.1 Predecessor evidence

Chain-of-thought and process-supervision research establishes value in explicit steps; call–return/context-pruning work motivates disposable scratch. Structured Thoughts’ contribution is a scalable supervised syntax and visibility mask rather than the first use of intermediate summaries.

### 10.2 Independent evaluation perspective

Seven established math benchmarks provide breadth within one domain. They do not test long-horizon agents, code execution, factual research, or situations where intermediate evidence must be auditable.

### 10.3 Contemporaneous alternatives

Alternatives include concise-reasoning training, recurrent latent state, learned token pruning, external notebooks, verifier-guided summaries, and periodic full-trace checkpoints. These trade interpretability, recoverability, and memory differently.

### 10.4 Publication, code, and reproducibility status

The source is arXiv v1. The paper names veRL and LLaMA-Factory but the reviewed source does not expose an official Structured Thoughts repository. Training configuration is partly reported, yet dataset transformation scripts, masks, checkpoints, and pruning runtime were not inspectable.[^code]

## 11. Research notes and critical considerations

### 11.1 Internal consistency

The notable numerical discrepancy is pruning loss: 8.67% in the abstract versus 8.76% in Table 2, later discussion, and conclusion. The table-supported value should govern until corrected. The paper also alternates “Structured Thoughts” and “Structured Reasoning,” which appears terminological rather than methodological.

### 11.2 Metric and baseline concerns

Relative improvement obscures absolute changes on low-scoring benchmarks. Accuracy alone cannot show whether conclusions are faithful to scratch. A pruned answer may be correct for the wrong reason or fail unpredictably when an early summary is wrong.

### 11.3 Unsupported or overbroad claims

“Memory/context savings” is supported structurally, not with an optimized allocator benchmark. “Improved reasoning” should be limited to the tested math suite. “Pruning is feasible” is appropriate; “deployment-ready” is not.

### 11.4 Alternative explanations

Gains may come from extra supervision tokens, periodic restatement, a teacher-model quality boost, or easier next-token prediction near summaries. Pruning loss may arise from heuristic boundary errors rather than an inherent need for scratch.

### 11.5 Unresolved questions

- Can an outcome verifier detect omitted premises before scratch is deleted?
- How often are tags malformed, and what safe fallback is used?
- Does repeated summarization accumulate semantic drift over dozens of steps?
- Are hidden scratch traces needed for audit, safety review, or debugging even if not needed by the model?

## 12. Potential implications

### 12.1 Scientific implications

Trace format is an architectural interface. Supervision can teach a model to expose state boundaries that a runtime can exploit, linking language modeling and memory management.

### 12.2 System-design implications

Agent runtimes could retain compact outcomes in active context while storing full scratch in cold, access-controlled logs for recovery. Outcome sufficiency should be checked before eviction.

### 12.3 Broader implications

The call–return pattern may transfer to tool agents, code debugging, research workflows, and planning. Transfer is speculative because those domains contain external observations and commitments that a short outcome may not preserve.

## 13. New hypotheses derived from the paper

### Hypothesis 1: verified outcomes reduce pruning loss

**Proposition:** a verifier that checks outcome entailment/coverage before deletion recovers much of the 8.76% loss.  
**Prediction:** rejected/expanded outcomes correlate with later failures and improve final accuracy.  
**Falsifier:** verification does not predict or reduce errors.  
**Minimum test:** generate outcomes, score coverage against try blocks, prune only above threshold.

### Hypothesis 2: typed outcomes beat free prose

**Proposition:** fields for facts, assumptions, unresolved branches, and conclusion preserve dependencies better than one summary paragraph.  
**Prediction:** typed outcomes improve pruned accuracy at equal tokens.  
**Falsifier:** free prose performs equally.  
**Minimum test:** matched-budget schema ablation.

### Hypothesis 3: occasional checkpoints control drift

**Proposition:** retaining every (k)th full try limits accumulated summary error with modest memory cost.  
**Prediction:** accuracy/context frontier dominates always-prune.  
**Falsifier:** checkpoints add memory without improving hard long traces.  
**Minimum test:** sweep (k\in\{2,4,8,\infty\}).

## 14. Deployment and governance considerations

### 14.1 Appropriate use

The method is appropriate for experimental math reasoning where complete traces can be retained privately and pruning failures are recoverable. It is not ready for high-stakes reasoning whose audit trail must remain available to the model or operator.

### 14.2 Poor fit and failure modes

Poor fits include tasks with long-lived hidden assumptions, external observations, legal audit requirements, malformed tags, and strict latency. Failures include lossy outcomes, incorrect deletion, positional bugs, summary drift, and privacy leakage through retained outcomes.

### 14.3 Required safeguards

1. Validate tag structure and fall back to no pruning on parser uncertainty.
2. Verify outcome coverage/consistency before deletion.
3. Preserve encrypted cold traces for audit and recovery when policy permits.
4. Version transformation prompts, teacher model, masks, tokenizer, and runtime.
5. Monitor task-level accuracy, deletion ratios, malformed blocks, and latency.

## 15. Replication and falsification agenda

### 15.1 Highest-priority result

Reproduce all three tables with at least three data/transformation and fine-tuning seeds. Resolve 8.67/8.76, report absolute/relative changes and confidence intervals, and archive per-example outputs.

### 15.2 Full pipeline

Archive raw example IDs, segmentation cues, try spans, summarizer prompt/model/revision, outcomes, SFT configs, exact attention masks, checkpoints, generation settings, prune events, and benchmark scoring.

### 15.3 Normalize evaluation

Equalize dataset examples, non-padding target tokens, optimizer steps, and hardware across raw, tags-only, summary, masked-control, and pruning-aware variants.

### 15.4 Test the mechanism

Measure whether outcome tokens receive later attention and whether outcome coverage predicts pruning success. Run oracle human outcomes and random boundaries.

### 15.5 Test realistic shifts

Evaluate code, multi-hop QA, tool use, long-horizon agents, malformed tags, more than fifty steps, smaller hardware, optimized dynamic-KV serving, and recoverable cold-memory designs.

## 16. Durable restatement

> Structured Thoughts teaches a model to alternate temporary reasoning work with explicit durable conclusions. A pruning-aware mask prevents future tokens from reading completed scratch, and an inference runtime can remove that scratch from active context. Structured supervision improves tested math accuracy; physical pruning saves about 85% of reasoning context but costs about 8.76% average relative accuracy and is not optimized for deployment.

The durable contribution is the semantic lifetime boundary: produce a handoff before forgetting, then train under the same visibility rule the runtime will enforce.
