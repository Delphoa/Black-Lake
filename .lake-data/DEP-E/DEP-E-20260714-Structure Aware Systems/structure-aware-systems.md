## Executive Summary

This artifact reviews ten complete 2026 preprints selected through `DEP-20260712-Tech Intel 1304`, plus two official repositories and one inaccessible prototype locator. The papers span prompt compression, trajectory watermarking, content protection, multi-agent diagnosis, continual fine-tuning, proactive context, mixed deterministic/probabilistic workflows, photonic measurement-based quantum computing, neutral-atom compilation, and quantum linear solvers.

The source authors make distinct claims, but the cross-source pattern is unusually consistent: performance improves when a system preserves the structure most relevant to its task rather than flattening everything into an undifferentiated sequence or generic primitive set. Examples include learned layer aggregation instead of token replay, complementary selection and tally marks in action traces, graph-structured enterprise state, explicit `SOLVE`/`ASSERT` workflow boundaries, recursive spectral subspaces, and native multiqubit phases. This is a reviewer interpretation supported by E11, not a unified claim made or experimentally tested by the papers.

The strongest direct evidence is within each paper's own evaluation: TRACE reports a selection-channel z-score of 4.06 after 70% deletion with true-positive rate at least 0.93; the photonic study reports four-photon star and linear graph-state fidelities of 83.5±1.8% and 75.6±1.1%; and SPL reports a best configuration of 93% over a 1,200-run benchmark. At the same time, activation compression drops sharply out of distribution, CAPE relies on adversarial assumptions, AgentLocate depends on evaluator visibility, Context Graphs uses synthetic environments, and both quantum-algorithm papers leave important end-to-end scaling questions open. Confidence is therefore moderate at portfolio level: the primary evidence is inspectable, but heterogeneous and unreproduced.

The practical consequence is a disciplined product hypothesis: systems should make task-relevant structure explicit, pair learned components with verifiable boundaries, and test the same representation under distribution shift, deletion, rewriting, incomplete context, hardware noise, and resource constraints. None of the reviewed results alone establishes production readiness.

## Detailed Summary

### Problem context

Generic pipelines often discard structure for convenience. Prompts become token sequences, agent runs become prose logs, organizational state becomes independent documents, computation becomes a chain of model calls, continual updates share one adapter geometry, and quantum programs are lowered into generic two-qubit gates. The reviewed papers each identify a cost of that flattening: excessive context, weak attribution, content retention by crawlers, poor failure localization, catastrophic interference, reactive retrieval, unverifiable arithmetic, or inefficient physical execution.

### Language and agent systems

*Prompt Compression via Activation Aggregation* learns a weighted aggregation over intermediate prompt activations and reinjects a single vector in place of the original instruction. On the in-distribution Toy Tasks setting, the paper reports 85.35 for its weighted-MLP approach versus 86.92 with the full prompt. The same method falls to 63.01 out of distribution while full prompts retain 94.95; on ARC-Easy with Llama 3.1, it reports 77 versus 92. The mechanism is therefore a real compression result within the tested regime, but the failure under shift shows that a compact activation is not automatically a portable instruction representation [E1].

TRACE places complementary watermarks into an LLM-agent trajectory. A selection channel embeds statistically detectable choices, while a tally channel records a separate aggregate invariant intended to survive rewriting. The paper reports utility within seed noise, a selection z-score of 4.06 after 70% deletion, and true-positive rate of at least 0.93 in that condition. The tally remains stable under the tested rewriting attack. Robustness depends on retaining a trustworthy provider execution record and redundant log evidence; the design does not eliminate the governance problem of who controls the record [E2].

CAPE protects online content by introducing perturbations chosen for what an agent's compression and memory pipeline will retain. The paper evaluates three content types across GPT-4.1, Gemini, LangGraph, and Copilot configurations, reporting up to 75.8% improvement in information loss and a 59.7% LangGraph result under its DRAD measure. This is defensive research, but deployment is adversarial: sanitization, adaptive crawlers, accessibility, and the inaccessible prototype all reduce confidence in immediate use [E3].

AgentLocate separates two questions in multi-agent failure analysis: which agent caused the failure and when the trajectory became irrecoverable. It introduces the Who&When and Aegis benchmarks and uses structured views plus multiple evaluators. The paper reports 69.05% agent localization and 38.10% step localization for a Qwen configuration, with lower baselines; an ablation identifies three evaluators as the best tested tradeoff. Visibility bias remains important because agents with more observable actions may be easier to blame, and the promised code was not yet available [E4].

ReCoLoRA recursively decomposes the current effective weight rather than repeatedly attaching new low-rank updates to the original frozen weight. A randomized SVD, an elbow criterion for effective rank, and staged access to principal and residual capacity aim to preserve earlier tasks. Across a six-task GLUE sequence and four backbones, the method is best on three of four backbones for the reported seed-42 comparison, while three-seed results narrow some margins. A task-bank oracle gives a zero-forgetting upper bound but changes the storage problem. The official repository exposes code and raw results, improving inspectability, though a root license was not visible [E5].

Context Graphs represent enterprise entities, relations, and temporal state changes, then use delta detection and a proactivity scorer to surface relevant information before a user query. In synthetic graphs of 50–150 nodes and 80–200 edges, with 200 events per scenario, the paper reports average precision at five of 0.83, false-positive rate of 0.11, mean time to surface of 0.3 minutes versus 47 minutes for a reactive baseline, and persona relevance of 0.91. The experiment supports the mechanism, not enterprise deployment: graph completeness, privacy, alert fatigue, and synthetic generation are major boundaries [E6].

SPL makes deterministic and probabilistic execution modes explicit in one declarative specification. `GENERATE` and `EVALUATE` route to models; `SOLVE` and `ASSERT` route to symbolic systems or verifiers. The paper reports 1,200 runs formed from ten repetitions, twenty tasks, two execution arms, and three configurations. Its best configuration reaches 93%, compared with 78% for a SymPy setup and 54% for SageMath in the reported table. The solver arm is checked, whereas the LLM-only arm is evaluated on produced output, so claims must retain that asymmetry. The Apache-2.0 official repository makes the language concrete [E7].

### Quantum systems

The photonic MBQC paper generates graph states with up to four qubits on an integrated silicon photonic chip. It reports star-state fidelity of 83.5±1.8% and linear-state fidelity of 75.6±1.1%; gate/process results include 84.8% for a single-qubit transformation, 76.9% for controlled-Z, and 90.3% for controlled-NOT. Demonstrations include Grover's search at 80.8% and Deutsch–Jozsa at 95.0% and 94.6%. These are physical measurements, but state rates around 12.1 and 13.3 mHz, loss, noise, and feed-forward requirements mark the distance to scale [E8].

The Möbius-guided compiler represents diagonal operations through a Möbius transform and maps terms to native multiqubit controlled-phase gates. The paper's modeled shared architecture and representative fidelities suggest improvements over decompositions into one- and two-qubit gates; higher-order benchmarks strengthen the estimate, while QFT and GHZ negative controls show overlap rather than universal superiority. Because the evidence is simulated under device assumptions rather than executed on neutral-atom hardware, its conclusion is best treated as a compiler hypothesis ready for hardware validation [E9].

The quantum-linear-solver study examines whether single-reference linearized coupled-cluster systems can retain favorable condition-number scaling. It uses proxy diagnostics and Qiskit simulations of small molecules at 19–28 qubits. The reported behavior supports the prospect of polylogarithmic condition-number dependence in selected regimes, but an exponential end-to-end advantage remains conditional on state preparation, matrix access, loading, readout, error, and chemistry-accuracy costs [E10].

### Synthesis

Across both domain clusters, the novel move is often not “more model” or “more hardware,” but a representation aligned to the system's causal or verifiable structure. The evidence favors three transferable principles: preserve task-relevant structure, expose transitions and boundaries for audit, and test robustness where that structure is damaged or incomplete. This synthesis is useful for prioritizing experiments, but it should not collapse the papers' incompatible metrics into a single score.

## Observations

- `Observed pattern`: Compression succeeds when the retained representation is aligned to task structure, but the prompt-compression results show that alignment can be distribution-specific [E1].
- `Observed pattern`: Auditability improves when state transitions are first-class: trajectory channels, multi-agent step views, context deltas, and declarative assertions all expose where a system changed [E2, E4, E6, E7].
- `Technical implication`: Learned components should be paired with deterministic checks or redundant evidence when correctness or attribution matters.
- `Contradiction or tension`: More structure can improve reliability while increasing governance cost. Context graphs centralize sensitive relations; detailed traces increase observability but can expose user or enterprise behavior.
- `Technical implication`: Hardware-aware quantum representations can reduce logical overhead, yet physical loss and state-preparation costs may dominate the savings [E8–E10].
- `Open question`: Which structural properties transfer across distributions, organizations, models, and devices, and which merely fit a benchmark?
- `Reviewer hypothesis`: “Structure under damage” is a useful common evaluation axis: delete trace steps, rewrite text, omit graph edges, shift prompt tasks, perturb spectral rank, or vary gate fidelity, then measure graceful degradation.

## Considerations

Adoption should begin with measurable boundaries. Agent systems need explicit provenance for logs, access controls for context graphs, retention limits, and a human escalation path. A watermark or failure-localization score should not be treated as dispositive evidence without record-integrity controls and uncertainty reporting. Proactive enterprise systems must minimize surveillance, explain why an item surfaced, and allow suppression or correction.

Content-protection research creates a tradeoff among publisher control, accessibility, interoperability, and adversarial adaptation. Any deployment should be reviewed for impact on assistive technologies and benign indexing. The safe use case is defensive evaluation by an authorized content owner, not covert interference with third-party systems.

Continual adaptation and workflow languages need versioned datasets, deterministic seeds where possible, dependency pinning, and tests that distinguish model quality from verifier quality. Repository existence is not proof of reproduction. Ambiguous licensing, as observed for ReCoLoRA, should block redistribution until clarified.

Quantum implementations need resource accounting that crosses abstraction layers. Logical gate savings are insufficient if state preparation, loss, feed-forward latency, matrix access, or readout dominate. Hardware results, modeled compiler results, and algorithmic simulations should remain clearly separated in product or investment decisions.

## Potential Implementations

### 1. Structure-aware agent observability

- `User`: Operator of a multi-agent workflow.
- `Goal`: Diagnose failures and preserve action provenance.
- `Core mechanism`: Store a typed event graph, redundant trace attestations, and agent/step localization views.
- `Required inputs`: Authorized tool-call events, agent IDs, timestamps, outcomes, and evaluator policies.
- `Outputs`: Evidence-backed incident timeline, candidate responsible agent/step, uncertainty, and missing-evidence warnings.
- `Risk controls`: Append-only signed records, role-based access, redaction, retention limits, and human adjudication.
- `Evaluation`: Synthetic failures with known ground truth, tamper tests, false-accusation rate, and operator time-to-resolution.

### 2. Verifiable mixed-computation workflow runner

- `User`: Engineer building data or reasoning pipelines.
- `Goal`: Use models for interpretation while reserving deterministic operations for solvers and assertions.
- `Core mechanism`: A declarative plan labels probabilistic and deterministic nodes and records verifier results.
- `Required inputs`: Workflow specification, safe public test data, model endpoint, deterministic tools, and assertions.
- `Outputs`: Result bundle, execution trace, assertion status, and provenance manifest.
- `Risk controls`: Sandboxed tools, schema validation, budget limits, secret isolation, and fail-closed assertions.
- `Evaluation`: Mutation tests that introduce arithmetic, routing, and formatting errors and verify rejection.

### 3. Quantum structure cost explorer

- `User`: Quantum compiler or algorithm researcher.
- `Goal`: Compare logical structure preservation with physical resource cost.
- `Core mechanism`: Map diagonal terms or graph-state programs to candidate hardware primitives and propagate fidelity/loss assumptions.
- `Required inputs`: Small public circuits, topology, calibrated or hypothetical gate models, and state-preparation assumptions.
- `Outputs`: Gate counts, depth, estimated success probability, sensitivity ranges, and explicit unsupported assumptions.
- `Risk controls`: Label modeled versus measured inputs; prohibit production or advantage claims from the estimator alone.
- `Evaluation`: Reproduce paper-scale examples and test negative controls where structure-aware lowering should not help.

## Three Ways to Exercise This Research

1. `Trace damage lab`: Objective—test whether structured provenance survives edits; inputs—a synthetic multi-agent trajectory and toy selection/tally markers; method—apply bounded deletion, rewriting, and reordering; output—a detection/localization report; success criterion—known edits and responsible steps are recovered with calibrated uncertainty; stop condition—any real user data, credentials, or unauthorized logs would be required.
2. `Deterministic boundary kata`: Objective—separate model interpretation from checkable computation; inputs—twenty public arithmetic and routing tasks; method—implement model-only and solver-plus-assertion arms with identical prompts; output—a versioned result table and failure taxonomy; success criterion—the verified arm rejects injected errors without silently changing task semantics; stop condition—tool execution cannot be sandboxed or assertions are not independently reviewable.
3. `Structure-cost notebook`: Objective—compare generic and structure-aware quantum lowering; inputs—small public diagonal circuits and graph-state examples; method—count primitives under stated hypothetical fidelities and sweep loss assumptions; output—sensitivity plots and an assumption ledger; success criterion—reported advantages disappear under the expected negative controls and remain clearly labeled as modeled; stop condition—claims would require unavailable hardware measurements.

## Example MVP Product

- `Product name`: Structure Ledger.
- `Target user`: Teams operating model-assisted workflows that combine agents, tools, and deterministic services.
- `Problem`: Flat logs make it hard to prove what happened, locate failure, or distinguish model judgment from verified computation.
- `Core workflow`: Ingest authorized typed events; construct a temporal execution graph; bind critical transitions to redundant attestations; run deterministic assertions; present an incident view with agent/step candidates and evidence gaps.
- `Data requirements`: Synthetic examples for development; in deployment, minimized tool-call metadata, explicit outcomes, policy versions, and verifier results. Raw prompts and payloads are optional and off by default.
- `Architecture`: Local or private event collector, append-only signed ledger, graph index, rule/verifier service, optional bounded evaluator models, and a read-only review UI.
- `Success metrics`: Time to locate seeded failures, false-attribution rate, tamper-detection rate, assertion coverage, operator agreement, storage overhead, and privacy incidents.
- `Risk controls`: Least-privilege collection, content hashing instead of raw payload retention, encryption, tenant isolation, access audit, calibrated uncertainty, appeal workflow, and deletion policy compatible with legal obligations.
- `Limitations`: It cannot prove intent, repair missing upstream evidence, or make model evaluators objective. Causal attribution remains an adjudication aid.
- `MVP boundary`: Synthetic and authorized internal workflows only; no employee scoring, covert monitoring, or automated sanctions.
- `Deployment model`: Local-first CLI plus private review dashboard.
- `Evaluation plan`: Seeded incident suite, tamper corpus, privacy review, verifier mutation tests, and human factors study.
- `Failure modes`: Incomplete instrumentation, colluding components, clock skew, evaluator bias, excessive retention, and false confidence from a valid signature over misleading data.
- `Maintenance plan`: Version schemas and policies, rotate signing keys, regression-test evaluators, monitor storage and access, and revalidate after model or tool changes.
